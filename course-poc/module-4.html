Deepfakes & Synthetic Media

In the run-up to South Africa’s 2024 election, election bodies, platforms, and civil-society organisations warned that manipulated and AI-generated political content could undermine trust in the electoral process. Post-election research found that conventional misinformation remained more widespread than synthetic media — but even isolated synthetic-media incidents can spread quickly and cause harm when their source and context cannot be verified. This module teaches you to verify potentially synthetic media under time pressure, understand the limits of detection tools, and respond responsibly before you share.

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Explain why deepfake detection heuristics are temporally bounded and should not be treated as permanent facts
  • Recognise when creating or sharing synthetic media may raise election-disinformation, incitement, privacy, defamation, image-based-abuse, platform-policy, workplace-policy, or other governance concerns
  • Apply source verification, corroboration, consequence analysis, and responsible escalation — the skills that hold across changing detection tools

Important: A detector result is evidence to consider — not a final verdict.

South Africa does not have one single “deepfake law.” Legal consequences depend on what the content depicts, whether it is false or deceptive, the sender’s intent, the harm involved, and which law applies in context.

Concept
Synthetic media, detection limits, and SA law · ~20 min

What are deepfakes and synthetic media?

A deepfake is synthetic or manipulated media that uses AI to imitate, alter, or fabricate a person, voice, event, or scene. It may involve a real person, a synthetic identity, or a wholly generated event. The term covers a spectrum: from crude face-swaps to sophisticated voice clones that are difficult to distinguish from the original speaker by ear alone.

Synthetic media is the broader category: any media generated entirely or substantially by AI, whether or not it depicts a real person. A fabricated news article written by an LLM is synthetic media. An AI-generated image of a protest that didn’t happen is synthetic media. A voice clone of a CEO announcing a merger is synthetic media.

During South Africa’s 2024 election, the risk of AI-generated political content was taken seriously by election bodies, platforms, and civil-society organisations. Post-election research found that conventional misinformation remained more widespread than synthetic media, but even isolated AI-generated or manipulated content can spread quickly and cause harm when people cannot verify its source or context.

Why detection heuristics are temporally bounded

Every deepfake detection heuristic you hear — “look for eye glitches,” “unnatural blinking,” “audio artifacts at breath groups” — describes a current limitation of current tools. It does not describe a permanent feature of synthetic media.

Why visual and audio clues expire

A clue can be useful, but it is not proof. Synthetic-media tools improve continuously, while compression, editing, bad recording quality, and platform re-uploads can also create suspicious-looking artifacts in genuine media.

Examples of clues worth noticing — not trusting alone:

  • Inconsistent lip-sync or speech timing
  • Abrupt audio edits or changes in background noise
  • Visual distortions around faces, hands, text, or motion
  • Missing source information or an unexplained first upload
  • A claim that cannot be corroborated by credible reporting or the person involved
The key principle: Detection heuristics describe what current tools can’t yet do. Every new generation of synthesis tools targets the tells of the previous generation. A suspicious clue should make you verify more carefully. An absence of clues should not make you trust the media.
The liar’s dividend: As people become aware of deepfakes, real evidence can be dismissed as fake. A public figure may deny authentic footage or audio by claiming “it is AI.” “Could be AI” is not proof that media is fake. Verify claims in both directions: do not assume unusual media is synthetic, and do not accept an unsupported denial as proof it is fake.

Context manipulation — not only AI manipulation

Many harmful clips are not deepfakes. They may be old audio presented as new, selectively edited audio, a real clip with a false caption, satire taken out of context, a voice recording from a different event, or misleading translation or subtitles.

The verification process is the same whether media is synthetic, edited, old, decontextualised, or real but falsely described.

Detection asks

Does the file show signs of manipulation?

Verification asks

What is the source, context, corroboration, and consequence of acting on it?

Verification is usually the more durable workplace skill — it works regardless of how detection tools change.

When synthetic media can raise legal or governance concerns

There is no single South African deepfake statute. Whether and which law applies depends on what the content depicts, intent, harm, context, and audience. Here are the frameworks most likely to be relevant:

Electoral Act 73 of 1998
Section 89 — Prohibition on False Information to Influence Voters
A fabricated political audio clip may raise section 89 concerns if false information is published with the intention of influencing the conduct or outcome of an election, disrupting an election, or creating hostility or fear for electoral purposes. The legal assessment depends on the content, what the sender knew, intent, and surrounding circumstances. The statutory penalty for certain contraventions can reach a fine or up to ten years’ imprisonment.
Cybercrimes Act 19 of 2020
Section 14 — Harmful Data Messages
A synthetic-media message may raise section 14 concerns where a person discloses it with the intention to incite violence against, or damage to the property of, a person or group. Section 14 is not a general false-information, reputation-harm, election-misinformation, or harmful-message offence. The content and intent matter: not every false or AI-generated clip falls under this section.
Films and Publications Amendment Act 11 of 2019
Harmful Content Classification
Some synthetic media may raise classification or prohibited-content concerns, especially where it involves sexual content, child sexual-abuse material, propaganda for war, incitement of imminent violence, or hate speech. The framework also provides complaint and takedown pathways for certain online content. Whether it applies depends on the content and distribution context — it is not a blanket deepfake or forwarding-liability statute.
Legal and professional caution: Forwarding is not always harmless. Sharing can amplify false, defamatory, threatening, election-related, privacy-invasive, or otherwise unlawful content. Whether it creates legal liability depends on the content, what you knew or intended, the harm, and the law that applies. When stakes are high — pause, verify, and seek authorised advice before amplifying.

The verification approach that holds across time

Since detection heuristics expire, the approach that holds across generations of synthetic media is source verification, not artifact detection:

  1. Source: Who first uploaded or recorded it? Can you find the earliest available version, original account, date, location, and surrounding context? A WhatsApp attribution or screenshot is easy to fake or strip of context.
  2. Corroboration: Has any credible, independent outlet, official source, or primary record verified the event or statement? Do not treat many accounts repeating the same clip as independent confirmation.
  3. Subject response: Has the attributed person, organisation, campaign, or spokesperson issued a confirmation, denial, or correction? A missing immediate response does not prove the clip is authentic.
  4. Institutional signal: Has a relevant authority, credible newsroom, independent fact-checker, platform, employer, regulator, or campaign issued a verification, correction, or warning?
  5. Consequence analysis: What could happen if you share this and it is wrong? The higher the stakes, the higher the verification bar.
  6. Preserve evidence safely: Save the original link/file, sender, timestamp, and surrounding context. Do not edit, repost, or upload the clip to public AI tools or unknown websites while checking it.

High-stakes media triage

High-stakes claim → Source unclear / context unverified
→ Do not amplify
→ Preserve evidence (link, sender, timestamp, context)
→ Check authorised source + independent corroboration
→ Escalate / report / correct where appropriate
Privacy rule: Do not upload unverified or potentially confidential media — especially political, corporate, or personally identifying content — to a public AI tool, public detector, or unknown website. Treat it as potentially confidential, harmful if amplified, or sensitive to the people depicted.
Safety note: If you encounter synthetic media that involves sexual abuse, violence against a specific person, child sexual-abuse material, or threats, do not download, repost, or circulate it. Preserve only the minimum information needed to report safely through authorised channels.
Reporting routes: Depending on context, report harmful or deceptive content to the platform (using its own reporting tools), your internal communications or IT-security team, an election authority, an independent fact-checker, a relevant regulator, or law-enforcement. Preserve evidence and follow your organisation’s authorised procedures. Match the channel to the content and harm.

Your Incident Response Snapshot

Model Answers — Acquisition Rumour Voice Note (Corporate Deepfake Scenario)

1. TASK — What is this audio being used to do?

This audio is being used to fabricate a material corporate announcement and attribute it to a named, credible individual (the CEO). Its designed effect is to prompt action: internal panic, external forwarding, and potentially market-moving behaviour before the company can respond. This is synthetic media deployed as a manipulation tool — not informational content. The stakes are high for three compounding reasons: (1) it involves a listed company, meaning premature market movement based on false information creates securities law exposure; (2) it is being circulated before the company can mount a verified response; (3) colleagues have already forwarded externally, meaning the window for containment is short. No legitimate use of synthetic media involves fabricating an executive announcement.

2. DATA — What is verifiable vs unverifiable?

The audio claims: (a) a specific acquisition is imminent, (b) the CEO made this announcement, (c) the announcement was made in a specific context. What you can verify in 45 minutes: whether the CEO is reachable by direct call; whether any JSE regulatory announcement has been filed (listed companies must file material announcements via SENS); whether company counsel has been notified of such a transaction. What you cannot verify from the audio alone: the authenticity of the voice, whether the recording context is as described. The audio also embeds sensitive personal information (CEO’s voice, company strategy) and potentially price-sensitive information — both categories that carry regulatory obligations under POPIA and JSE Listing Requirements respectively.

3. TOOL — What AI created this? What does “slightly unnatural” tell you?

This was likely created with a voice cloning tool — a TTS or voice synthesis model fine-tuned on samples of the CEO’s speech (publicly available from interviews, presentations, earnings calls). Current voice cloning tools require varying amounts of source audio depending on the model generation — often far less than you might expect — and this threshold continues to decrease with each new generation. Executives with any public speaking record (earnings calls, interviews, conference talks) typically have sufficient audio available to a motivated attacker. The “slightly unnatural” quality may reflect: current prosody limitations (emotional contour doesn’t match the scripted content’s urgency), audio compression artifacts from WhatsApp’s codec, or a genuine recording in suboptimal conditions. Critically: “slightly off” does not prove it’s a deepfake. Equally, “sounds pretty natural” would not prove it’s real. Detection heuristics are temporally bounded. The right stance: note the signal, don’t over-rely on it, and proceed to source verification regardless of your audio judgment.

4. TRUST — Verification steps in priority order

Step 1 (0–3 min): Call the CEO directly on their known number. If unreachable, call their PA, CFO, or Legal Director. This is source verification at the primary level. Step 2 (3–10 min): Check SENS (JSE Stock Exchange News Service) for any filed announcements. A real acquisition announcement for a listed company requires mandatory regulatory filing — no SENS filing means either the acquisition is not happening or is not yet public. Step 3 (10–20 min): Notify Legal and Company Secretary to issue an internal communication clarifying the voice note’s status while verification is underway. Step 4 (parallel): Request that colleagues who forwarded externally hold any further dissemination pending verification. Regarding external forwards: you cannot undo them, but you can contact the recipients and explain that the information is unverified and under investigation. Your threshold for action should be: confirmation from two independent, senior, trusted individuals with direct knowledge, or a negative SENS filing. “Sounds plausible” is not a threshold.

5. HUMAN — Accountability and SA legal exposure

The creator of the audio deepfake is liable under: Cybercrimes Act Section 14 (creating a harmful data message designed to cause harm), and potentially the Financial Markets Act 19 of 2012 (if the intent was to manipulate a listed company’s share price — market manipulation). Colleagues who forwarded the audio externally are exposed under Cybercrimes Act Section 14 — sharing a harmful data message is an offence. Their defence depends on whether they knew or ought reasonably to have known it was false or harmful. “I just forwarded it” is not a full legal defence. The company needs to: (1) immediately issue a SENS announcement clarifying the audio is unverified and under investigation; (2) notify company counsel; (3) consider whether the JSE Listings Requirements require formal disclosure of the incident; (4) document all steps taken, when, and by whom — this documentation is accountability evidence if regulatory scrutiny follows.
