3
Concept
Synthetic media, detection limits, and SA law · ~20 min

What are deepfakes and synthetic media?

A deepfake is AI-generated or AI-manipulated media — video, audio, or image — designed to make a real person appear to say or do something they did not say or do. The term covers a spectrum: from crude face-swaps to sophisticated voice clones indistinguishable from the original speaker by human ear.

Synthetic media is the broader category: any media generated entirely or substantially by AI, whether or not it depicts a real person. A fabricated news article written by an LLM is synthetic media. An AI-generated image of a protest that didn’t happen is synthetic media. A voice clone of a CEO announcing a merger is synthetic media.

In the 2024 SA election, the primary threat was audio deepfakes: voice clones of party leaders, circulated on WhatsApp, timed to reach voters on or before Election Day. The IEC documented formal complaints and publicly flagged multiple clips as suspected AI-manipulated content.

Why detection heuristics are temporally bounded

Every deepfake detection heuristic you hear — “look for eye glitches,” “unnatural blinking,” “audio artifacts at breath groups” — describes a current limitation of current tools. It does not describe a permanent feature of synthetic media.

Here’s what this looks like across generations of deepfake technology:

~2019
Eye glitch & unnatural blinking
Early face-swap models struggled to replicate natural blink rates. Detecting this reliably distinguished fakes from real video. Status 2026: obsolete — modern models replicate blink patterns accurately.
~2021
Facial boundary artifacts
Face-swap models left a “halo” or blur at the edge of the superimposed face, especially under motion. Clear in side-by-side comparison. Status 2026: significantly reduced — newer models smooth boundaries in real time.
~2022–23
Audio breath-group artifacts
Voice cloning models produced small dropouts or pitch inconsistencies at the end of breath groups. Human listeners trained on examples could detect these. Status 2026: mostly corrected — current models handle breath transitions more naturally.
2024–26
Prosody and emotional contour mismatches
Current models sometimes fail to replicate speaker-specific emotional patterns in high-stakes speech (e.g., live debate intensity vs. scripted reading). Status 2026: under active improvement — treat as a current signal, not a permanent fact.
The key principle: Detection heuristics are descriptions of what current tools can’t yet do. Every new generation of synthesis tools targets the tells of the previous generation. If you treat a detection heuristic as a permanent fact, you will be wrong in 12–18 months. The right stance is: “this is a current signal, useful now, that I should treat as expiring.”

The SA legal consequences of election deepfakes

Three SA statutes create overlapping liability for election deepfakes. These are not hypothetical — the IEC formally invoked them during the 2024 election:

Electoral Act 73 of 1998
Section 89 — Prohibition on False Information to Influence Voters
Creates criminal liability for any person who publishes or disseminates false information knowing it may influence voting behaviour. An audio deepfake of a political leader making a false policy commitment, circulated before an election, falls squarely under this provision. Penalty: up to 5 years imprisonment and/or a fine. Applies to creators and distributors who knew or reasonably ought to have known the information was false.
Cybercrimes Act 19 of 2020
Section 26 — Harmful Data Messages
Creates an offence for sharing a data message that: (a) incites violence or damage to property, (b) threatens a person, or (c) is designed to cause public harm. AI-generated election misinformation designed to damage a candidate’s reputation and influence voter behaviour falls within “designed to cause public harm.” Crucially, sharing is itself an offence under Section 26 — not only creating. Forwarding on WhatsApp, even as a forward, creates exposure.
Films and Publications Amendment Act 11 of 2019
Harmful Content Classification
Requires online distributors to classify and handle harmful content. AI-generated content designed to defame, harass, or incite hatred falls under classified harmful content. The Act extended content regulation to online platforms and online distributors — meaning individuals who disseminate harmful synthetic media via social platforms can be treated as distributors under this Act.
Key takeaway on legal exposure: In SA, you are not protected by “I just forwarded it.” Under the Cybercrimes Act Section 26, sharing a harmful data message is itself an offence. The burden on the sharer is: did you know, or should you reasonably have known, that the content was false or harmful? If an IEC advisory flagged it as suspected AI content and you forwarded it anyway, your exposure is real.

The verification approach that holds across time

Since detection heuristics expire, the approach that holds across generations of synthetic media is source verification, not artifact detection:

  1. Source: Who sent this, from where? WhatsApp attribution is trivially forgeable.
  2. Corroboration: Has any credible independent source verified this event or statement?
  3. Subject response: Has the attributed person or their campaign issued a denial or confirmation?
  4. Institutional signal: Has the IEC, a major news organisation, or a fact-checking body reviewed it?
  5. Consequence analysis: What happens if you share and it’s wrong? The higher the stakes, the higher the verification bar.

Apply
Funda Five on the WhatsApp acquisition rumour · ~20 min
Your role: It is 7:45 AM. You are the Communications Director of a listed South African company. A voice note is circulating in the company staff WhatsApp group claiming to be your CEO announcing the company will be acquired by a foreign entity. Details sound plausible. Several colleagues have already forwarded it externally. The voice sounds slightly unnatural. Markets open in 45 minutes. Apply the Funda Five — with particular depth on TRUST.
Context for non-financial-sector learners: A “listed company” is one whose shares are traded on the JSE (Johannesburg Stock Exchange). SENS is the JSE’s mandatory real-time filing system — all material corporate announcements must be filed there before they are official. No SENS filing means no confirmed announcement. If you work in healthcare, education, or government: substitute your organisation type and verification channel. The Funda Five principles apply identically — the time pressure and verification logic are the same in any sector where AI-generated content reaches others before you can confirm it.
1
TASK — What is this audio being used to do? Is it being used correctly?
What is the function of this voice note? What decision or action is it designed to prompt? What makes this a high-stakes use of synthetic media?
2
DATA — What information does this audio contain? What is verifiable vs unverifiable?
What specific claims does the audio make? Which of those claims can be independently verified before the market opens? What personal data or sensitive business information is embedded?
3
TOOL — What type of AI likely created this? What does “slightly unnatural” actually tell you?
What AI tool would produce a voice clone? What are the current failure modes of voice cloning (without treating them as permanent facts)? Does “sounds slightly off” prove it’s a deepfake — or could it be a real recording in poor conditions?
4
TRUST — How do you verify this before markets open? Step by step.
Give your specific verification steps in priority order. What sources do you check? What thresholds do you need to meet before you can confidently say “act” or “don’t act”? What do you do about colleagues who’ve already forwarded externally?
5
HUMAN — Who is accountable if this turns out to be a deepfake? What about the people who already forwarded it?
Name the accountability chain: the creator, the first distributor, the colleagues who forwarded externally, your company. What are their specific exposures under SA law? What does your company need to do immediately from a governance perspective?

Model Answers — Acquisition Rumour Voice Note (Corporate Deepfake Scenario)

1. TASK — What is this audio being used to do?

This audio is being used to fabricate a material corporate announcement and attribute it to a named, credible individual (the CEO). Its designed effect is to prompt action: internal panic, external forwarding, and potentially market-moving behaviour before the company can respond. This is synthetic media deployed as a manipulation tool — not informational content. The stakes are high for three compounding reasons: (1) it involves a listed company, meaning premature market movement based on false information creates securities law exposure; (2) it is being circulated before the company can mount a verified response; (3) colleagues have already forwarded externally, meaning the window for containment is short. No legitimate use of synthetic media involves fabricating an executive announcement.

2. DATA — What is verifiable vs unverifiable?

The audio claims: (a) a specific acquisition is imminent, (b) the CEO made this announcement, (c) the announcement was made in a specific context. What you can verify in 45 minutes: whether the CEO is reachable by direct call; whether any JSE regulatory announcement has been filed (listed companies must file material announcements via SENS); whether company counsel has been notified of such a transaction. What you cannot verify from the audio alone: the authenticity of the voice, whether the recording context is as described. The audio also embeds sensitive personal information (CEO’s voice, company strategy) and potentially price-sensitive information — both categories that carry regulatory obligations under POPIA and JSE Listing Requirements respectively.

3. TOOL — What AI created this? What does “slightly unnatural” tell you?

This was likely created with a voice cloning tool — a TTS or voice synthesis model fine-tuned on samples of the CEO’s speech (publicly available from interviews, presentations, earnings calls). Current voice cloning tools require varying amounts of source audio depending on the model generation — often far less than you might expect — and this threshold continues to decrease with each new generation. Executives with any public speaking record (earnings calls, interviews, conference talks) typically have sufficient audio available to a motivated attacker. The “slightly unnatural” quality may reflect: current prosody limitations (emotional contour doesn’t match the scripted content’s urgency), audio compression artifacts from WhatsApp’s codec, or a genuine recording in suboptimal conditions. Critically: “slightly off” does not prove it’s a deepfake. Equally, “sounds pretty natural” would not prove it’s real. Detection heuristics are temporally bounded. The right stance: note the signal, don’t over-rely on it, and proceed to source verification regardless of your audio judgment.

4. TRUST — Verification steps in priority order

Step 1 (0–3 min): Call the CEO directly on their known number. If unreachable, call their PA, CFO, or Legal Director. This is source verification at the primary level. Step 2 (3–10 min): Check SENS (JSE Stock Exchange News Service) for any filed announcements. A real acquisition announcement for a listed company requires mandatory regulatory filing — no SENS filing means either the acquisition is not happening or is not yet public. Step 3 (10–20 min): Notify Legal and Company Secretary to issue an internal communication clarifying the voice note’s status while verification is underway. Step 4 (parallel): Request that colleagues who forwarded externally hold any further dissemination pending verification. Regarding external forwards: you cannot undo them, but you can contact the recipients and explain that the information is unverified and under investigation. Your threshold for action should be: confirmation from two independent, senior, trusted individuals with direct knowledge, or a negative SENS filing. “Sounds plausible” is not a threshold.

5. HUMAN — Accountability and SA legal exposure

The creator of the audio deepfake is liable under: Cybercrimes Act Section 26 (creating a harmful data message designed to cause harm), and potentially the Financial Markets Act 19 of 2012 (if the intent was to manipulate a listed company’s share price — market manipulation). Colleagues who forwarded the audio externally are exposed under Cybercrimes Act Section 26 — sharing a harmful data message is an offence. Their defence depends on whether they knew or ought reasonably to have known it was false or harmful. “I just forwarded it” is not a full legal defence. The company needs to: (1) immediately issue a SENS announcement clarifying the audio is unverified and under investigation; (2) notify company counsel; (3) consider whether the JSE Listings Requirements require formal disclosure of the incident; (4) document all steps taken, when, and by whom — this documentation is accountability evidence if regulatory scrutiny follows.
