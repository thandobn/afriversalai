AI in Finance:
When the Model Is Right and the Outcome Is Wrong

South Africa's credit scoring AI is working exactly as designed. And that's the problem. This module is built around a structural failure that isn't a bug — it's a design choice with deep consequences for who gets access to financial services.

60 minutes · 4 phases Finance & Banking track · Requires Module 0 foundation

By the end of this module, you will be able to

  • Explain the difference between financial exclusion and credit invisibility in South Africa, and why the distinction matters for AI lending models
  • Identify the legal obligations that apply to automated financial decisions under SA law (POPIA s71, NCA, FAIS, FSCA)
  • Distinguish between indirect discrimination on listed grounds under PEPUDA and the US-origin disparate impact standard
  • Apply the Funda Five to an AI credit scoring or financial tool deployment scenario

The Full Picture

Millions of South African adults remain invisible to formal credit scoring models — not because they're high risk, but because the models were never designed to see them.

What the research shows:

SA's major banks have deployed credit scoring AI trained predominantly on formal-economy, payslip-earning, credit-bureau-registered borrowers. Financial inclusion and credit inclusion are not the same thing. FinScope's 2023 South Africa survey found 98% of adults had access to formal financial services — yet only 27.3 million adults (about 61% of the adult population) were credit-active. Many people have a bank account but still have limited formal credit history, irregular income, or insufficient conventional documentation to be assessed by traditional lending models. Nedbank and JUMO's 2026 AI-powered lending partnership specifically targets more than 20 million South Africans typically underserved by conventional loan providers, including customers with limited bureau profiles. (FinScope SA 2023; JUMO/Nedbank press release, June 2026)

This is not a bug. It's a consequence of which training data was used. The model learned what creditworthiness looks like from historical loan data — data generated by a formal economy built under apartheid, which excluded most Black South Africans from formal financial participation. As Capitec's leadership has described it, traditional creditworthiness models were never designed for the SA market — and simply importing those assumptions reproduces the exclusions at scale. (Daily Maverick, September 2025)

The design question: The electrician in our scenario is not a bad credit risk. She is an invisible credit risk — to a system that was never trained to see her. The question for finance professionals isn't "is the AI working?" It's "working for whom?"

Concept — The regulatory framework and the tools reshaping SA finance
What applies to you and where the gaps are · ~20 min

Why training data is a liability, not just a technical detail

An AI model reflects the world it was trained on. In SA financial services, that world was shaped by decades of exclusion from formal economic participation. Credit models trained on who got credit historically will perpetuate the patterns of who was allowed to get credit. This is bias in the most literal sense: a systematic lean in one direction, built into the model's foundations.

The core insight for finance professionals

The question is not whether the model is calculating correctly — it often is. The question is whether the model is asking the right questions. A model optimised on formal-economy data will perform well on formal-economy applicants and fail systematically on everyone else. That systematic failure is the financial services professional's responsibility to understand and manage.

Three SA regulations finance professionals must know

National Credit Act (NCA) — Sections 80–81 and Affordability Assessment Regulations

Section 80 defines reckless credit — granted without proper affordability assessment. Section 81 requires lenders to assess discretionary income and document how they arrived at the finding. An AI model must be able to explain its affordability determination. The right to a plain-language reason for credit refusal exists in law; AI models that output only reason codes do not meet this standard. The NCR requires AI credit models to be tested for indirect discrimination on listed grounds across demographic groups.

FSCA-PA AI Report (November 2025) — First comprehensive AI risk assessment of SA financial sector

Published 24 November 2025 by the Financial Sector Conduct Authority and Prudential Authority. Based on approximately 2,100 industry survey responses covering banking, insurance, investments, payments, pensions, fintechs, and lending. Banks lead AI adoption at 52%, followed by payment providers at 50%. (FSCA/PA Artificial Intelligence in the South African Financial Sector, November 2025) Key identified risks: bias and discrimination, low explainability (especially GenAI), data privacy, systemic vulnerability through vendor concentration. Recommends regular bias audits, mandatory disclosure when AI influences credit decisions. No sector-wide AI governance framework exists yet — professionals are operating in regulatory white space.

POPIA — Section 71 and cross-border data processing

Section 71 creates a right not to be subject to decisions made solely by automated means that have legal or significant effects — directly applicable to AI credit, insurance pricing, and fraud flags. SA financial data cannot be processed offshore without adequate protection agreements. GenAI tools used for client document analysis must comply; free-tier tools typically do not provide adequate cross-border data protection under POPIA.

Where AI in SA finance is getting it right — JUMO's alternative credit model: When Lula (SA SME lender using JUMO's engine) incorporated SA-specific regional business payment cycles into their credit model, approval rates for informal retailers increased by 30% while default rates decreased. The technology didn't change. The training data did. This is the most important lesson in this module: the model learns from what you show it. Show it the right data, and inclusion and accuracy go up together.

Run the Funda Five on a real finance scenario · ~15 min
Scenario — Fraud Analyst · FNB

47 flagged transactions. 8 of them are small purchases from spaza shops. The model says suspicious. You have 2 hours.

The AI flags transactions based on deviation from account behaviour profiles. Eight of the 47 flags are under R200 each, from informal vendors with no merchant category code (MCC). The customers are pensioners. Their nearest Pick n Pay closed 60 days ago. They've shifted to spaza purchasing. The model flagged them because their spending pattern changed — which it has, because the local retail landscape changed. The model doesn't know that.

Your SLA: action all 47 flags within 2 hours. The model's stated accuracy: 92%. That means 3.7 of your 47 flags are expected false positives — but you don't know which 3.7.

Your task: Use the Funda Five to work through how to handle the 8 spaza-shop flags — and what they reveal about the limits of the model's design.

Model Answers — Fraud Analyst Queue Scenario (FNB)

1. TASK — What is the fraud AI doing? Why isn't a pattern change the same as fraud?

This is predictive AI — it classifies transaction patterns against a learned baseline and flags deviations. It is optimised to detect anomalies, not to determine their cause. A pattern change and fraud are different problems: pattern changes have many explanations (contextual shifts, life events, new vendors). The model can only identify deviation; it cannot assess whether that deviation has a legitimate cause. The analyst is the mechanism for distinguishing between the two. A detection model that flags is doing its job. A detection model that decides is doing the wrong job.

2. DATA — What data does the model have? What's missing?

The model has: transaction amount, merchant category code, timing, frequency, and historical account behaviour. It does not have: knowledge that a local Pick n Pay closed 60 days ago, that these are pensioners in a specific neighbourhood, or that spaza spending increased across multiple accounts in the same area — a population-level signal that would flag contextual shift rather than individual fraud. The missing data is not about the transaction itself — it's about the context that changed the transaction pattern. Context is the gap between anomaly and fraud. That gap is the analyst's professional responsibility to close.

3. TOOL — Should the fraud model have final authority on these 8 flags?

The fraud detection model is the right tool for initial flagging — it is doing exactly what it was designed to do. Where it fails is as a final decision-maker on contextual cases. For low-value flags with plausible alternative explanations, a tiered review process — flag → human review → action — is the appropriate design. The model should inform; the analyst should decide. A system where the analyst's only function is to action the model's flags, with no pathway to exercise independent judgment, has removed the human from the loop. Under POPIA Section 71 and the NCA, the bank must be able to explain decisions with significant effects — and "the model said so" is not an explanation.

4. TRUST — What does 92% accuracy mean for these 47 flags?

92% accuracy across millions of transactions means roughly 3.7 of these 47 flags are expected false positives — but we don't know which 3.7. These 8 spaza flags share a plausible contextual explanation (Pick n Pay closure, pensioner spending shift). Acting on all 8 as confirmed fraud means potentially blocking elderly customers from their legitimate funds. A 92% accuracy figure is an average; it is not a guarantee about any specific case. The analyst's professional role is precisely to identify where the model is likely wrong, especially for unusual flag patterns that have alternative explanations. Statistics justify the tool; they don't replace the judgment call.

5. HUMAN — Who is accountable if these customers are wrongly flagged?

The model flagging them is not the decision. The analyst actioning the flag is the decision. Under POPIA Section 71, the bank has an obligation to ensure decisions with significant effects on individuals have human oversight built in. If the analyst actions all 47 flags without reviewing the plausibility of each, they are abdicating the professional role they are employed to perform. The accountability chain: the analyst (who actioned) → the supervisor (who approved the workflow) → the bank's information officer and executives (who designed a system without meaningful human review at the individual-case level). "I was following the model" is not a professional defence; it is a description of the professional failure.
