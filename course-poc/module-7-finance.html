AI in Finance:
When the Model Is Right and the Outcome Is Wrong

South Africa's credit scoring AI is working exactly as designed. And that's the problem. This module is built around a structural failure that isn't a bug — it's a design choice with deep consequences for who gets access to financial services.

60 minutes · 4 phases Finance & Banking track · Requires Module 0 foundation

The Full Picture

Approximately 51% of South African adults are excluded from formal credit markets — not because they're high risk, but because they're invisible to the model.

What the research shows:

SA's major banks have deployed credit scoring AI trained predominantly on formal-economy, payslip-earning, credit-bureau-registered borrowers. The result: a majority of working South Africans — who earn real incomes in the informal economy — fail the model's criteria, not because they can't repay, but because the model was never designed with them in mind. The Capitec CTO stated publicly: "In the South African theme market, traditional creditworthiness models mean most people will fail — you can't just copy-paste those assumptions."

This is not a bug. It's a consequence of which training data was used. The model learned what creditworthiness looks like from historical loan data — data generated by a formal economy built under apartheid, which excluded most Black South Africans from formal financial participation. The model has learned to reproduce those exclusions at scale.

The design question: The electrician in our scenario is not a bad credit risk. She is an invisible credit risk — to a system that was never trained to see her. The question for finance professionals isn't "is the AI working?" It's "working for whom?"

Concept — The regulatory framework and the tools reshaping SA finance
What applies to you and where the gaps are · ~20 min

Why training data is a liability, not just a technical detail

An AI model reflects the world it was trained on. In SA financial services, that world was shaped by decades of exclusion from formal economic participation. Credit models trained on who got credit historically will perpetuate the patterns of who was allowed to get credit. This is bias in the most literal sense: a systematic lean in one direction, built into the model's foundations.

The core insight for finance professionals

The question is not whether the model is calculating correctly — it often is. The question is whether the model is asking the right questions. A model optimised on formal-economy data will perform well on formal-economy applicants and fail systematically on everyone else. That systematic failure is the financial services professional's responsibility to understand and manage.

Three SA regulations finance professionals must know

National Credit Act (NCA) — Sections 80–81 and Affordability Assessment Regulations

Section 80 defines reckless credit — granted without proper affordability assessment. Section 81 requires lenders to assess discretionary income and document how they arrived at the finding. An AI model must be able to explain its affordability determination. The right to a plain-language reason for credit refusal exists in law; AI models that output only reason codes do not meet this standard. The NCR requires AI credit models to be tested for disparate impact across demographic groups.

FSCA-PA AI Report (November 2025) — First comprehensive AI risk assessment of SA financial sector

Based on 2,100+ industry responses. Banks lead AI adoption at 52%. Key identified risks: bias and discrimination, low explainability (especially GenAI), data privacy, systemic vulnerability through vendor concentration. Recommends regular bias audits, mandatory disclosure when AI influences credit decisions. No sector-wide AI governance framework exists yet — professionals are operating in regulatory white space.

POPIA — Section 71 and cross-border data processing

Section 71 creates a right not to be subject to decisions made solely by automated means that have legal or significant effects — directly applicable to AI credit, insurance pricing, and fraud flags. SA financial data cannot be processed offshore without adequate protection agreements. GenAI tools used for client document analysis must comply; free-tier tools do not.

Where AI in SA finance is getting it right — JUMO's alternative credit model: When Lula (SA SME lender using JUMO's engine) incorporated SA-specific regional business payment cycles into their credit model, approval rates for informal retailers increased by 30% while default rates decreased. The technology didn't change. The training data did. This is the most important lesson in this module: the model learns from what you show it. Show it the right data, and inclusion and accuracy go up together.

Apply — The fraud analyst's queue
Run the Funda Five on a real finance scenario · ~15 min
Scenario — Fraud Analyst · FNB

47 flagged transactions. 8 of them are small purchases from spaza shops. The model says suspicious. You have 2 hours.

The AI flags transactions based on deviation from account behaviour profiles. Eight of the 47 flags are under R200 each, from informal vendors with no merchant category code (MCC). The customers are pensioners. Their nearest Pick n Pay closed 60 days ago. They've shifted to spaza purchasing. The model flagged them because their spending pattern changed — which it has, because the local retail landscape changed. The model doesn't know that.

Your SLA: action all 47 flags within 2 hours. The model's stated accuracy: 92%. That means 3.7 of your 47 flags are expected false positives — but you don't know which 3.7.

Your task: Use the Funda Five to work through how to handle the 8 spaza-shop flags — and what they reveal about the limits of the model's design.
1
TASK — What is the fraud AI actually doing?
What type of AI is this (from Module 0: generative, predictive, or computer vision)? What is it optimised for? Why is detecting a pattern change not the same as detecting fraud?
2
DATA — What data does the model have? What's missing?
The model has the transaction record. What real-world context does it not have access to? What would you need to know to make a good judgment on the 8 spaza flags?
3
TOOL — Is the fraud detection model the right tool to make a final decision on these 8 cases?
What should the model's role be — and where should human judgment take over? What would a better workflow look like for low-value anomaly flags?
4
TRUST — The model says 92% accurate. What does that mean in practice for these 47 flags?
Do the maths: if 92% accuracy means ~3.7 false positives in your batch, and these 8 spaza-shop flags look suspicious to you, what is the risk of acting on all of them as fraud? What would you lose if you're wrong?
5
HUMAN — Who is accountable if these 8 customers are wrongly flagged as fraud?
The model flagged them. You're the analyst who acted on the flag. Under the NCA and POPIA, what is your professional accountability here? Who else in the chain bears responsibility?
