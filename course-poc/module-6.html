Encounter
Two systems. Same outcome. Different design. React before we explain · ~15 min
Look at these two systems side by side. Both involve AI and humans working together. Both produce a result that a human reviews before action is taken. But they got to “human reviews before action” in completely different ways. What do you notice about the difference?
System A — Dr Math
Human tutor via MXit (CSIR/UP, 2007–2015)

A maths tutoring service for SA school learners, delivered via MXit — the text-based messaging app that had 8 million SA users before smartphones arrived. Every learner query was answered by a human tutor: largely university student volunteers coordinated by CSIR and the University of Pretoria.

The AI component was limited to routing and matching. The human was the answer. Human-in-the-loop was the design intent, not a regulation or a safety constraint. Dr Math put a person in the loop because that was the service it was offering.

Won multiple education innovation awards. Served over 60,000 learners at peak. Discontinued ~2015 as MXit declined.
System B — CAD4TB (Delft Imaging)
AI-assisted chest X-ray reading for TB screening
CAD4TB Report — Chest X-ray Analysis
ABNORMALITY DETECTED — Confidence: 0.84
Findings consistent with: pulmonary TB pattern
Priority score: HIGH — recommend clinical review
AI model version: CAD4TB v7 — trained on European + limited SA dataset
This output is a decision-support tool. Diagnosis requires clinician review. CAD4TB does not diagnose.

Dutch company. European-dominated training data. Deployed in SA public health clinics — where radiology expertise is scarce. A clinician must review every AI flag before any clinical action is taken.

Used in SA TB screening programs in under-resourced settings where there are not enough trained radiologists.
Both systems have a human reviewing before action. What’s different about why the human is there? What does this difference imply?

Concept
Human-in-loop by design vs by constraint — and a worked example · ~20 min

Human-in-loop by design vs by constraint — why the distinction matters

Both Dr Math and CAD4TB have a human reviewing before action. But the reasons are different, and the difference has real implications for what happens when the human review is removed or compromised.

Dr Math: human-in-loop by design intent. The human tutor was the product. Dr Math didn’t route queries to a human because a regulation required it — it routed them because human tutoring was the point. If you remove the human, you have no service. This is the most robust form of human oversight: the human is structurally load-bearing (meaning the human is what makes the service function at all).

CAD4TB: human-in-loop by constraint. The clinician reviews the AI flag because: (1) SA law does not allow AI to diagnose; (2) the CAD4TB system’s terms of use require clinician review; (3) HPCSA (Health Professions Council of SA) holds the clinician, not the software, accountable for the diagnosis. If you remove the human review — if a harassed clinician starts rubber-stamping AI outputs without genuine review — the system appears to have oversight while actually losing it. The human is there by legal constraint, not structural necessity. This is a weaker form.

Neither is bad. Both are legitimate designs. But when you evaluate any AI system, you should ask: Is the human in the loop because they’re structurally necessary, or because a rule says they have to be? The answer tells you how robust the oversight actually is.

CAD4TB in the SA context — the training data question

CAD4TB is built by Delft Imaging, a Dutch company. Its model was trained predominantly on European chest X-ray datasets. This is relevant in SA for two reasons:

  1. TB presentation variation: TB patterns on chest X-rays can look different in populations with high HIV co-infection rates (common in SA) than in lower-HIV European populations. A model trained on European data may have lower sensitivity for SA-specific presentations.
  2. Resource deployment context: CAD4TB is deployed in SA because radiology capacity is scarce in under-resourced public health clinics. The very settings where it’s used most are those with the least clinician capacity to critically review AI flags. The oversight is formally present but practically thin.

This is not a reason to refuse to use CAD4TB — it performs better than no TB screening in low-resource settings, and the alternative is no radiological assessment at all. But a responsible clinician using it knows these limits and applies judgment accordingly.

What is a Responsible AI stance, and what is a Professional AI Judgment Statement?

Responsible AI is a broad term for the set of principles and practices that govern how AI is developed and deployed in ways that are safe, fair, accountable, transparent, and respectful of human rights. In the AfriversalAI course, Responsible AI is not a compliance checkbox — it is the ongoing professional judgment you exercise every time AI is involved in a consequential decision in your work.

A Professional AI Judgment Statement is a documented professional declaration of how a named professional evaluates AI-assisted decisions and what accountability they retain. It is not a policy; it is a personal professional commitment. It could serve as evidence in a disciplinary inquiry, a professional review, or an audit: evidence that you thought about the AI you used, applied a framework, and retained human judgment.

Worked Example

Professional AI Judgment Statement — Worked Example (fictional scenario for practice)

Sister Thembeka Dlamini, Professional Nurse — Limpopo District Hospital

Context

Our facility uses CAD4TB (Delft Imaging v7) for AI-assisted chest X-ray reading. Radiologist review is not available on-site. All clinical decisions remain mine as the responsible clinician. I document this judgment statement as my professional position on how I evaluate CAD4TB outputs.

What I verify before acting on a CAD4TB flag (TRUST step)

When CAD4TB flags an X-ray as abnormal, I do not act on the AI output alone. I: (1) review the X-ray image myself against the clinical presentation; (2) take a full symptom history with attention to cough duration, weight loss, night sweats, and HIV status; (3) cross-reference the AI confidence score against my own clinical assessment — if these disagree significantly, I note the disagreement and do not default to the AI; (4) consider whether this patient’s clinical context (HIV co-infection, prior TB treatment, nutritional status) may affect X-ray presentation in ways that a European-trained model might miss.

What I know about the tool’s limits (TOOL step)

CAD4TB was trained predominantly on European datasets. HIV co-infection, which affects TB radiological presentation, is prevalent in our patient population. I treat CAD4TB’s outputs as a triage priority signal, not a diagnostic conclusion. A HIGH confidence flag increases my index of suspicion; it does not confirm TB. A low-confidence or negative output does not exclude TB in a high-risk presentation.

What I retain (HUMAN step)

I retain full clinical accountability for every decision made using CAD4TB. Under HPCSA (Health Professions Council of South Africa) guidelines, my registration as a professional nurse is the accountability unit — not the software. “CAD4TB flagged it” is not a clinical finding; “Sister Dlamini assessed the patient and found findings consistent with TB” is a clinical finding. The AI supports my capacity. It does not replace my professional judgment or my legal accountability.

When I escalate

I escalate to the district Medical Officer when: a CAD4TB flag contradicts my clinical assessment and I cannot resolve the disagreement through additional history or repeat X-ray; when I suspect the AI output may be affected by image quality issues (poor positioning, motion artifact); or when a patient has a complex comorbidity profile I believe is outside the model’s training distribution. I document every escalation.

What makes this a Professional AI Judgment Statement: It names the tool, names its limits, specifies the verification steps, explicitly retains human accountability, and states escalation criteria. If Sister Dlamini ever faced a HPCSA inquiry about a CAD4TB-related clinical decision, this document is accountability evidence — proof that she applied professional judgment, not just deferred to the AI.

Model Answers — Synthesis & Professional AI Judgment Statement

1. The pattern across five cases

The structural pattern that appears in every case where AI caused harm: the AI was deployed in a consequential decision context without adequate human oversight of the AI’s outputs — often because the human oversight was nominal, bypassed by time pressure, or absent by design. In each case: the DCDT hallucinated a policy and no human caught it before it spread; the fintech AI scored using proxy discrimination and no human reviewed the outcomes; Riskio scored Siyanda with no human contesting the factors; the election deepfake circulated without source verification; the Meridian ATS screened Lerato without any human seeing her CV. The pattern is not “AI was wrong.” It is: the accountability gap between what the AI decided and what a human verified was never closed.

2. What the DATA step consistently revealed

The DATA step consistently revealed that the data being processed was not neutral — it was carrying history: apartheid geography, historical policing intensity, apartheid-era hiring patterns, manipulated political content. In every case, the AI was not processing objective facts; it was processing the recorded outputs of systems that were themselves biased or manipulated. The DATA step also consistently revealed that the data subjects had rights they weren’t being told about: POPIA Section 71 rights, PEPUDA rights, EEA rights. The one-sentence guidance: “Always ask: what history is encoded in this data, and whose interests shaped how it was collected?”

3. Generalised principle for HUMAN accountability

The principle that emerges across all five cases: A human must be in the loop not just in form, but in substance — meaning they have the information, the time, and the authority to genuinely contest the AI’s output before the consequential decision is finalised. A human who rubber-stamps AI outputs under time pressure, without access to the factors used, is present in form and absent in substance. Human oversight is not enough when: the human lacks the information to contest the AI (the algorithm is opaque); the human faces structural incentives to defer to the AI (time pressure, liability shield, convenience); or the AI is structurally load-bearing (the human can’t reverse its outputs). In those situations, the loop must be redesigned, not assumed.

4. Professional AI Judgment Statement (example)

I, [name], [role at organisation], evaluate AI-assisted decisions using the following standard:

When AI is used in my work for [type of decision], I verify by: (1) checking that the output is consistent with my own professional knowledge of the situation; (2) asking what data was used and whether any of it may carry historical bias in the SA context; (3) confirming that I can see and contest the inputs, not just the output; (4) applying the TRUST step — seeking at least one independent source of corroboration before I act on an AI-assisted recommendation.

I know this tool’s limits: it [specific limitations relevant to my role and tool]. I retain full accountability for every decision I make using AI-assisted tools. “The system recommended it” is not a defence for a professional decision I made; “I evaluated the system’s recommendation and exercised professional judgment” is. I escalate when the AI output conflicts with my professional assessment, when I cannot see the basis for the output, or when the stakes of error are irreversible.

5. Apply to a specific situation

This answer will be specific to each learner’s professional context. The key test: does your answer (a) name a specific AI tool or type of AI tool used in your actual work; (b) describe what your statement requires you to do in that specific context — not in general terms, but concretely; and (c) identify at least one limit of the AI tool that your professional judgment must compensate for? If you find yourself unable to name a specific AI tool in your current role, it is legitimate to say: “AI is not currently deployed in my specific work, but I anticipate it will be used for [X] within [Y months]. My statement prepares me to evaluate that deployment when it arrives.”
