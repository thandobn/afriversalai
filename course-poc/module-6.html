Responsible AI

This is the capstone module. You don’t apply the Funda Five™ to another case. You synthesise what you’ve learned across five scenarios and real-world contexts and write a documented Professional AI Judgment Statement — a form that can contribute to an organisation’s accountability record in a professional review, internal investigation, governance process, or audit.

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Synthesize your Funda Five™ applications from Modules 1–5 into a generalized professional AI judgment standard
  • Articulate a documented professional stance on AI use that could serve as accountability evidence in a professional review, disciplinary inquiry, or audit
  • Distinguish meaningful human oversight from a rubber-stamp review or a person who is only asked to intervene after harm occurs
  • Identify when an AI concern should be documented, paused, escalated, or referred to HR, legal/compliance, data protection, technical specialists, clinical leadership, or executive leadership

3
Concept
Meaningful oversight, the Professional AI Judgment Statement, and a worked example · ~25 min
Screen 1 of 4 — What meaningful human oversight looks like

Three categories of human involvement in AI systems

Both Dr Math and CAD4TB have a human reviewing before action. But the nature of that human involvement is fundamentally different — and the difference matters when you evaluate whether oversight is genuine.

1. Human-led service with AI support. The human provides the substantive service; technology helps with access, routing, retrieval, administration, or scale. Remove the human and you have no service. Example: Dr Math. The tutor was the answer.

2. AI-supported professional judgment. AI provides a score, signal, ranking, or suggestion; a qualified person interprets it in context and remains responsible for the decision. The human brings context, clinical or professional history, threshold choices, and accountability for the outcome. Example: CAD4TB in a defined clinical pathway.

3. Human rubber stamp. A person is technically present but cannot see the relevant evidence, question the system, access excluded alternatives, or meaningfully override the output. Oversight appears intact but has been hollowed out. Example: a recruiter who sees an AI shortlist but cannot inspect rejected candidates or the reasons for exclusion.

The right question for any AI system is not “is there a human in the loop?” It is: Does the person have the context, information, authority, time, and ability to change the outcome?

Human in the loop vs human in control: A human reviewer is not meaningful if they only receive an AI output after the system has already excluded alternatives, if they lack reasons or evidence, if they cannot override, or if workload makes review performative. Human oversight needs information, authority, time, and a genuine ability to change the decision — not just formal presence in the process.

CAD4TB and local-context questions — why validation matters

CAD4TB analyses chest X-rays and produces a score and image-based outputs that can help identify people who may need further TB assessment. It is designed to support screening and programme decisions, particularly where access to experienced image readers is limited.

Its output is not a diagnosis. Thresholds, local population characteristics, symptoms, clinical history, image quality, confirmatory testing, and available follow-up care all affect what should happen next. In responsible use, qualified clinical teams remain accountable for the care pathway.

Responsible deployment does not assume that a tool’s performance transfers automatically from one setting to another. Organisations should examine validation evidence relevant to the population and care pathway where the tool will be used, set appropriate thresholds, and monitor outcomes after deployment. AI should support, not replace, the accountable health practitioner’s professional judgment in clinical care.

Questions any organisation should ask before deploying a clinical AI tool in a new setting

  • What population, care pathway, and disease burden was the tool validated on?
  • Does that match the setting where it will be used?
  • What threshold is appropriate for this workflow and patient population?
  • What confirmatory testing, follow-up pathway, and escalation route is in place?
  • Who is responsible for monitoring outcomes and flagging performance concerns?
  • What happens when the tool’s output conflicts with clinical assessment?
Equity and accessibility: Before approving any AI use, ask: who might be missing, misread, excluded, or disproportionately burdened by this process — and how would we know? In a clinical setting this includes patients with atypical presentations, disability-related histories, language barriers, or limited access to confirmatory care.

What is a Responsible AI stance, and what is a Professional AI Judgment Statement?

Responsible AI is not a compliance checkbox — it is the ongoing professional judgment you exercise every time AI is involved in a consequential decision in your work. It is not deciding whether AI is “good” or “bad.” It is deciding, in a specific situation, whether the task is appropriate, whether the evidence is sufficient, who could be harmed, what safeguards are required, who can stop or override the system, and when the responsible choice is to pause.

A Professional AI Judgment Statement is a documented record of how a professional evaluated an AI-assisted decision in a specific context: what the tool was used for, what evidence and limits were considered, what safeguards were applied, who retained decision authority, and what would trigger escalation.

It is not a legal shield, policy substitute, or full audit report. A contemporaneous judgment statement can contribute to an accountability record by showing what the practitioner knew, what they verified, what limits they considered, what action they took, and when they escalated — but it does not replace institutional policy, evidence of competence, or formal investigation.

The six-element structure

  1. TASK — What the tool is being used for; whether the use is appropriate for the stakes; what should happen if output is wrong or uncertain.
  2. DATA — What information the system uses; what may be missing; what assumptions or proxy variables may shape the output; whether the data is appropriate and lawful.
  3. TOOL — The tool’s intended use, known limits, validation evidence, and whether it is being used in a setting different from the one it was evaluated on.
  4. TRUST — What verification steps are taken before acting on high-impact output; what independent evidence is consulted; what would make the output unreliable.
  5. HUMAN — Who retains accountability; what authority they have to question, override, or stop the outcome; who they escalate to.
  6. ESCALATION AND DOCUMENTATION — Specific triggers for pausing, limiting, or referring the concern; what is recorded; who is responsible for the next decision.
Documented uncertainty: A responsible statement should name what is unknown — not only what is known. “I cannot verify this yet” can be a responsible conclusion when the stakes are high. Claiming certainty you do not have is itself a professional risk.
Escalation needs named owners: When you escalate, name the destination — line manager, clinical lead, legal/compliance, HR, Information Officer, vendor, IT/security, procurement, board committee, or regulator. “Escalate” alone is not a plan.
Stop/pause rule: Your statement should include a specific trigger for pausing or limiting use — what evidence, error pattern, complaint, data gap, or change in context would make continued use unsafe until a review is complete?
Your statement records your role — it does not replace organisational duties: A personal judgment statement documents your reasoning and choices. It does not substitute for the organisation’s duty to have policy, governance, training, logging, procurement controls, monitoring, and complaint routes.

Worked Example

Professional AI Judgment Statement — Worked Example (fictional practice scenario)

Dr Thembeka Dlamini, Medical Officer — Limpopo District Hospital

TASK — What the tool is being used for

Our facility uses CAD4TB (Delft Imaging) for AI-assisted chest X-ray screening to support TB triage where on-site radiology capacity is limited. The tool is used to prioritise patients for further clinical assessment, not to replace clinical diagnosis or determine treatment. If CAD4TB output is unavailable or unreliable, patients proceed through clinical symptom assessment alone.

DATA — What information is involved

The system processes digital chest X-ray images. Clinically relevant context — symptom history, HIV status, prior TB treatment, weight loss, cough duration, nutritional status, and image quality — is not automatically available to the model. I ensure that each clinical assessment integrates the imaging output with the full patient presentation.

TOOL — Limits and fitness for purpose

CAD4TB produces a priority score and image-based output that may help identify patients who need further assessment. I treat this score as a triage priority signal, not a diagnostic conclusion. A HIGH confidence flag increases my index of suspicion; it does not confirm TB. A low-confidence or negative output does not exclude TB in a high-risk clinical presentation. I have reviewed the validation evidence available for our programme setting and apply the threshold recommended for our pathway.

TRUST — What I verify before acting

When CAD4TB flags an X-ray as abnormal, I do not act on the AI output alone. I correlate the score with the patient assessment, available history, and image quality. If the AI output and my clinical assessment disagree significantly, I note the disagreement, do not default to the AI, and follow the referral pathway. I cannot verify this score alone — that is why the clinical correlation step is non-negotiable.

HUMAN — Accountability and oversight

I retain full clinical accountability for every decision made using CAD4TB, in accordance with HPCSA ethical guidelines for AI-assisted clinical practice. My professional registration is the accountability unit — not the software. “CAD4TB flagged it” is not a clinical finding; “Dr Dlamini assessed the patient and found findings consistent with TB” is a clinical finding. The AI supports my capacity. It does not replace my professional judgment or my legal accountability.

ESCALATION AND DOCUMENTATION

I pause my reliance on CAD4TB and escalate to the district TB Programme Lead or supervising consultant when: a CAD4TB output contradicts my clinical assessment and I cannot resolve the disagreement; when image quality problems (poor positioning, motion artifact) may have affected the output; or when a patient has a complex presentation I believe is outside the expected model performance. I document every escalation. If I observe a pattern of outputs that consistently diverge from clinical outcomes, I report this to the programme supervisor responsible for tool monitoring.

Which is a strong professional AI statement?

Statement A: “The AI is accurate, so I trust its recommendation.”  |  Statement B: “I use the tool only within its intended purpose, verify high-impact output, retain decision responsibility, document uncertainty, and escalate defined concerns.”

Statement B. Accuracy alone does not make a statement professional. A model can have high average accuracy and still be unreliable for a specific patient, context, or presentation. Trusting accuracy without verification, named accountability, or an escalation trigger is a rubber stamp — not oversight. A strong professional AI statement names what the tool does, what it cannot see, who is responsible, and what would make continued use unsafe.

What makes the worked example a Professional AI Judgment Statement: It names all six elements: task scope, data context, tool limits, verification steps, named human accountability with HPCSA grounding, and specific escalation criteria with a named owner. It can contribute to an accountability record in a professional review — as evidence of deliberate professional judgment, not passive deference to the AI.

Statement structure

TASK — When AI is used for [specific task], confirm its use is appropriate for the stakes and does not replace a decision requiring human judgment.

DATA — Consider what information the system uses, what may be missing, what assumptions or proxy variables may shape the output, and whether the data is appropriate and lawful.

TOOL — State the tool’s intended use, known limits, validation evidence, and whether it is being used in a setting different from the one it was evaluated on.

Model Answers — Synthesis & Professional AI Judgment Statement

1. The recurring pattern across the five scenarios

The structural pattern that recurs across every scenario where harm became more likely: AI was used in a consequential decision context without adequate meaningful human oversight — because the human oversight was nominal, bypassed by time pressure, absent by design, or not backed by enough information, authority, or time to be genuine. In each scenario: no human caught the hallucinated DCDT policy before it spread; no human reviewed fintech scoring outcomes for proxy discrimination; no human contested the risk score applied to Siyanda; the deepfake circulated without source verification; Lerato’s application was screened without any human seeing her CV. The pattern is not “AI was wrong.” It is: the accountability gap between what the AI decided and what a human verified was never closed.

2. What the DATA step consistently helped you question or uncover

The DATA step consistently helped uncover that the data being processed was not neutral — it was carrying history: apartheid geography, historical policing intensity, apartheid-era hiring patterns, manipulated political content, missing information about who was not in the data at all. In every scenario, the AI was not processing objective facts; it was processing the recorded outputs of systems that were themselves shaped by power, exclusion, or manipulation. The DATA step also revealed that the data subjects had rights they were not being told about. The one-sentence guidance: “Always ask: what history is encoded in this data, what is missing, and whose interests shaped how it was collected?”

3. What conditions make a human accountable rather than a rubber stamp

The principle that emerges across all five scenarios: Meaningful human accountability requires that the person has the information, time, and authority to genuinely question the AI’s output before a consequential decision is finalised. A human who approves AI outputs without seeing the evidence, understanding the factors used, or having any practical ability to override is present in form and absent in substance. A rubber stamp looks like: the person sees only the recommendation, cannot inspect excluded alternatives, has no time to engage, and routinely approves. The loop is not enough when the human lacks the information to contest the output, faces structural pressure to defer, or cannot reverse the AI’s decision. In those situations the oversight must be redesigned, not assumed.

4. Professional AI Judgment Statement (example)

I, [name], [role at organisation], evaluate AI-assisted decisions using the following standard:

When AI is used in my work for [type of decision], I verify by: (1) checking that the output is consistent with my own professional knowledge of the situation; (2) asking what data was used and whether any of it may carry historical bias in the SA context; (3) confirming that I can see and contest the inputs, not just the output; (4) applying the TRUST step — seeking at least one independent source of corroboration before I act on an AI-assisted recommendation.

I know this tool’s limits: it [specific limitations relevant to my role and tool]. I retain full accountability for every decision I make using AI-assisted tools. “The system recommended it” is not a defence for a professional decision I made; “I evaluated the system’s recommendation and exercised professional judgment” is. I escalate when the AI output conflicts with my professional assessment, when I cannot see the basis for the output, or when the stakes of error are irreversible.
