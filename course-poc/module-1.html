Research indicates that the rapid adoption of generative AI tools in South African workplaces has significantly outpaced institutional readiness. Lesson 1 of 3 — What happened & why
In Module 0 you learned that AI predicts rather than knows — it generates plausible-sounding text whether or not that text is accurate. What you read in the Encounter phase is a direct consequence of that. Let's name it.
Lesson 1

What happened — and why

What you were reading was a simulation of AI hallucination — in a real-world policy context. The excerpt was created for this course. The three sources cited in it do not exist.
Course simulation vs real case: The document above was not drawn from the actual SA government draft. The real incident involved fabricated references in an 86-page policy document published for public comment — different sources, same failure mode.

What actually happened in April 2026

South Africa's Department of Communications and Digital Technologies (DCDT) published an 86-page draft national AI policy for public comment. Shortly after publication, journalists and officials identified fictitious references in the document's bibliography — sources that appeared to have been generated by AI.

The Minister said the most plausible explanation was that AI-generated citations had been included without proper human verification. Sixteen days after publication, the Minister announced that the draft would be withdrawn. South Africa's AI policy process had to restart.

This was not simply a technical error. It was a failure of verification, review and accountability. A document can look polished, cite specific research and still fail the most basic professional test: are the claims and sources real?

The problem was not that the document looked unprofessional. The problem was that professional-looking details were treated as evidence before anyone checked the original sources.
Lesson 2

Why this happens — and why it matters for you

What is AI hallucination?

You already know from Module 0 that AI predicts rather than knows. When asked for a source, a language model may produce something that has the shape of a real citation — an author, article title, journal and page numbers — without that source being real.

This is called hallucination — when a generative AI produces text that sounds real and credible but is not grounded in an actual source. The term does not mean the AI is confused or lying: it means the generation process produces plausible-sounding output regardless of whether a real source exists. The same process runs whether the citation is real or fabricated — the model has no internal check that changes the output when a source doesn't exist.

AI does not reliably warn you when this happens. A citation can look complete and authoritative even when it cannot be independently verified. The output looks exactly the same whether the source is real or fabricated.

Why this matters for professionals

Whenever AI helps draft a report, summarise research, or support a recommendation, the output still needs human verification. Confidence, detail and professional formatting are not evidence.

AI will not reliably catch its own unsupported claims. Human review and organisational verification processes are still responsible for checking the output before others act on it.

Your sector context

If you create, approve, share, or act on an AI-assisted document, you are part of the accountability chain. "I used AI" does not remove the responsibility to check the claims before others rely on them.

This applies even when a human has edited or approved the final document. AI-assisted work can still contain AI-generated errors — the editing or approval step does not automatically catch what the underlying model generated.
Lesson 3

The signals you could have caught

Warning signs — not a lie detector

A document can pass every quick check below and still be wrong. These checks tell you when to slow down and verify — not when to trust automatically. Look back at the course excerpt. Three patterns a trained reader checks:

Check 1 — Can the institution be found?

In the course simulation, "South African Institute of Digital Futures Research" and "Pan-African Technology Readiness Council" do not exist — a search returns nothing. Real policy documents cite organisations with established public presences, official websites, and a track record of publications. If you cannot find the institution through an official or credible source, treat the citation as unverified.

Check 2 — Can the statistic be traced?

A precise number is not automatically suspicious — real research can produce exact figures. The question is traceability: What was measured? Over what period? In which population? Who conducted the study? Can you find the original report or dataset? If a specific figure cannot be traced to its method and source, treat it as unverified.

Check 3 — Can you reach the original source?

If you cannot verify a source quickly, do not rely on it yet — treat the claim as unverified and escalate the check. Where to look: the official publisher or government archive; a DOI or journal website; a library database; the author's institutional profile; or ask the person who shared the document for the original.

What verifiable looks like — vs what it doesn't
Use for: recognising patterns when reading a document
Weak / unverified citation Strong / verifiable citation
Institution cannot be found Official website or recognised journal record exists
Generic or vague report title Original publication title and date confirmed
No accessible trail to the source Government archive, DOI, publisher page, or library record
Statistic without method or context Population, timeframe, method, and original dataset named
Finding a real source is Step 1. Checking whether it actually supports the claim being made is Step 2. A real study can be outdated, from a different country or population, or cited in a way that overstates what it shows.

Pocket checklist — use this before acting on any AI-generated document — Use for: deciding what to do before acting

Can I find the named institution or author through an official or credible source?
Can I locate the original publication, report, or dataset — not just a citation to it?
Do the statistic's population, date, and method match the claim being made?
Can I access at least one independent source — an official URL, DOI, journal record, or government archive?

If any answer is "no" or "I'm not sure" — do not treat the claim as verified.

AfriversalAI Core Framework

The Funda Five™

Five questions every professional should ask before acting on AI output — or before using AI to produce something others will act on. You'll use these in every module. By Module 6, asking them will be automatic.

1
TASK
What is this AI being used for, who could it affect, and what happens if it is wrong?
Is this the right kind of task for AI? What is at stake if the output is inaccurate, unfair, delayed, or misunderstood? Low-risk drafting is different from decisions about employment, credit, healthcare, legal rights, or public policy.
2
DATA
What information is being entered, used, or produced?
What sensitive, personal, confidential, regulated, or copyrighted information is involved? What evidence or source material is the system relying on? What information could be exposed, distorted, omitted, or invented?
3
TOOL
What kind of system is this, and what are its likely limits?
Is it generating text, scoring risk, recommending options, matching identities, interpreting images, retrieving information, or taking actions? Does it rely on a model, fixed rules, external sources, or a mix? Different systems fail in different ways.
4
TRUST
How do I verify this before acting?
AI confidence is not evidence. What specific steps would confirm or deny this output? Can you name the original source, person, record, calculation, policy, or independent expert who would verify the claim?
5
HUMAN
Who chose, supplied, reviewed, approved, and acted on this output — and who remains accountable?
"The AI did it" is not a defence. Trace the full accountability chain: who chose the tool, set the rules, supplied the data, reviewed the output, approved the decision, and is responsible if harm occurs?
Now apply them. You've just read the DCDT scenario in detail. Work through each of the five questions as if you were the professional who received that document and had to decide what to do with it.
Step 1 of 5 — Task
1
TASK — What is this AI being used for, who could it affect, and what happens if it is wrong?
What job was AI doing in this document? Is that the right kind of job for AI? What was at stake?

Example — what a strong response looks like

1

"AI was used to draft evidence for a public policy document. This is high-risk because policy claims influence public decisions and must be traceable to real, verifiable sources."

It names what AI was doing, then evaluates whether that was appropriate.

Step 2 of 5 — Data
2
DATA — What information is being entered, used, or produced?
What kind of evidence or information did the AI produce? Is any of it sensitive, regulated, or difficult to verify?
Confidentiality reminder: Use a general description only. Do not enter confidential company, client, employee, customer, financial, health, legal, or personal information.

Step 3 of 5 — Tool
3
TOOL — What kind of system is this, and what are its likely limits?
What type of AI was likely used here? What does that kind of system do well — and where does it fail?

Example — what a strong response looks like

3

"This was a large language model — a text-generation system that predicts what words should come next. These systems produce fluent, professional-sounding output but have no internal check for whether a source actually exists. They are unreliable for citation because the generation process is the same whether the source is real or fabricated."

It names the system type, then explains its relevant failure mode for this specific task.

Step 4 of 5 — Trust
4
TRUST — How do I verify this before acting?
What exact steps would you take to verify this before using or sharing it?
Remember: Unverified is not the same as false. It means: do not rely on this claim until you can trace it to an original, credible source. If you cannot verify a high-stakes claim yourself, escalate it — to a subject-matter expert, your compliance team, or the document's author.

Step 5 of 5 — Human
5
HUMAN — Who chose, supplied, reviewed, approved, and acted on this output?
If someone made a decision based on this document and the sources turned out not to exist — who is responsible? What should have been in place?

Example — what a strong response looks like

5

"Responsibility sat with the team that chose to use an AI tool for policy drafting, the drafter who included AI-generated citations, the reviewer who did not check them, the official who approved publication, and the department that allowed the document to reach the public. Not one person — the whole chain. What should have existed: a mandatory citation verification step before any AI-assisted document is submitted for approval."

It names every link in the chain, not just the final approver.

Model answer — compare yours:

1 — TASK: "AI was used to draft evidence for a public policy document. This is high-risk because policy claims influence public decisions and must be traceable to real, verifiable sources — AI cannot reliably produce these."
2 — DATA: "The document included research claims, statistics, citations, institutional names, and policy recommendations. These are not necessarily private, but they are high-impact and require reliable sourcing. The risk was that AI fabricated sources that look like real publications."
3 — TOOL: "A large language model. These are excellent at producing fluent, authoritative-sounding text. They are unreliable at factual citation — they generate plausible-sounding references whether or not the source exists."
4 — TRUST: "Each citation should have been checked against the original source before publication: official government archive, journal record, DOI, publisher website, or named institution. If a citation cannot be verified independently, treat the claim as unverified — and escalate rather than publishing."
5 — HUMAN: "Responsibility sat with the people who selected the tool, drafted the document, reviewed the references, approved publication, and acted on the policy. AI does not carry institutional responsibility. What should have existed: a mandatory source-verification step before any AI-assisted document reaches publication or a decision. Where AI output falsely attributes work or statements to a named individual — even a fictional one in a draft — this also raises POPIA accuracy obligations: personal information must not be misleading, incomplete, or incorrectly attributed."
