Module 7 · Sector Specific · Government & Public Sector

AI and the Citizen:
Accountability in the Public Sector

When government deploys AI, the people most affected often have the least power to challenge it. This module starts with a real message that millions of South Africans have received — and traces who is responsible when that message is wrong.

60 minutes · 4 phases Government & Public Sector track · Requires Module 0 foundation

By the end of this module, you will be able to

  • Explain why automated government decisions require a higher accountability standard than equivalent private-sector AI decisions
  • Identify the constitutional and statutory framework for contestable government AI decisions (Section 33, PAJA s3/s6, PAIA, POPIA)
  • Apply the consequence-calibrated autonomy principle to assess appropriate AI decision authority in a public-sector context
  • Apply the Funda Five to a government AI scenario involving citizen rights or access to public benefits

What Actually Happened

The decision was made by an automated multi-database cross-reference engine — with no human in the loop.

How SASSA's SRD system works:

Every month, SASSA's system cross-references applicant data against SARS, UIF, NSFAS, Home Affairs, and bank account records. If any bank account shows deposits totalling R628 or more in a calendar month, the system automatically declines the application. No human reviews a flagged case before the decline is issued. The decision is fully automated.

The system cannot distinguish between a salary and a once-off informal payment. A domestic worker who received R700 for a single cleaning job is flagged the same as someone with regular employment income. There is no explanation in the SMS of what "Alternative Income Source" was found, or where it came from.

The accountability gap: An IEJ analysis found approximately 75% of social grant fraud cases reported to police between 2014 and 2025 involved government officials, contractors, or employees — not beneficiaries. (IEJ UBIG Factsheet 13, December 2025) Yet all automated fraud-targeting is aimed at the beneficiary population. When a legitimate recipient is wrongly declined, there is no named person at SASSA responsible for that error. The algorithm is the decision-maker. Nobody owns the mistake.

This is not a failure in the technology. It's a failure in the design of accountability around the technology.

3
Concept — The framework for public sector AI accountability
Laws, obligations, and where the gaps are · ~20 min

Why automated decisions need a different standard in government

When a private company makes a bad recommendation, a customer can take their business elsewhere. When a government system wrongly denies someone a grant they need to eat, there is no alternative. The stakes of AI errors in public services are categorically different — and the legal framework reflects that, though incompletely.

The core principle

Automated government decisions that affect citizens' rights, benefits, or legal status must be explainable, contestable, and linked to a named accountable official — not just "the system." This is not optional ethics. It's the constitutional obligation under Section 33 (just administrative action) — implemented procedurally by PAJA s3 (procedurally fair administrative action) and s6 (grounds of judicial review) — and the statutory obligations under PAIA and POPIA.

The consequence-calibrated autonomy principle

The appropriate level of AI decision authority is proportional to the reversibility and consequence of the outcome. A grant denial that can be appealed and reversed within weeks is a different risk category from a disability assessment that determines housing eligibility for five years. The less reversible the outcome and the more vulnerable the affected person, the more human judgment must be substantively in the decision loop — not as a signature formality, but as a real check. This principle is implicit in Section 33 and PAJA; applying it to AI deployments means asking: if this decision is wrong, how hard is it to fix, and who pays the cost of the error?

The four laws that apply when government AI goes wrong

POPIA Section 71 — Right against automated decision-making

A data subject may not be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing if that decision has legal or substantial personal consequences. SASSA must provide an opportunity for the affected person to make representations and must disclose sufficient information about the logic used. Critical gap: POPIA doesn't require SASSA to tell the person the decision was automated. Without notification, the right can't be invoked.

PAIA — Promotion of Access to Information Act

Citizens can request the records behind a government decision that affects them. In theory, a declined SASSA beneficiary can file a PAIA request to understand what data drove the automated exclusion. In practice: PAIA requests can take months; there is no requirement to produce the algorithm's logic — only the data held about the individual.

PEPUDA — Equality Act

Algorithmic systems that discriminate on race, gender, disability, or socioeconomic status violate PEPUDA. An AI trained on historical data reflecting apartheid-era inequality can reproduce that inequality. Courts can order audits and wide remedial relief. Under PEPUDA s13, once a statistical disparate outcome on a listed ground is shown, the burden of justification shifts to the respondent — model access is not required to establish a prima facie case. What is not currently required is proactive algorithmic disclosure before harm occurs.

PFMA — Public Finance Management Act

Accounting officers (directors-general, municipal managers) are personally accountable for systems deployed with public funds. When AI causes harm — wrongly withholding grants, wrongly targeting citizens — PFMA creates a chain of accountability upward. In practice, this has not yet been invoked for AI-caused harm in SA.

Where public sector AI does work: MomConnect

MomConnect — advisory, not gatekeeping: The Department of Health's WhatsApp-based maternal health platform handles 880,000+ questions across all 11 official languages. Its AI detects urgency and routes serious cases to human health workers. What makes it work: the AI extends human capacity, it doesn't replace human judgment on consequential decisions. A false positive results in a human call. A false negative in SASSA's system results in a person going without food money for a month. The difference is in how the human override is designed.

The 5 AI systems active in SA government right now

SASSA SRD — automated eligibility engine

Cross-references SARS, UIF, NSFAS, Home Affairs, bank data. No human review before exclusion decisions.

SARS — AI auto-assessment

5.8 million taxpayers auto-assessed in 2025. ML flags high-risk taxpayers for human audit. Dispute pathway exists but requires taxpayer initiative.

Home Affairs — biometric verification

Facial recognition + fingerprint matched against National Population Register. Used by SASSA, Treasury, and banks. 2025 upgrade reduced error rate below 1%.

Vumacam — Safe City surveillance (Joburg)

7,000+ cameras, AI license plate recognition, 28,000 "vehicle of interest" alerts per day. Private company operating public safety infrastructure. Affluent areas have coverage; townships don't.

Department of Health — MomConnect AI (WhatsApp)

422,000+ monthly users. 83% FAQ accuracy, 85% urgency detection accuracy. ~100,000 urgent cases escalated to human health workers. All 11 official languages. SMS fallback for non-smartphone users.

Apply — The SASSA field officer's dilemma
Run the Funda Five on a real public sector scenario · ~15 min
Scenario — SASSA Field Officer · Khayelitsha

You have a queue of declined beneficiaries. The system says they all have "Alternative Income." You have no override.

A 47-year-old domestic worker shows you an R800 bank deposit from March — she cleaned someone's house once. The system flagged her. You have no authority to override the automated decision. You can only direct her to the online appeals portal, which requires a smartphone and data she doesn't have. Fifteen more people are in the queue behind her.

Your task: Use the Funda Five to analyse what is happening in this system — and what you, as the public servant in the room, can and cannot do about it.
1
TASK — Is this the right job for AI?
What is the AI being asked to do in SASSA's system? Is cross-referencing financial databases an appropriate AI task? Where does the task scope go wrong?
2
DATA — What data is the system using, and what is it missing?
What data sources does the automated system cross-reference? What context about the beneficiary does it not have access to? How does missing context produce wrong decisions?
3
TOOL — Is a fully automated system appropriate for benefit eligibility decisions at this stakes level?
What would a better-designed system look like? At minimum, what human oversight should exist before a decline is issued?
4
TRUST — What would it take to verify whether the system's decision is correct in this specific case?
You're the field officer. What information would you need to assess whether this specific decline is legitimate or an error? Do you have access to that information?
5
HUMAN — Who is accountable for this error? What can you do from where you sit?
Name the specific people or roles that should bear accountability for a wrongful decline in this system. As a field officer with no override, what legitimate channels do you have — and what is your professional obligation to the person in front of you?

Model Answers — SASSA Field Officer Scenario

1. TASK — Is database cross-referencing the right AI task? Where does scope go wrong?

Automated database cross-referencing for eligibility screening is a legitimate AI task — it's faster and more consistent than manual cross-referencing at SASSA's scale. The scope problem is not the task itself but how the output is used: the system was designed to screen for potential ineligibility, but it operates as a final decision-maker. A flag should trigger human review, not an automated decline. The AI is doing the right job; the job has been given too much authority. The scope error is the difference between screening and deciding — and that line was crossed without accountability.

2. DATA — What data does the system use? What's missing?

The system cross-references SARS, UIF, NSFAS, Home Affairs, and bank records. What it does not have: context about whether income is once-off or recurring, knowledge of informal work patterns, awareness of what a specific bank deposit actually represents. The R628 threshold treats all bank income identically — a single cleaning job equals a month's salary to this system. The missing data is context: the kind of context that a human case worker in the room can assess in minutes, but a database cross-reference cannot. Missing context at this volume produces systematic errors at scale, affecting the most vulnerable applicants most often.

3. TOOL — Is fully automated decline appropriate at this stakes level?

At this stakes level — a person's ability to buy food — the tool should be advisory, not determinative. The design that works is: automated screening flags potential ineligibility → a human reviews before a decline is issued. MomConnect demonstrates the alternative: when AI triage leads to a human call-back for urgent health cases, false negatives are caught. When AI triage directly declines without review in SASSA's system, false negatives mean people without food money for a month, with an appeal pathway that requires digital access they don't have. The consequence of the error shapes how much autonomy the AI should be allowed to exercise.

4. TRUST — What would you need to verify this specific decline?

As a field officer, to verify this decline I would need: which data source triggered the flag (SARS? UIF? bank record?), the specific amount and transaction type that was flagged, and how the system classified the income type. I currently have access to none of this — I see only the outcome. The system is a black box from my operational position. I cannot assess whether the decline is correct or erroneous with the information available to me. This is itself a governance problem: the system makes decisions I cannot audit, affecting people I am responsible to serve.

5. HUMAN — Who is accountable? What can you do from where you sit?

Named accountability chain: the system architect who designed decline-without-review, the SASSA official who signed off on that design, and the Director-General under PFMA for public funds and citizens' rights. As a field officer with no override authority, my professional obligations include: documenting this case in writing, escalating to my supervisor, directing the beneficiary to the appeal process and noting that she lacks digital access, and flagging the digital access barrier as a systemic issue through official channels. I cannot fix the system. I can document the failure in a way that creates a paper trail — because accountability requires evidence, and evidence starts with the person in the room who saw what happened.
