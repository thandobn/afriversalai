AI Judgment for South African Professionals

Core Course Outline — AfriversalAI

Prepared June 2026  ·  For stakeholder review  ·  afriversalai@gmail.com

1. Executive Summary

AfriversalAI is a South African AI literacy initiative delivering a six-module online course that equips working professionals with the critical judgment skills needed to use AI responsibly in their roles. The course is built around real South African case studies, delivered in an interactive online-first format, and assessed through a real-world performance task rather than a quiz.

The course is designed for the professional in the middle — not a developer, not a C-suite executive, but the team lead, analyst, coordinator, HR manager, or administrator already encountering AI at work and needing to know what to do with it.

The core outcome: Learners leave with one repeatable framework — the Funda Five — that they can apply to any AI situation in their workplace in two minutes, without technical knowledge.

2. The Problem We're Solving

In April 2026, South Africa's Department of Communications and Digital Technologies published a national AI policy draft that contained fabricated academic citations — generated by AI without verification. The professionals who wrote it were not careless. They simply did not have the framework to know when to question AI output.

This is not an isolated incident. It is the gap that exists across South African workplaces right now:

  • 1.4 million South Africans have been trained on AI tools by programmes like Microsoft SA's initiative — on how to use them, not how to evaluate them.
  • 37% gender penalty faced by women-led SMEs in African fintech credit scoring due to AI bias — often invisible to the human decision-maker acting on the recommendation.
  • 5–15% completion rate for standard self-paced AI courses. The judgment gap doesn't close through passive learning.

Existing training programmes teach button-pressing. AfriversalAI teaches judgment.

3. Course Philosophy

African-centred, not imported

Every case study, example, and regulatory reference is drawn from the South African context. POPIA, the Employment Equity Act, the DCDT policy crisis, the 2024 election deepfakes — learners encounter real events from their own professional and civic landscape, not anonymised US or European scenarios.

Judgment over tools

AI tools change rapidly. The judgment skills to evaluate any AI output do not. This course teaches transferable critical thinking, not software tutorials. A learner who completes the course will be able to evaluate any AI system they encounter — including ones that did not exist when the course was written.

Demonstrated competency, not completion

The AfriversalAI certificate is not awarded for watching videos or finishing modules. It is earned by submitting a real-world evaluation of an AI system from the learner's own job, assessed by a trained human assessor against a published rubric. The certificate says "demonstrated," not "completed."

Freire-informed pedagogy

The course draws on Paulo Freire's philosophy of education as a process of critical consciousness-raising — not the "banking model" of depositing information into passive learners. Each module begins with experience before theory. Learners engage with a real artifact, reflect on their instincts, and then receive the conceptual framework that explains what they were sensing. The learning emerges from the tension between experience and understanding.

Accessible by design

AI is new to most people. The course assumes no prior technical knowledge. Jargon is introduced only when necessary, always defined in plain language, and grounded in a concrete example from the learner's sector. Every concept is explained as if the learner has never formally thought about AI before — because most haven't.

5. The Funda Five Framework

The Funda Five is AfriversalAI's proprietary decision framework. It gives every learner a consistent, repeatable method for evaluating any AI situation at work — in approximately two minutes, without technical knowledge. It is introduced in Module 1 and applied in every subsequent module.

The Funda Five: Task → Data → Tool → Trust → Human
Five questions. Any AI situation. Two minutes.
Step Question What it checks
1. Task Is this the right kind of job for AI? Whether the task's risk profile and verification requirements suit AI assistance
2. Data What information is involved? Is any of it POPIA-protected? Data sensitivity, confidentiality obligations, and whether the AI tool is approved for this data type
3. Tool Is this the right AI for this task? Is it approved? Tool-task fit, organisational approval, and known limitations of the AI type being used
4. Trust How would I detect an error in this output? Whether the verification step is achievable, specific, and proportionate to the stakes
5. Human Who checks this? Who is accountable if it's wrong? Whether a clear review step and accountability chain exist before AI output reaches anyone who could be affected

6. Module-by-Module Breakdown

Each module follows the same four-phase structure. Time estimates are for self-paced completion; facilitated cohort sessions typically run 90–120 minutes per module with group discussion integrated into the Reflect and Apply phases.

Four-phase structure per module: Encounter (~20 min) → Reflect (~20 min) → Concept (~30 min) → Apply (~30 min)
Total per module: approximately 100 minutes self-paced.
0
AI Fundamentals: What Is It, Really?
Foundation module · Free · All sectors · Required for all learners
Why this module exists: The 6 core modules assume no AI foundation — but without one, learners treat AI as magic or dismiss it as hype. Module 0 builds the shared vocabulary and conceptual grounding that all sector trajectories depend on. It is free, public, and completed before Cohort 1 begins.

Learning objectives — learners will be able to:

  • Define AI in plain, non-technical language and distinguish it from other software
  • Identify the three most common types of AI encountered in professional settings (Generative, Predictive/Decision, Computer Vision)
  • Name at least five things AI cannot do — regardless of how confident its output sounds
  • Spot one AI system in their own workplace and classify it by type
  • Identify one failure mode or bias risk in a system they already use
EncounterSort 10 everyday tools: AI or not AI?
ReflectWhat surprised you? Where is AI in your own job?
ConceptWhat AI is, what it isn't — the 3 types, 5 things it cannot do
ApplyClassify one tool from your workplace and name a failure mode

Duration: ~45 minutes self-paced. No live session required — learners complete before cohort kickoff. Access: module-0.html — no registration required.

7. Cohort Delivery Model

AfriversalAI is delivered in structured six-week cohorts, not as open-ended self-paced content. The cohort model is the most significant design decision in the course: research consistently shows 70%+ completion rates for cohort-based learning versus 5–15% for self-paced. The accountability, peer discussion, and scheduled touchpoints are not optional extras — they are the mechanism by which learning actually happens.

Cohort cycle

Pre-cohort — Week 0
Orientation & welcome
Learners complete the welcome video, access their sector learning path, and are introduced to their cohort group. Technology check and expectations setting.
Weeks 1–2
Modules 1 & 2 — What AI Is and How It Fails
Self-paced module completion + live group session (60 min) at end of Week 2. Facilitator debriefs the DCDT case with the cohort; peer discussion of Module 2 Apply exercises.
Weeks 3–4
Modules 3 & 4 — Bias and Verification
Self-paced + live session (60 min) at end of Week 4. Guest speaker option: SA AI researcher or civil society voice on algorithmic discrimination. Capstone brief issued at Module 5 kickoff.
Weeks 5–6
Modules 5 & 6 — Accountability and Synthesis
Self-paced + final live session (90 min). Learners share their capstone AI judgment statements. Capstone submission window opens at end of Week 6.
Post-cohort — 2 weeks
Assessment window & break
Capstone submissions assessed by trained human assessor. Results returned within 7 days. One revision opportunity for learners below standard on any criterion. New cohort begins after 2-week break.

Live touchpoints

  • 3 live group sessions per cohort (Weeks 2, 4, and 6) — facilitated by AfriversalAI
  • Q&A sessions — asynchronous via cohort discussion space; facilitator responds within 24 hours
  • Guest speakers — minimum one per cohort: SA AI professional, legal expert, or sector practitioner

8. Assessment & Certification

The capstone brief

Issued at Module 5. Learners submit after Module 6. One revision opportunity if below standard on any criterion.

Task: Identify one AI system or AI-generated output encountered in your professional context during this course. Produce a written evaluation of 600–900 words that: (1) describes what the AI system does and how it is used in your context; (2) identifies at least two specific ways the system could produce biased, incorrect, or unaccountable outputs; (3) applies the Funda Five to assess whether the output should be acted on; (4) states clearly what you would do — use it, modify how you use it, flag a concern, or recommend against it — and explains why; (5) identifies who in your organisation is accountable if the AI produces a harmful outcome.

Assessment rubric

Learners must meet or exceed standard on at least 4 of 5 criteria. All assessment is conducted by a trained human assessor — not automated scoring.

Criterion Below Standard Meets Standard Exceeds Standard
Describes AI accurately Vague or incorrect description Clear, jargon-free description of the system's function Shows understanding of how the system learns and fails
Identifies failure modes Generic ("AI can be biased") Two specific mechanisms explained with reasoning Mechanisms tied to SA context and evidence
Applies Funda Five Does not apply the framework Applies all five steps with reasoning Extends framework to context-specific considerations
Makes a judgment No clear position taken Clear judgment with stated reasoning Judgment addresses competing considerations
Accountability "The company is responsible" (generic) Named accountability chain with specific role Identifies gaps and suggests what should be in place

9. Welcome Video Outline

The welcome video is the first thing learners see before Module 1. It sets the tone, builds trust, and tells the learner exactly what to expect. Recommended length: 8–10 minutes. Record in a professional but warm setting.

Suggested structure

  1. Open with the problem (1 min) — "You're probably already using AI at work. Maybe ChatGPT for emails, maybe an AI tool your company adopted, maybe something you're not even sure counts as AI. Here's what we know: most of us were never taught how to evaluate what it gives us. This course changes that."
  2. Who we are (1 min) — Introduce AfriversalAI. Why South Africa, why now, why this matters locally and globally.
  3. What you'll learn (2 min) — Walk through the six modules briefly. Emphasise that every module is built around something that actually happened — in South Africa. Name two or three of the case studies to build curiosity.
  4. The Funda Five (2 min) — Introduce the framework by name. "By the end of Module 1, you'll have a tool you can use in two minutes on any AI situation at work. Here's what it looks like…" Walk through the five steps at a high level. Don't explain — just name and tease.
  5. How the course works (2 min) — Cohort model, live sessions, the capstone. Be honest: "This is not a passive course. You will write, you will apply, you will be assessed. Because the only way to build judgment is to practice it."
  6. Close (1 min) — "I'm glad you're here. South Africa needs professionals who can think critically about AI — not just use it. Let's build that skill together. See you in Module 1."
