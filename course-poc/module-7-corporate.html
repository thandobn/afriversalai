Core Course / Module 7 · COABU

AI in Your Workplace:
Verification, Governance & Accountability

A recent South African policy failure showed what can happen when AI-generated research enters a high-stakes workflow without independent verification. The same risk appears in corporate reports, proposals, board packs, client communications, market research, compliance work, and internal decision-making. This module shows where AI output enters a business workflow, what must be verified before it is acted on, and who remains accountable when an AI-assisted decision goes wrong.

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Identify high-risk points where AI output enters a corporate workflow
  • Distinguish polished AI-generated content from independently verified evidence
  • Map responsibility across the requester, reviewer, approver, decision-maker, vendor, and executive owner
  • Apply a practical “verify, document, escalate” process before AI-assisted work reaches a high-stakes audience
  • Recommend whether an AI use should proceed with safeguards, be limited, paused, or escalated
Case note: This module begins with a real South African public-policy incident. Corporate workplace examples are labelled where they are fictional simulations.

The Real Case — April 2026

South Africa’s draft national AI policy was withdrawn because fictitious references were found in the document.

South African Government News Agency (SAnews) · April 2026

In April 2026, South Africa withdrew its draft national AI policy after fictitious academic references were found in the document. The Minister said the most plausible explanation was that AI-generated citations had been included without proper verification. The incident was not a technical glitch: it was a failure of review, source-checking, and accountability before a high-stakes document was published.

Sources: SAnews.gov.za · Reuters · Verified public reporting. The specific number of fabricated references and precise internal response details have not been independently confirmed in primary source material and are not cited here.

Why this matters in your organisation: Your organisation may never publish national policy. But board packs, client proposals, investor communications, tender responses, market reports, HR documents, and product claims can create the same risk when AI-generated material reaches a decision-maker without a named verifier and independently checked sources.

Concept — Why corporate AI fails, and what stops it
The framework behind the failure · ~20 min
Screen 1 of 4 — Why polished AI output can fail

Why polished AI output can fail

Generative AI can produce fluent, confident-sounding text whether a claim is accurate, incomplete, outdated, or fabricated. Confidence and style are not reliable evidence of truth.

Generation vs retrieval

Search tools retrieve existing sources. Generative AI predicts and composes text. Some enterprise tools combine retrieval with generation — but the user still needs to know which claims come from verifiable sources and which are generated synthesis. The DCDT case failed at this distinction: drafters treated generated citations as if they had been retrieved from real sources.

In World Wide Worx’s 2025 survey of more than 100 mid-sized and large South African organisations, 67% reported current GenAI use. Only 15% reported using officially approved tools only, while 32% reported unofficial use without formal approval and 20% reported both approved and unofficial use. The report identifies a gap between rapid adoption and formal strategy, governance, and responsible-use structures. Source: World Wide Worx, South African Generative AI Roadmap 2025.

Which claim can you open and independently verify?

“The SA commercial property market reached R1.8 trillion in 2024”  |  “According to World Wide Worx (2025), 32% of SA companies used GenAI without formal approval”  |  “Studies show AI tools increase productivity by 40%”

Only the World Wide Worx figure can be independently verified by opening the named report. The property market figure and the productivity study each need a named original source before appearing in a board document. AI can compose plausible numbers with apparent precision — that precision is not evidence of accuracy.

Tool types and data boundaries

Microsoft Copilot
Emails, meeting summaries, report drafts, presentations
Potentially safer — still requires review

Depending on configuration, permissions, and connected data sources, an enterprise assistant may operate under stronger organisational controls. It can still omit context, misattribute statements, summarise inaccurately, or expose content to the wrong audience if underlying permissions are wrong.

ChatGPT (free tier)
Research, drafts, proposals, policy documents
High risk for confidential or personal information

Entering client, employee, financial, or other sensitive information into a consumer AI service may create POPIA, confidentiality, contractual, security, and cross-border data-transfer risks. The organisation should not assume a free/public service is approved for work data.

Unapproved / shadow tool
Used when approved tools feel slow or expensive
High risk — blind spots on data retention and control

Unapproved use is high risk because the organisation may not know where data is processed, retained, accessed, or reused. Public reporting in 2025 identified an exposed DeepSeek database containing chat histories. The wider lesson applies to any unapproved tool: blind spots on retention, access, incident response, and auditability.

An approved AI tool does not make every use of it approved. A system may be approved for low-risk drafting, meeting summaries, or internal search — but not for personal information, client contracts, legal advice, board materials, employment decisions, consumer-facing claims, or regulated data.

Four South African legal and governance frameworks that may be relevant when organisations use AI

POPIA — Protection of Personal Information Act

When an AI tool processes personal information, the organisation must consider lawful processing, purpose, security safeguards, operator/vendor controls, retention, access, and cross-border transfers. POPIA section 72 restricts transfers outside South Africa unless a permitted condition applies — for example, adequate protection through law or binding safeguards, consent, or a necessary contractual basis.

Entering employee, client, customer, or other personal information into an unapproved AI tool may create a significant compliance and confidentiality risk. It should be assessed, not assumed safe.

Consumer Protection Act, section 41

Where a business uses AI-generated content in consumer-facing marketing, product information, pricing, or chat support, it remains responsible for avoiding false, misleading, or deceptive representations. A fluent AI response is not a defence if the company publishes or relies on it. This applies to consumer-facing contexts — not every internal board document.

Employment Equity Act

AI-assisted recruitment, promotion, performance, or workforce tools can raise employment-equity concerns where they produce unequal outcomes linked to protected or historically disadvantaged groups. A neutral-looking criterion may create indirect-unfair-discrimination risk even without discriminatory intent. Employers should investigate outcome patterns, job relevance, proxy variables, and available safeguards rather than assume automated scoring is neutral.

For designated employers, employment-equity planning, reporting, and sectoral targets increase the importance of monitoring how recruitment and workforce systems affect access, advancement, and representation.

B-BBEE and AI-supported procurement

Where AI is used to rank suppliers, recommend vendors, analyse procurement spend, or optimise sourcing, organisations should ensure the tool does not ignore or undermine applicable B-BBEE procurement, supplier-development, and transformation commitments.

A cost- or speed-only optimisation model may miss relevant procurement criteria. The organisation remains responsible for defining the criteria, checking the recommendation, and ensuring AI-assisted procurement supports — not silently defeats — its approved transformation objectives.

What controlled corporate AI deployment looks like: Nedbank’s Enbi

Enbi — governance features that support safer deployment: Nedbank describes Enbi as an AI-enabled digital assistant embedded in its banking channels. Nedbank says it has handled 22 million service requests from more than 1.8 million clients and can direct customers to human colleagues for specialised help.

The case illustrates three governance features often associated with safer deployment: (1) clearly limited scope — defined to Nedbank products and services; (2) escalation to a human with sufficient context — when Enbi cannot help, a live agent receives the conversation; (3) domain-specific configuration and permissions — not a generic model in a new environment. These are illustrative governance features, not proven exclusive causes of success. Contrast them with the DCDT case: AI used outside its reliable scope, with no independent verification of output.

Before approving or renewing a corporate AI tool, ask the vendor:

  • What precise task is this tool approved for?
  • What data does it receive, infer, store, retain, or reuse?
  • Where is data processed, and under which legal basis?
  • Is the vendor an operator or service provider under applicable contracts?
  • Can the organisation audit, delete, export, or investigate data?
  • What permissions or connectors are enabled?
  • Who can override or correct output?
  • What happens when a user reports an error or harm?
  • Is the vendor’s use case validated and within agreed scope?

Spot the governance failure

A market analyst sends a client-facing report drafted in an unapproved AI tool. The data includes a client’s portfolio figures. The manager approves it in two minutes without reading it. No source is cited.

Which governance failures are present?
  • Data boundary: Client portfolio figures entered into an unapproved tool
  • Source verification: No cited source for claims in a client-facing document
  • Review ownership: Manager approved without reading — approval is nominal, not substantive
  • Approval scope: The unapproved tool is outside any sanctioned environment
  • Escalation: No escalation to legal, compliance, or IT about the tool or data
  • Monitoring: No process to check whether this pattern is repeated

Most corporate AI failures are not one error. They are several missing controls lining up at once.

Apply — The board presentation scenario
Run the Funda Five™ on a real situation · ~15 min

A business analyst at a mid-size SA company uses Copilot to prepare a market analysis for a board presentation. The AI includes three statistics with apparent source attributions. The analyst does not verify them. The board approves a R2.3 million budget based on the presentation. One statistic — the market size figure — is later found to be fabricated.

1
TASK — What was AI being asked to do, and which part required independent evidence?
Was Copilot being used to structure a presentation, summarise verified research, generate possible research questions, or supply evidence for a board decision? Which part was suitable for AI assistance, and where did the analyst need to stop and verify independently?
2
DATA — What information did the analyst rely on, and what should have been checked?
Which claims, figures, source links, dates, definitions, assumptions, and methods were used in the presentation? Was any confidential, personal, commercially sensitive, or market-sensitive information included? If the same work had been done in an unapproved consumer tool, what additional data-flow questions would arise?
3
TOOL — Was Copilot configured and used appropriately for this evidence task?
Did the tool retrieve from approved, traceable sources, or generate a plausible synthesis? What tool or source should have been used for authoritative market evidence?
4
TRUST — How would you verify a board-level statistic before relying on it?
What would you check in the original source: publisher, date, jurisdiction, market definition, method, sample, and whether the cited source actually supports the precise claim? The higher the consequence, the higher the verification bar.

Model answers — how a strong response looks

TASK — What was AI being asked to do?

AI is appropriate for structuring a board presentation, writing transitions, and summarising supplied research. It should not be treated as the final source of market-size evidence. The analyst needed independently verifiable data before presenting a number that would influence a budget decision. The clear stop point: any claim that will be used as evidence for a material decision must come from a named, openable source — not from AI synthesis.

DATA — What information did the analyst rely on?

The key claims and figures needed independent source verification before entering the board pack. An approved enterprise tool may provide stronger controls than a consumer AI service, but the analyst would still need to know the tool’s configuration, data sources, and retention settings. If confidential or commercially sensitive information was included, an unapproved consumer tool would create additional POPIA, confidentiality, contractual, and cross-border-transfer risks that must be assessed before use.

TOOL — Was Copilot the right choice for this specific task?

Copilot is appropriate for drafting and structuring. It is not a substitute for a named market-research source. For a board-level market size figure, the right tool is the original industry report, government data, a recognised database, or commissioned research — then cite that source directly. The enduring habit: what source did this claim actually come from, and can I open, inspect, and verify it?

TRUST — How to verify a board-level statistic

Open the cited source, not just the link title. Confirm it exists and is authoritative enough for the decision. Check that the number appears in the source. Check the date, geography, definition, methodology, and whether the figure was copied from another source out of context. Use a second independent source where the figure is material. Record the source in the board pack for traceability. A board can delegate research — it cannot delegate responsibility for whether the evidence is real.

HUMAN — Who needed to act, and what must happen now

The analyst should not have presented unverified AI-generated claims as evidence. The manager or board-paper approver should have verified that material figures were independently sourced. The subject-matter owner should have validated market assumptions. Finance/risk should have challenged the figure supporting the R2.3 million decision. “The AI wrote it” is not a defence. Now: stop relying on the affected budget assumption; preserve the deck, prompt/output, and sources; verify all material statistics; notify the relevant committee with a corrected analysis; determine whether external communications or decisions relied on the figure; strengthen review controls.

Incident response — what must happen now

Immediate steps: stop implementation or pause reliance on the affected statistic; preserve all records (presentation versions, prompts, output, source attempts, approval chain); verify all other material statistics in the pack, not just the one known false claim; notify the board committee promptly with a corrected analysis; determine whether external parties, contracted parties, or regulators received materials relying on the fabricated figure; assess whether this is a one-off error or a wider workflow control gap; improve verification requirements for future board materials and AI-assisted work.
