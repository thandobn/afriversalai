The Real Case — April 2026

South Africa's national AI policy had to be withdrawn because it cited sources that didn't exist.

News24 · 26 April 2026 · Verified reporting

"SA government withdraws national AI policy after hallucinated academic citations discovered"

The Department of Communications and Digital Technologies (DCDT) published South Africa's Draft National AI Policy on 10 April 2026. News24 subsequently found that at least 6 of the policy's 67 academic citations were fabricated — articles attributed to real journals and real authors who had never written on the topics cited.

The department admitted that drafters had used a generative AI tool and published without verifying a single reference. Minister Malatsi told Parliament the department only discovered the fabrications after the media reported them. The policy was withdrawn on 26 April 2026. Two officials were suspended. The entire consultation process had to restart.

Sources: The Register · The Next Web · CNBC Africa · Times Live

Why this matters for your organisation: The professionals who drafted that policy knew how to use AI tools. They didn't know when to verify them. That gap exists in every organisation that uses GenAI to produce documents, reports, or client-facing content — including yours.

Section 1 of 4 — The pattern behind the DCDT case

The pattern behind the DCDT case

Generative AI produces confident-sounding output regardless of accuracy. The DCDT case is not unusual — it's the default risk when professionals treat AI as a retrieval tool (like a search engine) rather than a generation tool (which predicts plausible text).

The core distinction

A search engine finds existing pages. A generative AI model predicts what text would logically follow your prompt, based on patterns in its training data. It does not look things up — it constructs an answer. That answer will sound identical whether it is accurate or fabricated.

74% of SA corporate employees now use Microsoft Copilot. 67% use ChatGPT. 32% use AI tools not approved by their IT department. The professional risk isn't in using these tools — it's in not understanding what they can and cannot do.

DeepSeek (shadow AI)

Data breach history · unapproved

DeepSeek's database was breached in early 2025, exposing chat histories. Any work data entered is beyond your organisation's control.

Four SA regulations that affect corporate AI use

POPIA — Protection of Personal Information Act

Personal data cannot be processed outside SA without adequate protection agreements. Free AI tools process data offshore. Pasting employee or client PII into an unapproved tool is a reportable breach. The Information Regulator has moved from compliance guidance to active enforcement — issuing enforcement notices and administrative penalties for POPIA breaches, including offshore data transfer violations.

Consumer Protection Act (Section 41)

Prohibits false, misleading, or deceptive marketing. AI-generated product descriptions, pricing claims, or chatbot responses that contain hallucinated information are the company's liability — not the AI tool's. "The AI wrote it" is not a defence.

Employment Equity Act (amended 2022)

If AI-assisted shortlisting or performance tools produce outcomes that disproportionately disadvantage designated groups, that is unfair discrimination — regardless of intent. The employer is responsible for the tool's outcomes. The EEA's 2025 sectoral targets raise the stakes: discriminatory AI in hiring now directly affects your B-BBEE scorecard.

B-BBEE Codes

AI procurement tools optimising purely on cost or speed may systematically deprioritise qualifying B-BBEE suppliers. The 2024 B-BBEE Codes update increased weighting for black-owned SMME procurement. An AI recommendation engine unaware of these requirements will harm your scorecard.

Where AI in corporate SA actually works: Nedbank's Enbi

Enbi — what right looks like: Nedbank's AI chatbot handled over 10 million customer requests and reduced live-agent chat by 50%. Three factors separated it from deployments that fail: (1) narrow scope defined upfront — Nedbank products and services only, nothing else; (2) human escalation with full context — when Enbi can't help, a live agent receives the complete conversation history; (3) domain-specific training — SA vernacular ("Send-iMali"), Nedbank-specific financial context, not a generic model dropped into a new environment. Remove any one of those three and the outcome changes. Contrast that with the DCDT case: AI used outside its reliable scope, with nobody checking the output.

Model answers — how a strong response looks

TASK — Was this the right job for AI?

AI is reliable for structuring, formatting, and drafting connected prose — not for generating verified statistics. The analyst should have used Copilot to structure the narrative and write transitions, then sourced every market figure from a verified source (Stats SA, Statista, industry reports). AI drafted the skeleton; human judgment sourced the bones.

DATA — Was any of this sensitive?

Copilot (enterprise) kept data within the organisation's M365 environment, so data processing risk was relatively low here. However, if the analyst had used the free ChatGPT tier with client names and internal financial projections, that would constitute a reportable POPIA breach — personal and financial data processed offshore without adequate protection agreements.

TOOL — Was Copilot the right choice for this specific task?

Copilot is appropriate for drafting, summarising, and structuring. It is not a verified data source. For market statistics, the right tool is a verified database (Stats SA, Statista, PwC Africa reports). The mistake was treating a generation tool as a retrieval tool — Copilot completed "the market is worth..." by predicting a plausible number, not by retrieving a verified one.

TRUST — How would you have detected the error?

Searching for the cited source directly (DOI link, journal name + article title) would have revealed that it either doesn't exist or doesn't contain the stated figure. This takes under 5 minutes per statistic. For board-level financial decisions, any AI-generated figure should be independently verified before it enters a slide — one search per number is a reasonable standard.

HUMAN — Who is accountable when the board acts on a fabricated number?

The analyst is the primary professional responsible — they submitted a document containing unverified data. "The AI generated it" is not a defence under the Consumer Protection Act (Section 41), professional codes of conduct, or common law. Management also bears accountability for approving a R2.3 million decision without a verification requirement. The organisation needs a policy: any AI-generated statistic used in a decision document must be independently verified and sourced.
