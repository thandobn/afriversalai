AI in Recruitment

AI applicant tracking systems are screening South African job seekers before any human sees their CV. The SA Board for People Practices formally documented this in 2023. This module maps the accountability chain when AI recruitment produces unfair outcomes — and shows you which law applies, where to complain, and who bears responsibility.

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Map the accountability chain in an AI-assisted recruitment process from data collection to hiring decision
  • Identify which EEA provisions apply when AI recruitment tools produce indirect unfair discrimination in employment
  • Name at least two formal channels through which an affected candidate can raise an AI recruitment complaint

3
Concept
The accountability chain, the law, and the formal channels · ~20 min

How AI applicant tracking systems work in SA

An Applicant Tracking System (ATS) is a software platform that receives, stores, and processes job applications. Modern ATS platforms include AI scoring: they rank candidates against each other and against a model of a “successful hire” derived from the company’s historical data. In many SA deployments documented by SABPP, the system filters candidates before any human reads a single CV.

The model’s definition of “success” is learned from historical hiring data. If the company’s past hires skewed toward graduates of historically white universities (as they did in most formal SA employers before aggressive EEA compliance), the model learns that this pattern predicts success — and scores candidates from historically Black universities lower. The algorithm is doing exactly what it was designed to do. The discrimination is in what it was trained on.

The accountability chain — who is in it

When AI produces an unfair recruitment outcome, accountability is distributed across a chain. Each link has a distinct responsibility:

1
ATS Vendor — the builder
Responsible for the model architecture, training methodology, and auditing for bias before deployment. If the vendor knowingly deploys a system with foreseeable discriminatory impact without disclosing this, they bear significant liability. Vendors sometimes claim their system is “neutral” — but a model trained on historical SA hiring data is structurally biased toward historical hiring patterns.
2
HR Director — the deployer
Responsible for procurement decisions, configuration of the system, and monitoring outcomes. Under EEA, employers are required to actively implement employment equity. Deploying an AI screening tool without auditing it for EEA compliance is not a neutral act — it is an abdication of the employer’s affirmative equity obligations.
3
Hiring Manager — the decision-maker
Receives a shortlist from the ATS and interviews from it. If the hiring manager never knows the demographics of who was screened out, they cannot identify the problem. But they are still a node in the chain: if they raise no questions about the shortlist’s diversity, they bear some accountability for the outcome.
4
Board / CEO — the principal
The company is the juristic person ultimately accountable for EEA compliance. The Employment Equity Plan submitted to the Department of Employment and Labour is a board-level commitment. Systemic AI discrimination that undermines the EEA plan is a compliance failure that flows to the top of the organisation.
The monitoring obligation: The accountability chain doesn’t end at deployment. Under EEA Sections 19–27, designated employers submit Employment Equity Plans with measurable equity targets to the Department of Employment and Labour. An AI tool that systematically undermines those targets is a compliance failure — and the EEA creates an affirmative duty to monitor outcomes, not just report inputs. Meridian’s 18-month gap between deployment and complaint is the failure mode the monitoring obligation is designed to prevent.

The legal framework — EEA in the employment context

This is where EEA applies — employment decisions specifically. Do not confuse with Module 3 (PEPUDA for non-employment AI discrimination). The statutes are different because the contexts are different:

EEA Section 6
Employment Equity Act 55 of 1998 — Section 6: Prohibition of Unfair Discrimination
Prohibits unfair discrimination in employment decisions on any listed ground — including race, gender, disability, and “arbitrary grounds.” Critically, Section 6 covers indirect unfair discrimination: a criterion that appears neutral but produces discriminatory outcomes on a listed ground. University attended, as a proxy for race in SA, is indirect discrimination under Section 6. The employer bears the burden of proving the discrimination was fair or not discrimination.
EEA Sections 19–27
Employment Equity Plan — Affirmative Obligations
Designated employers (50+ employees, or certain thresholds) must submit an Employment Equity Plan to the Department of Employment and Labour. The plan commits the company to measurable equity targets. An AI system that systematically screens out candidates from designated groups undermines the EEA plan and creates a direct compliance failure that can trigger fines and public reporting.
POPIA Section 71
Protection of Personal Information Act — Section 71 (Automated Decision Right)
Also applies in recruitment: the ATS is processing personal information (CV data) to make or significantly influence a consequential employment decision. Lerato has the right to request human review of the decision before it is finalised. She also has the right to know what information was used and to make representations. Section 71 also entitles Lerato to a reasoned explanation of the specific factors used to reach the outcome — not just what data was processed, but why her application was screened out. “Our system reviewed your application” is not sufficient disclosure of automated processing under POPIA.
Why EEA here and PEPUDA in Module 3: EEA applies to employment decisions specifically. PEPUDA applies to all public and private conduct. When AI discriminates in a policing or bail context, PEPUDA is the hook. When AI discriminates in a recruitment or employment context, EEA Section 6 is the primary hook — with POPIA Section 71 for the automated decision right. Both apply in recruitment; PEPUDA may also apply but EEA is the lex specialis (the more specific statute) for employment.

Formal channels for raising AI recruitment complaints

CCMA
Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration
The primary forum for unfair labour practices and unfair discrimination in employment. Candidates who believe they were unfairly discriminated against in a recruitment process can refer to the CCMA. The CCMA can order reinstatement, compensation, or require the employer to revise its processes. Fast and accessible — no legal representation required.
South African Human Rights Commission (SAHRC)
Constitutional body investigating human rights violations
Can investigate systemic discrimination patterns — including AI recruitment systems that produce widespread discriminatory outcomes across multiple candidates. The SAHRC is particularly relevant when the discrimination is systemic (affecting a class of candidates) rather than a single incident. They can investigate and make binding recommendations.
Labour Court
Specialist court for employment disputes
For complex discrimination cases, including interdicts against employer conduct and declaratory relief. More formal than the CCMA; legal representation typical. Can award substantial damages in serious indirect discrimination cases and can order mandatory equity audits of AI systems.
Information Regulator (POPIA)
South Africa’s data protection authority
If Lerato is denied information about how her personal data was processed (what the system used, how it scored her), she can complain to the Information Regulator under POPIA. The Regulator can compel disclosure and investigate whether the employer complied with Section 71’s automated decision requirements.

Model Answers — Meridian ATS Discrimination Complaint (AI Recruitment)

1. TASK — What is the ATS being used for? Is this correct?

The ATS is being used to make the first-stage screening decision in recruitment — determining which candidates a human ever sees. This is consequential: Lerato’s CV was rejected before a single human read it. The absence of human review before shortlisting is the core problem: it removes the human oversight required to catch algorithmic bias and fulfil EEA affirmative equity obligations. Correct use of AI in recruitment: AI may assist in ranking and sorting, but a human reviewer must see the full applicant pool (or a meaningful stratified sample) before the shortlist is finalised; the system must be audited for equity outcomes; and candidates must be informed that automated processing is used and given a right to contest under POPIA Section 71.

2. DATA — What proxies might encode race?

A typical ATS uses: name, university attended, location/address, employment history (companies worked at, tenure), years of experience, professional certifications, skills keywords. In SA, several of these are proxies for race: university attended (historically Black universities vs historically white universities — a direct legacy of apartheid education policy); location/address (apartheid geography means many Black professionals still list addresses in townships or historically Black areas); name (SA names can signal race, ethnicity, and language group, all of which correlate with race). All of this is personal information under POPIA. The automated use of it to make consequential employment decisions triggers Section 71 rights. Processing special categories of information (which can be inferred from these proxies) requires explicit consent or specific legal grounds under POPIA.

3. TOOL — Why is training on historical SA hiring data structurally biased?

The ATS uses a ranking or classification model trained on Meridian’s historical hiring and success data. The model learns: what does a successful Meridian hire look like? The answer in historical data: predominantly graduates of Wits, UCT, and Stellenbosch, with particular employment histories, names, and address areas. This is not a bug. The model is correctly learning from the data. The problem is that the data encodes 30+ years of apartheid-constrained recruitment — Black professionals were excluded from formal sector employment and from historically white universities until the 1990s. The model can’t know this. It just sees the patterns: these attributes correlate with “success.” This is the same structural mechanism as Module 3’s Riskio case — accurate data encoding historical discrimination, replicated at scale by a model.

4. TRUST — How do you audit this system?

A meaningful equity audit requires: (1) Raw score data for all applications over 18 months, with demographic breakdowns — you need to compare AI advance rates by race, gender, and disability across the full applicant pool; (2) Feature weights from the vendor — what factors does the model score highest? Are university attended, location, or name-proxied features weighted heavily? (3) A comparison of AI advance rates vs final hire demographics — if the ATS is screening out candidates from historically Black universities at double the rate of candidates from historically white universities, that is EEA Section 6 evidence; (4) Vendor documentation: has the system been independently audited for equity? If the vendor refuses to provide feature weights or audit documentation, the system is not auditable and must not be used for employment decisions in SA. The 18-month outcome data is itself strong prima facie evidence: a statistically significant difference in advance rates across racial groups shifts the burden to the employer to prove the discrimination is not unfair.

5. HUMAN — Accountability chain and immediate obligations

ATS vendor: accountable for deploying a system with foreseeable discriminatory impact in the SA context without adequate disclosure or equity auditing. Previous HR Director (and the company): accountable for procuring and deploying the system without EEA equity impact assessment, and for 18 months of unchecked operation. Hiring managers: accountable for not questioning the demographic homogeneity of successive shortlists. Board/CEO: accountable for EEA plan compliance failures — this is a notifiable compliance event. Meridian must immediately: (1) Suspend AI-only screening pending audit; (2) Commission an independent equity audit of 18 months of ATS outcomes; (3) Notify the Department of Employment and Labour of the compliance review; (4) Respond to Lerato’s CCMA referral: disclose what data was used in her application, provide the POPIA Section 71 information she is entitled to, and consider whether to settle; (5) Notify other candidates who were screened out via ATS-only in the same period. What to tell Lerato: she has the right to the specific information used in her automated scoring (POPIA Section 71), and her CCMA referral will be engaged transparently.
