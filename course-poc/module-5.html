Core Course / Module 5

AI in Recruitment

AI-assisted recruitment tools can parse CVs, rank candidates, recommend matches, schedule interviews, and support early screening before or alongside human review. These systems can save time — but they can also reproduce unfair patterns, obscure why someone was excluded, and make accountability harder to trace. This module maps the recruitment accountability chain: from data collection and screening criteria to shortlist, interview, and hiring decision. It shows where Employment Equity Act, assessment-validity, privacy, and governance concerns may arise — and what an affected candidate or responsible employer can do next.

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Map the accountability chain in an AI-assisted recruitment process from data collection to hiring decision
  • Recognise when an AI recruitment tool may raise indirect-unfair-discrimination concerns under the Employment Equity Act, and know what evidence and specialist review may be needed
  • Distinguish administrative automation from AI-assisted assessment or scoring, and identify questions about validity, reliability, fairness, and bias
  • Identify internal and external routes for raising a recruitment concern, including employer processes and, where appropriate, the CCMA, Labour Court, Department of Employment and Labour, SAHRC, or Information Regulator

Important: A lower interview rate or a rejected application is a reason to ask questions — not automatic proof that a recruitment system has broken the law.

Concept
The accountability chain, the law, and the formal channels · ~20 min
Screen 1 of 4 — What recruitment systems actually do

How AI applicant tracking systems work in SA

An Applicant Tracking System (ATS) is a software platform that receives, stores, and processes job applications. Modern ATS platforms include AI scoring: they rank candidates against each other and against a model of a “successful hire” derived from the company’s historical data. The key question is not whether a tool is called “AI.” It is what role it plays in the decision: administration, recommendation, ranking, assessment, or automatic exclusion.

If a system is trained on historical hiring, promotion, or performance data, it may learn patterns associated with people the organisation previously hired or labelled as successful. Those patterns may reflect job-relevant experience — but they may also reflect historical recruitment networks, unequal opportunity, managerial preferences, inconsistent performance ratings, or biased definitions of “success.” The question is not whether history is automatically discriminatory. It is whether the organisation has tested whether historical patterns are relevant, reliable, fair, and appropriate for the role being filled.

Which use needs the highest scrutiny?

Tap to reveal:

Scheduling interviews / Parsing CV formatting / Ranking candidates by keyword match / Automatically rejecting based on predicted future performance / Analysing face, voice, or emotion in a video interview

The more a tool assesses people, excludes them automatically, or affects access to work, the more employers need to test validity, fairness, accessibility, human review, and candidate recourse. Automated rejection based on predicted future performance and emotion/voice/face analysis in video interviews require the highest level of scrutiny — and may engage EEA section 8 assessment-validity requirements.

The accountability chain — who is in it

When AI produces an unfair recruitment outcome, accountability is distributed across a chain. Each link has a distinct responsibility:

1
ATS Vendor — the builder
The vendor should be able to explain the tool’s intended use, data requirements, validation evidence, known limitations, human-review design, and monitoring recommendations. Contractual and product responsibility may sit with the vendor, but buying a vendor system does not remove the employer’s employment-equity obligations.
2
HR Director — the deployer
HR and recruitment leaders influence procurement, screening criteria, configuration, candidate communication, review processes, and monitoring. Before using a high-impact screening or assessment tool, the employer should assess risks, document purpose and criteria, test outcomes where appropriate, and establish escalation and review processes.
3
Hiring Manager — the decision-maker
The hiring manager should understand what the shortlist represents, question unexplained exclusions, and avoid treating an AI ranking as a final decision. Meaningful involvement includes reviewing role requirements, challenging obvious anomalies, and escalating concerns — not merely accepting a shortlist.
4
Board / CEO — the principal
Senior leadership is responsible for governance, risk appetite, resourcing, oversight, and ensuring that appropriate people are assigned to implement employment-equity obligations. For designated employers, senior managers must be assigned responsibility for implementing and monitoring the Employment Equity Plan.
The monitoring obligation: The accountability chain doesn’t end at deployment. For designated employers — generally employers with 50 or more employees, as well as organs of state — the Employment Equity Act requires Employment Equity Plans, reporting, and implementation aligned with applicable sectoral targets. An AI tool that systematically undermines those targets is a compliance concern. A long gap between tool deployment and a complaint is a practical warning sign: organisations should not wait for a candidate complaint before checking whether a high-impact recruitment process is producing concerning patterns.

The legal framework — EEA in the employment context

This is where EEA applies — employment decisions specifically. Do not confuse with Module 3 (PEPUDA for non-employment AI discrimination). The statutes are different because the contexts are different:

EEA Section 6
Employment Equity Act 55 of 1998 — Section 6: Prohibition of Unfair Discrimination
Section 6 prohibits unfair direct or indirect discrimination in employment policies and practices, including recruitment and selection. A neutral-looking criterion may raise concern if it produces unequal effects linked to a listed ground. Whether it is unlawful requires evidence and a contextual fairness assessment. University attended can act as a proxy for unequal access and historically shaped opportunity — if a recruitment rule relying on it produces unequal effects linked to a listed ground, it may raise indirect-unfair-discrimination concerns and should be investigated. Under section 11, where unfair discrimination is alleged on a listed ground, the employer bears an important burden to show that the discrimination did not occur as alleged or was rational, not unfair, or otherwise justifiable.
EEA Section 8
Employment Equity Act 55 of 1998 — Section 8: Psychological Testing and Similar Assessments
Some AI recruitment tools do more than organise applications. They assess personality, suitability, ability, communication, future performance, or “fit.” Where a tool functions like a psychological or similar assessment, employers should ask whether it is valid, reliable, fair, non-biased, and appropriately certified or governed. A tool should not be trusted simply because it produces a score. Examples where section 8 scrutiny may be relevant: video-interview analysis of face, voice, or emotion; personality or “culture fit” scoring; communication or tone assessment; future-performance prediction; leadership-potential or cognitive-ability scoring; honesty or reliability scoring.
EEA Sections 19–27
Employment Equity Plan — Affirmative Obligations
For designated employers — generally employers with 50 or more employees, as well as organs of state — the Employment Equity Act requires Employment Equity Plans with measurable equity targets submitted to the Department of Employment and Labour. An AI system that systematically screens out candidates from designated groups may undermine the EEA plan and create a compliance concern that can trigger fines and public reporting. Sectoral targets, plans, reporting, and compliance rules were updated in the 2025 EEA regulations.
Meaningful human review: A human reviewer is not meaningful if they only click “approve,” never see excluded candidates, cannot inspect the reasons for a score, cannot correct errors, or cannot override the tool. Human oversight needs information, authority, time, and a genuine ability to change the decision.
Accessibility and disability: Fairness is not only about university, race, or gender. Recruitment AI can disadvantage candidates with non-standard CV formats, disability-related employment gaps, assistive-technology use, speech differences, video-interview accessibility needs, or different communication styles. A recruitment process should be tested for accessibility, disability accommodation, language, and non-standard career paths.
Language and multilingual candidates: Language models and keyword tools may favour dominant business-English patterns or familiar terminology. Evaluate whether language requirements are genuinely job-relevant and whether the system mistakes communication style for competence.
Data minimisation and retention: Collect and retain only what is necessary for a lawful recruitment purpose. Ask where applicant data travels, who can access it, how long it is stored, whether it is reused for training, and whether vendors can use it for their own model development.

Before buying or renewing a recruitment-AI tool, ask the vendor:

  • What does the tool do: organise, rank, assess, or exclude?
  • What data does it use and infer?
  • How was it validated for this role and population?
  • What evidence exists on reliability, fairness, and accessibility?
  • What known limitations or error patterns exist?
  • Can the employer audit, log, override, and appeal outcomes?
  • Does the vendor use applicant data for model training?
  • What happens if the tool produces a concerning disparity?
POPIA Section 71
Protection of Personal Information Act — Section 71 (Automated Decision Right)
Section 71 may be relevant where an applicant is subject to a decision that has legal consequences or substantially affects them, is based solely on automated processing of personal information, and is intended to profile them — for example, in relation to work performance, reliability, preferences, or conduct. A recruitment tool that only assists a recruiter may not trigger section 71 in the same way as a system that automatically rejects an applicant without meaningful human involvement. Where section 71 applies, the responsible party may need measures that allow the applicant to make representations and receive sufficient information about the underlying logic of the automated processing. A generic automated email may be inadequate for a high-impact solely automated decision, but whether section 71 applies depends on the actual decision process and statutory conditions. The key question is: Was there meaningful human involvement before the exclusion decision took effect?
EEA, POPIA, and other frameworks in recruitment: In recruitment and employment, the Employment Equity Act is usually the most directly relevant employment-discrimination framework. POPIA may also matter where personal information or automated decision-making is involved. Other equality, constitutional, contractual, and sector-specific rules may be relevant depending on the facts.

Formal channels for raising AI recruitment complaints

CCMA
Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration
Applicants and employees may refer certain unfair-discrimination disputes to the CCMA. The CCMA generally begins with conciliation; depending on the claim, earnings threshold, consent, and statutory route, the dispute may be arbitrated there or proceed to the Labour Court. The process has time limits, so early advice is important.
South African Human Rights Commission (SAHRC)
Constitutional body investigating human rights violations
The SAHRC can receive and investigate equality and human-rights complaints, including systemic concerns, and can assist affected people in seeking redress. It may be especially relevant where a concern appears to affect a broader class of applicants rather than only one individual.
Labour Court
Specialist court for employment disputes
The Labour Court hears employment-equity and discrimination matters that fall within its jurisdiction and can grant appropriate relief. It is generally the more formal route, so legal advice is often useful.
Information Regulator (POPIA)
South Africa’s data protection authority
If Lerato is denied information about how her personal data was processed (what the system used, how it scored her), she can complain to the Information Regulator under POPIA. The Regulator can compel disclosure and investigate whether the employer complied with Section 71’s automated decision requirements.

Which response is most responsible?

A system shows a meaningful disparity in who advances. Which next step is most responsible?

Delete the race field and declare the issue fixed / Ask the vendor to confirm the model is fair / Measure the disparity, inspect data/labels/proxies, introduce interim safeguards, seek specialist review, and remediate before continuing unchanged / Ignore the issue because average accuracy is high

Measure, investigate, safeguard, escalate, and remediate. Deleting a field does not remove proxy patterns already embedded in the model. A vendor confirmation is not an independent audit. Ignoring disparity while average accuracy is high is the most common and most dangerous response. The appropriate route is: measure the disparity, inspect data and proxies, introduce interim human-review safeguards, seek specialist HR/legal/data-protection support, and remediate before deploying unchanged.

Your role: You are the new HR Director at Meridian Financial Services. Your first week. You learn that the company has been using an AI ATS for 18 months. No human reviews CVs before the shortlist is generated. A candidate (Lerato Dube) has formally complained to the CCMA that she was unfairly screened out. Internal data shows that candidates from historically Black universities are advancing at half the rate of candidates from historically white universities. Apply the Funda Five™ — your task is to understand this situation fully, map the accountability, and determine what Meridian needs to do.
1
TASK — What is the AI being used for? Is this the right use, and is it being used correctly?
What decision is this system making or materially influencing? Is automatic exclusion appropriate for this task? What should happen immediately while the complaint is investigated? Should Meridian continue unchanged, limit use, require human review, suspend automatic rejection, or pause the tool entirely?
2
DATA — What data is the ATS using? What proxies might be encoding race?
What applicant data, metadata, and proxy variables might influence the result? Consider: university/school attended, postcode/location, home language, employment gaps, prior salary, name, referral source, work-history patterns, device or application-source data. Which may reflect unequal access, historical opportunity, or protected characteristics? What information must be preserved for audit? Do not assume removing a direct race field removes the need for fairness or privacy review.
3
TOOL — What type of AI is this? What should Meridian ask about validity and fairness?
Is this tool only administering applications, or is it assessing suitability, personality, future performance, or “fit”? Why might training on historical hiring data create fairness risk in the South African context? What assumptions, labels, historical patterns, or proxy variables could be carried into the model? What evidence of validity, reliability, fairness, and job relevance should Meridian require?
4
TRUST — How do you audit this system? What data do you need?
What audit steps and evidence are needed? A meaningful recruitment-AI audit should examine: purpose and job relevance of each criterion; training/validation data and the definition of “success”; model inputs, weights, thresholds, rules, and vendor documentation; candidate flow (applied → screened → shortlisted → interviewed → hired); outcomes across relevant groups; whether candidates can correct inaccurate data; whether human review is meaningful; logs, overrides, complaints, and changes over 18 months. Group-outcome testing must be lawful and privacy-aware.
5
HUMAN — Map the accountability chain and state what Meridian must do now
Who must act now? Who owns recruitment governance, data protection, vendor management, candidate communication, and executive oversight? What immediate actions should Meridian take: preserve evidence, stop or limit automatic rejection, put meaningful human review in place, notify HR/legal/compliance/IT/executive sponsor. What should Meridian say to Lerato — and what remedy or control should it consider? Do not prejudge the complaint.
+
CANDIDATE COMMUNICATION — What should Meridian tell Lerato now?
Acknowledge the complaint; confirm review is underway; preserve her records; avoid unsupported claims about the tool; explain internal process and available external avenues; do not retaliate or quietly alter records; do not promise an outcome before investigation.

Recruitment AI Incident Response Snapshot

Model Answers — Meridian ATS Discrimination Complaint (AI Recruitment)

1. TASK — What is the ATS being used for? Is this correct?

The ATS is being used to make the first-stage screening decision in recruitment — determining which candidates a human ever sees. This is consequential: Lerato’s CV was rejected before a single human read it. The absence of human review before shortlisting is the core problem: it removes the human oversight required to catch algorithmic bias and fulfil EEA affirmative equity obligations. Correct use of AI in recruitment: AI may assist in ranking and sorting, but a human reviewer must see the full applicant pool (or a meaningful stratified sample) before the shortlist is finalised; the system must be audited for equity outcomes; and candidates must be informed that automated processing is used and given a right to contest under POPIA Section 71.

2. DATA — What proxies might encode race?

A typical ATS uses: name, university attended, location/address, employment history (companies worked at, tenure), years of experience, professional certifications, skills keywords. In SA, several of these are proxies for race: university attended (historically Black universities vs historically white universities — a direct legacy of apartheid education policy); location/address (apartheid geography means many Black professionals still list addresses in townships or historically Black areas); name (SA names can signal race, ethnicity, and language group, all of which correlate with race). All of this is personal information under POPIA. The automated use of it to make consequential employment decisions triggers Section 71 rights. Processing special categories of information (which can be inferred from these proxies) requires explicit consent or specific legal grounds under POPIA.

3. TOOL — Why is training on historical SA hiring data structurally biased?

The ATS uses a ranking or classification model trained on Meridian’s historical hiring and success data. The model learns: what does a successful Meridian hire look like? The answer in historical data: predominantly graduates of Wits, UCT, and Stellenbosch, with particular employment histories, names, and address areas. This is not a bug. The model is correctly learning from the data. The problem is that the data encodes 30+ years of apartheid-constrained recruitment — Black professionals were excluded from formal sector employment and from historically white universities until the 1990s. The model can’t know this. It just sees the patterns: these attributes correlate with “success.” This is the same structural mechanism as Module 3’s Riskio case — accurate data encoding historical discrimination, replicated at scale by a model.

4. TRUST — How do you audit this system?

A meaningful equity audit requires: (1) Raw score data for all applications over 18 months, with demographic breakdowns — you need to compare AI advance rates by race, gender, and disability across the full applicant pool; (2) Feature weights from the vendor — what factors does the model score highest? Are university attended, location, or name-proxied features weighted heavily? (3) A comparison of AI advance rates vs final hire demographics — if the ATS is screening out candidates from historically Black universities at double the rate of candidates from historically white universities, that is EEA Section 6 evidence; (4) Vendor documentation: has the system been independently audited for equity? If the vendor refuses to provide feature weights or audit documentation, the system is not auditable and must not be used for employment decisions in SA. The 18-month outcome data is itself strong prima facie evidence: a statistically significant difference in advance rates across racial groups shifts the burden to the employer to prove the discrimination is not unfair.

5. HUMAN — Accountability chain and immediate obligations

ATS vendor: accountable for deploying a system with foreseeable discriminatory impact in the SA context without adequate disclosure or equity auditing. Previous HR Director (and the company): accountable for procuring and deploying the system without EEA equity impact assessment, and for 18 months of unchecked operation. Hiring managers: accountable for not questioning the demographic homogeneity of successive shortlists. Board/CEO: accountable for EEA plan compliance failures — this is a notifiable compliance event. Meridian must immediately: (1) Suspend AI-only screening pending audit; (2) Commission an independent equity audit of 18 months of ATS outcomes; (3) Notify the Department of Employment and Labour of the compliance review; (4) Respond to Lerato’s CCMA referral: disclose what data was used in her application, provide the POPIA Section 71 information she is entitled to, and consider whether to settle; (5) Notify other candidates who were screened out via ATS-only in the same period. What to tell Lerato: she has the right to the specific information used in her automated scoring (POPIA Section 71), and her CCMA referral will be engaged transparently.
