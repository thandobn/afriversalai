Bias & Discrimination

Discrimination can happen without discriminatory intent. In South Africa, AI trained on historical data carries the weight of apartheid and structural inequality forward — often invisibly. This module shows you how bias enters, how South African law sees it, and how to audit any tool for it.

By the end of this module, you will be able to:
  1. Define algorithmic bias in plain language for a non-technical audience
  2. Tell disparate treatment from indirect discrimination (what US law calls “disparate impact”) — and spot proxy discrimination
  3. Identify indirect discrimination on a listed ground in an AI recommendation, and explain when PEPUDA and POPIA s71 apply
  4. Apply an equity lens to AI deployment decisions in your sector
  5. Name who is accountable under South African law when AI discriminates

Lesson 1 of 7 — What algorithmic bias really is

The big idea

Algorithmic bias is rarely a glitch, and rarely malice. It is what happens when a system learns from an unequal past and then applies those patterns at scale — fast, cheaply, and wearing a mask of objectivity.

In South Africa, “the past” carries the weight of apartheid spatial planning and structural inequality. This phase gives you the vocabulary — disparate impact and proxy discrimination — and the South African law that governs both.

Lesson 1

What algorithmic bias really is

Forget the word “bias” for a second. The thing we actually care about is systematically worse outcomes for a particular group — produced by an AI system, because of the data it learned from or how it was built.

Plain-language definition

Algorithmic bias is when an AI system gives consistently different — and unfair — results to people based on race, gender, age, disability, language, or where they live, even when no one intended it to.

Two things follow from that definition, and both matter:

  • It is about groups and patterns, not a single wrong answer. One mistake is an error; a pattern of mistakes that lands on the same group is bias.
  • Intent is not required. A system can discriminate while everyone who built it had good intentions. That is the part most people get wrong.
Lesson 2

Disparate treatment vs disparate impact

The law — and good judgement — recognises two very different ways a decision can be unfair. Telling them apart is the single most useful skill in this module.

Disparate treatment

Treating someone differently because of a protected attribute.

Overt and usually obvious — e.g. “reject applicants from group X.” Rare in AI, because no one codes it openly.

Disparate impact

A neutral-looking rule that lands harder on a protected group.

Invisible and common — the rule never mentions the group, but the outcomes fall unequally. This is the AI problem.

Example

A lender’s AI never asks an applicant’s race. But it rejects applicants from certain townships far more often. No disparate treatment — clear disparate impact. The harm is identical; only the mechanism is hidden.

SA law note: What US legal doctrine calls “disparate impact,” South African law calls indirect discrimination — prohibited under PEPUDA (Act 4 of 2000) and the Constitution (s9) on listed grounds (race, gender, disability, age, language, and more). The test is the same: a neutral rule that produces unequal outcomes. But in SA, liability attaches regardless of intent.
Lesson 3

Proxy discrimination — the postal-code problem

“Just remove race and gender from the data and the AI can’t discriminate.” It sounds right. It is wrong — because of proxies.

!A proxy is an innocent-looking feature that stands in for a protected one

Postal code → race  ·  First language → ethnicity  ·  School attended → class  ·  Name → gender / ethnicity  ·  Phone & device → income

In South Africa, location is the most powerful proxy of all. Apartheid engineered where people live by race — so a model that uses suburb or postal code can discriminate by race almost perfectly, without ever seeing the word “race.”

Takeaway: Deleting the protected field does not make a model fair. The bias hides in the proxies that remain.
Lesson 4

Where bias enters the pipeline

Bias is not one thing in one place. It can enter at several points — you met some of these as “failure modes” in Module 2. Here they are through an equity lens:

1Historical bias

The past was unequal, so data drawn from it is unequal. The model learns “this is normal.”

2Sample / representation bias

Some groups are under-represented in the data (few rural, informal, or women-owned records), so the model’s patterns for them are thin and unreliable.

3Label bias

Someone had to decide what counted as a “good hire” or “high performer.” If those past human judgements were biased, the AI inherits that definition.

4Proxy features & feedback loops

Proxies (Lesson 3) smuggle protected attributes back in; feedback loops (Module 2) make a biased prediction come true and then cite itself as proof.

Lesson 5

The South African legal lens

This is where South Africa is different from a generic “AI ethics” course. Unfair discrimination — including the indirect, no-intent kind — is already unlawful here. AI does not get an exemption.

The instruments that already apply to AI decisions

  • Constitution, s9 (Equality): prohibits unfair discrimination, direct or indirect, on listed grounds (race, gender, age, disability, language, and more).
  • PEPUDA / Equality Act (Act 4 of 2000): outlaws unfair discrimination including indirect discrimination — a neutral rule with a discriminatory effect. Intent is not required to be liable.
  • Employment Equity Act: governs fair hiring, promotion and assessment — so AI CV-screening and scoring tools fall squarely under it.
  • POPIA, s71: gives people the right not to be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing that significantly affects them — and the right to ask for human intervention.
  • National Credit Act: governs fairness and transparency in credit decisions, including automated scoring.

The line that matters: in South African law, “we didn’t mean to” is not a defence. Indirect discrimination is unlawful even with no intent.

Lesson 6

The “objective machine” trap

Why is AI bias often more dangerous than human bias? Because of how much we trust a number that came from a computer.

⚠Automation bias

People defer to a machine’s output more than to a colleague’s opinion — even when the machine is wrong. A biased recommendation gets a free pass because “the system said so.”

Human bias is one person, sometimes. AI bias is scaled, consistent, instant — and wears a mask of neutrality.

“It’s just maths, it can’t be racist” is the trap. The maths is real; the data and the definitions behind it carry human history.

Lesson 7

Detecting it: the indirect discrimination test

You cannot fix what you cannot see. The practical way to check any tool is to compare outcomes across groups — the test you’ll run in Phase 4.

Four questions that surface bias

  1. Outcomes by group: are approval / flag / score rates very different across race, gender, or area?
  2. Proxies: does the model use location, school, language, name, or device?
  3. Who’s missing: which groups are thin or absent in the training data?
  4. Explainability: can you explain a single decision to the person it affected?
Rule of thumb: if you can’t measure outcomes by group, you can’t claim the system is fair — you’re only assuming it.
The key principle

Fairness is not the default.
It is a design decision — and a legal duty.

AI inherits the inequality in its data and hands it back wearing the mask of objectivity. Detecting and correcting that is a human responsibility — and in South Africa, a legal one.

Encounter — Predictive policing: who gets flagged?
A South African profiling scenario · ~20 min
Scenario · Public Sector
A “data-driven” predictive policing tool

A metro police department buys an AI system to decide where to send patrols. The vendor’s pitch:

“Objective, data-driven policing. The algorithm sends officers where crime will happen — no human bias.”

The model learns from the department’s records: past arrests & reported crime · location / suburb · time of day · prior call-outs. It never uses race.

Three months later

  • Patrols are concentrated in a handful of low-income, historically Black townships
  • More officers there → more stops → more arrests — which the model reads as “more crime”
  • Wealthier suburbs are barely patrolled, so little is recorded there
  • Residents say they are being watched because of where they live, not what they did

In the oversight meeting

“We removed race from the model. How can it possibly be profiling by race?”

— The project lead

SA in Focus · Two real patterns, one city

Place-based AI

Camera network surveillance

Mass CCTV networks concentrated in Sandton and Rosebank, with far lower coverage in Soweto and other townships. The AI monitors places — but apartheid spatial planning means the places it watches most closely are defined by race.

Person-based AI

Automated risk-scoring

Systems that assign individual risk scores using residential area, employment type, and prior police contact — even contacts that led to no charges. The score follows the person, and the same apartheid geography is now encoded in their file.

Both patterns constitute indirect discrimination under PEPUDA: a neutral-looking criterion (place / employment type) that produces unequal outcomes by race because of South Africa’s spatial legacy. Both trigger the right to human review under POPIA s71.

What this case teaches

“We removed race” misses the point

Three mechanisms from Phase 1 are running at once.

Proxy discrimination (suburb stands in for race), a feedback loop (patrols manufacture the arrests that justify more patrols), and missing data (unrecorded crime in wealthy areas). The result is textbook indirect discrimination under PEPUDA — what US doctrine calls “disparate impact” — profiling by race with the race field deleted.

Under the Constitution and PEPUDA this is indirect discrimination — unlawful regardless of intent. Accountability sits with the department and officials who deployed it, not the algorithm. “The system flagged it” is not a defence.

Reflect — What assumptions are built in?
Surfacing the values hidden in a model · ~20 min

Every model carries hidden assumptions — decisions about what to measure, what counts as “success,” and whose history to learn from. The policing tool assumed “where we arrested before = where crime is.” That one assumption did the damage. Now turn the lens on your own world.

Assumptions hide in ordinary choices…

  • Hiring: “good candidates look like the people we hired before” — which schools, which names, which areas?
  • Credit & insurance: “this area is high-risk” — risk of what, measured how, inherited from when?
  • Performance: “a high performer is someone like our current top performers” — defined by whom?

Each is a value judgement dressed as a data field.

One question to sit with: If you removed every protected attribute from your data tomorrow, would your model actually be fair — or just harder to interrogate?

Worked Equity Audit — Metro Police Predictive Patrol Deployment (Encounter scenario)

Worksheet completed

Tool audited: Metro police AI patrol routing system — an algorithmic tool that assigns patrol density by area based on historical crime and arrest data.

Decision it drives: Where to send police patrols; which areas receive heightened enforcement presence.

Listed grounds potentially affected: Race (via location proxy); socioeconomic status (via area classification).

Proxies in the data: Historical arrest and call-out records (biased by decades of over-policing in specific areas); suburb/location (apartheid spatial planning makes location a near-perfect race proxy in SA); prior police contact (reflects policing intensity, not underlying crime rates).

Disparate-impact check: Patrols concentrated in historically Black townships; comparable historically white suburbs receive minimal deployment. Outcome data confirms the disparity — but the data itself is biased by the deployment pattern. More patrols produce more recorded arrests, which the model reads as evidence of more crime, reinforcing the loop. Meaningful measurement requires comparing AI deployment data against independent crime data (victimisation surveys, not police records).

Who is harmed if wrong: Residents of over-policed areas — subject to disproportionate stops, searches, and arrest risk without evidence of higher actual crime rates relative to under-policed areas.

Legal exposure: PEPUDA — indirect discrimination on the ground of race through location proxy (non-employment context). POPIA s71 — where the system produces individual risk scores, data subjects have the right not to be subject to decisions based solely on automated processing.

Human oversight: Patrol commanders receive deployment recommendations and can override them. In practice, automation bias means the AI allocation is rarely contested. Oversight is present in form; absent in substance.

1 — Where could this tool produce indirect discrimination, and through which proxy?

Township and historically Black residential area residents are the affected group. The neutral-looking rule is: “deploy patrols where recorded crime density is highest.” The proxy is historical arrest density — which in SA reflects where police looked, shaped by decades of over-policing specific areas. This creates the feedback loop from the Encounter: more patrols → more recorded arrests → model reads as higher crime → more patrols. The discrimination is not in the algorithm. It is in what the algorithm was trained to optimise.

2 — How would you detect it?

Compare patrol hours per capita across two suburb groups: historically designated Black residential areas and historically designated white residential areas of comparable population size. A statistically significant disparity (>2:1 patrol density ratio is a useful starting threshold) constitutes prima facie indirect discrimination evidence under PEPUDA. A second check: compare recorded crime rates (police data) against independent victimisation survey data by area — if these diverge sharply, the recorded data is reflecting policing intensity rather than actual crime rates, confirming the bias mechanism.

3 — Who is accountable under SA law, and what is your one recommended control?

The departmental official who procured and approved the system bears primary accountability under PEPUDA — not the algorithm, and not individual officers following deployment recommendations. “The system recommended it” is not a defence for a public authority deploying AI that produces racially discriminatory outcomes on a listed ground. One control: institute a quarterly equity review comparing patrol deployment density with independent crime data and suburb demographic profiles, with a mandatory audit trigger if deployment ratios between comparable areas exceed a defined threshold. The current absence of any outcome monitoring is itself an accountability gap.
