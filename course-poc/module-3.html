Bias & Discrimination

Discrimination can happen without discriminatory intent. In South Africa, AI trained on historical information can reproduce patterns shaped by apartheid and ongoing structural inequality—often through variables that appear neutral on their face. This module shows you how bias enters, how South African law sees it, and how to audit any tool for it.

By the end of this module, you will be able to:
  1. Define algorithmic bias in plain language for a non-technical audience
  2. Distinguish direct discrimination from indirect unfair discrimination, and recognise proxy discrimination in AI-assisted decisions
  3. Recognise when an AI-assisted recommendation may raise equality, privacy, employment, credit, or automated-decision-making concerns — and know when to seek specialist advice
  4. Apply an equity lens to AI deployment decisions in your sector
  5. Identify people and organisations that may carry responsibility when an AI-assisted decision produces unfair outcomes

Unequal outcomes are a reason to investigate — not automatic proof that a system has broken the law.

Concept — Bias, disparate impact & SA law
The mental model before the case study · ~25 min
Lesson 1 of 7 — What algorithmic bias really is

The big idea

Algorithmic bias is rarely a glitch, and rarely malice. It is what happens when a system learns from an unequal past and then applies those patterns at scale — fast, cheaply, and wearing a mask of objectivity.

In South Africa, “the past” carries the weight of apartheid spatial planning and structural inequality. This phase gives you the vocabulary — indirect unfair discrimination and proxy discrimination — and the South African law that applies to both. (“Disparate impact” is a commonly used US term for a related idea.)

Lesson 1

What algorithmic bias really is

Forget the word “bias” for a second. The thing we actually care about is systematically worse outcomes for a particular group — produced by an AI system, because of the data it learned from or how it was built.

Plain-language definition

Algorithmic bias occurs when an AI system systematically produces worse, less accurate, or less favourable outcomes for some groups because of the data, assumptions, design choices, or deployment conditions behind it. These patterns can raise fairness and discrimination concerns — even when nobody intended them.

Two things follow from that definition, and both matter:

  • It is about groups and patterns, not a single wrong answer. One mistake is an error; a pattern of mistakes that lands on the same group is bias.
  • Intent is not required. A system can discriminate while everyone who built it had good intentions. That is the part most people get wrong.
Lesson 2

Disparate treatment vs disparate impact

The law — and good judgement — recognises two very different ways a decision can be unfair. Telling them apart is the single most useful skill in this module.

Disparate treatment

Treating someone differently because of a protected attribute.

Overt and usually obvious — e.g. “reject applicants from group X.” Rare in AI, because no one codes it openly.

Indirect unfair discrimination

A neutral-looking rule, policy, or system may produce unequal effects on people linked to a protected ground.

Invisible and common — the rule never mentions the group, but the outcomes fall unequally. This is a major risk in AI-assisted decisions because models can scale neutral-looking rules and historical patterns across many people quickly.

Example

A lender’s AI does not ask applicants’ race, but applicants from certain townships are rejected far more often. This may indicate an indirect-unfair-discrimination or proxy-discrimination risk. The next step is to investigate the inputs, outcomes, justification, alternatives, and safeguards.

SA law note: South African law calls this indirect unfair discrimination — prohibited under PEPUDA (Act 4 of 2000) and the Constitution (s9) on listed grounds (race, gender, disability, age, language, and more). Intent is not the only issue. A lack of discriminatory intent does not end the inquiry: a system can still produce unfair effects that require investigation, justification, and possible correction.
Lesson 3

Proxy discrimination — the postal-code problem

“Just remove race and gender from the data and the AI can’t discriminate.” It sounds right. It is wrong — because of proxies.

!A proxy is an innocent-looking feature that stands in for a protected one

Postal code may correlate with race and socio-economic inequality  ·  First language may correlate with ethnicity or cultural background  ·  School attended may reflect unequal access to opportunity  ·  Name may correlate with gender, ethnicity, or language  ·  Device type may correlate with income or digital access

In South Africa, residential location can be a particularly powerful proxy for race and socio-economic inequality because apartheid spatial planning continues to shape where people live, work, access services, and experience policing. A model using suburb or postal code may reproduce racialised and class-based inequalities without receiving race as an explicit input.

Takeaway: Deleting the protected field does not make a model fair. The bias can remain in proxies and patterns that still need scrutiny. In South African law, a lack of discriminatory intent does not automatically make a discriminatory outcome lawful — unequal effects may still require a contextual assessment.

Lesson 4

Where bias enters the pipeline

Bias is not one thing in one place. It can enter at several points — you met some of these as “failure modes” in Module 2. Here they are through an equity lens:

1Historical bias

The past was unequal, so data drawn from it is unequal. The model learns “this is normal.”

2Sample / representation bias

Some groups are under-represented in the data (few rural, informal, or women-owned records), so the model’s patterns for them are thin and unreliable.

3Label bias

Someone had to decide what counted as a “good hire” or “high performer.” If those past human judgements were biased, the AI inherits that definition.

4Proxy features & feedback loops

Proxies (Lesson 3) smuggle protected attributes back in; feedback loops (Module 2) make a biased prediction come true and then cite itself as proof.

5Deployment and oversight bias

A model may be used in a setting, population, or workflow it was not designed for. Reviewers may over-trust it, apply it unevenly, or lack the power to correct harmful outcomes.

Lesson 5

The South African legal lens

South African law prohibits unfair discrimination, including direct and indirect unfair discrimination. A lack of discriminatory intent does not automatically end the inquiry, but unequal outcomes still need context, evidence, and a fairness assessment.

The instruments that already apply to AI decisions

  • Constitution, section 9: protects equality and prohibits unfair direct or indirect discrimination on listed grounds, including race, gender, disability, age, language, and others.
  • PEPUDA (Act 4 of 2000): gives effect to the equality framework by prohibiting unfair discrimination. A neutral-looking rule can raise indirect-unfair-discrimination concerns if it produces unequal effects linked to a prohibited ground. Whether it is unlawful depends on the facts, impact, purpose, justification, and available alternatives.
  • Employment Equity Act: prohibits unfair direct or indirect discrimination in employment policies and practices. AI-assisted hiring, promotion, assessment, and scoring tools may raise Employment Equity Act concerns where they affect applicants or employees.
  • POPIA, section 71: restricts certain decisions based solely on automated processing of personal information for profiling, where the decision has legal consequences or substantially affects a person. The exact protection depends on the system and circumstances, including whether meaningful human involvement exists.
  • National Credit Act: promotes a fair and non-discriminatory consumer-credit market, regulates credit information, and gives people rights to access and challenge credit records. These protections can be relevant where automated scoring is used, but the exact remedy depends on the facts and legal provisions involved.

The line that matters: “We did not intend to discriminate” does not end the inquiry. A neutral-looking AI system can still create unequal effects that require investigation, justification, correction, or legal advice.

Lesson 6

The “objective machine” trap

Why is AI bias often more dangerous than human bias? Because of how much we trust a number that came from a computer.

⚠Automation bias

People defer to a machine’s output more than to a colleague’s opinion — even when the machine is wrong. A biased recommendation gets a free pass because “the system said so.”

Human bias is one person, sometimes. AI bias is scaled, consistent, instant — and wears a mask of neutrality.

“It’s just maths, it can’t be racist” is the trap. The maths is real; the data and the definitions behind it carry human history.

Important: Human review only helps if the reviewer has authority, enough information, time to question the output, and a meaningful ability to override it.
Lesson 7

Detecting it: the indirect discrimination test

You cannot fix what you cannot see. The practical way to check any tool is to compare outcomes across groups — the test you’ll run in Phase 4.

Four questions that surface bias

  1. Outcomes by group: are approval, flag, or score rates — including false positive and false negative rates — notably different across race, gender, age, disability, language, or area?
  2. Proxies: does the model use location, school, language, name, employment type, or device — variables that may reflect unequal access rather than underlying ability or risk?
  3. Who’s missing: which groups are thin or absent in the training data?
  4. Explanation and contestability: can the person affected understand why the decision was made — and is there a meaningful way to question or appeal it?
Rule of thumb: if you cannot test for unequal outcomes in a lawful, privacy-aware way, you cannot claim the system is fair — you are only assuming it. If meaningful differences appear across groups, the next step is to investigate and, where needed, seek specialist support.

Fairness is not one metric

Equal error rates across groups, equal positive rates, and equal accuracy can all be measured — and they can conflict with each other. Choosing which fairness measure matters most is a value decision, not a technical one. It depends on who bears the cost of a wrong answer.

Intersectionality

A system may perform adequately for women and adequately for Black people — but perform worse for Black women specifically. Checking one dimension at a time can miss harm that only appears at the intersection of multiple characteristics. Where possible, test for intersecting groups.

Community voice

People most affected by an AI system often identify failure modes that technical audits miss — through lived experience of the decisions it makes about them. Meaningful involvement of affected communities in design, testing, and review is not just ethical practice; it is also better engineering.

Vendor and procurement accountability

Buying or licensing an AI tool does not transfer responsibility. Before procuring or renewing, ask: What data was it trained on? What populations was it tested on? What are its known limitations? Who monitors ongoing fairness, and what audit rights do we have? What is the human-override procedure?

Remedies after detection

Detecting unfair outcomes is not the end of the process. Remedies can include: retraining the model on more representative data; removing or replacing biased proxies; adjusting decision thresholds for different populations; adding or strengthening human-review steps; suspending or decommissioning a tool that cannot be made fair; and informing or compensating affected people where harm has occurred.

The key principle

Fairness is not the default.
It must be designed, tested, monitored, and governed.

AI inherits the inequality in its data and hands it back wearing the mask of objectivity. Detecting and correcting that is a human responsibility — and in South Africa, equality, employment, credit, privacy, and sector-specific duties may all apply.

A South African profiling scenario · ~20 min
Scenario · Public Sector
A “data-driven” predictive policing tool

Composite learning scenario based on documented South African surveillance, spatial inequality, and predictive-policing concerns. Not a finding that a named agency deployed this exact system or produced these exact outcomes.

A metro police department buys an AI system to decide where to send patrols. The vendor’s pitch:

“Objective, data-driven policing. The algorithm sends officers where crime will happen — no human bias.”

The model learns from the department’s records: past arrests & reported crime · location / suburb · time of day · prior call-outs. It never uses race.

Three months later

  • Patrols are concentrated in a handful of low-income, historically Black townships
  • More officers there → more stops → more arrests — which the model reads as “more crime”
  • Wealthier suburbs are barely patrolled, so little is recorded there
  • Residents say they are being watched because of where they live, not what they did

Camera network surveillance

In a city shaped by apartheid spatial planning, where cameras are placed can affect which places and people become more visible to policing and data collection.

Hypothetical person-based risk-scoring scenario

Automated risk-scoring

A system that scores individuals using residential area, employment type, or prior police contact could reproduce unequal treatment if those inputs reflect historic over-policing, unequal economic opportunity, or prior biased enforcement.

These patterns may raise concerns about indirect unfair discrimination, because apparently neutral criteria — such as location, employment type, or police-contact history — can produce unequal effects along racialised and spatial lines. What this case teaches

“We removed race” misses the point

Three mechanisms may be operating at once.

Proxy discrimination, where location or arrest history reflects racialised inequality; feedback loops, where patrol deployment creates records that justify more patrols; and uneven recording, where some communities become more visible to the system than others.

The key question is not only “Did the system use race?” It is: Does the system produce unequal effects, and what data, proxies, feedback loops, and human choices are creating them?

These patterns may raise indirect-unfair-discrimination concerns. A lack of discriminatory intent does not end the inquiry. Responsibility remains with the people and institutions that chose the tool, defined its objective, supplied the data, deployed it, reviewed it, and acted on its outputs.

What evidence would you need before deciding this system is unfair?

e.g. deployment data by area; stop, search, and arrest rates; false-positive and false-negative rates; evidence of harm; audit results; override and appeal records; community consultation.

Reflect — What assumptions are built in?
Surfacing the values hidden in a model · ~20 min

Every AI system reflects choices and assumptions: what to measure, what counts as “success,” whose history is included, and what is left out. The policing tool treated past arrests as evidence of future crime risk — an assumption that can confuse enforcement patterns with underlying harm. Now turn the lens on your own world.

Assumptions hide in ordinary choices…

  • Hiring: “good candidates look like the people we hired before” — which schools, which names, which areas?
  • Credit & insurance: “this area is high-risk” — risk of what, measured how, inherited from when?
  • Performance: “a high performer is someone like our current top performers” — defined by whom?

Each is a value judgement dressed as a data field.

Worked Equity Audit — Metro Police Predictive Patrol Deployment (Encounter scenario)

Worksheet completed

Tool audited: Metro police AI patrol routing system — an algorithmic tool that assigns patrol density by area based on historical crime and arrest data.

Decision it drives: Where to send police patrols; which areas receive heightened enforcement presence.

Listed grounds potentially affected: Race (via location proxy); socioeconomic status (via area classification).

Proxies in the data: Historical arrest and call-out records (biased by decades of over-policing in specific areas); suburb/location (apartheid spatial planning makes location a near-perfect race proxy in SA); prior police contact (reflects policing intensity, not underlying crime rates).

Disparate-impact check: Patrols concentrated in historically Black townships; comparable historically white suburbs receive minimal deployment. Outcome data confirms the disparity — but the data itself is biased by the deployment pattern. More patrols produce more recorded arrests, which the model reads as evidence of more crime, reinforcing the loop. Meaningful measurement requires comparing AI deployment data against independent crime data (victimisation surveys, not police records).

Who is harmed if wrong: Residents of over-policed areas — subject to disproportionate stops, searches, and arrest risk without evidence of higher actual crime rates relative to under-policed areas.

Legal exposure: PEPUDA — indirect discrimination on the ground of race through location proxy (non-employment context). POPIA s71 — where the system produces individual risk scores, data subjects have the right not to be subject to decisions based solely on automated processing.

Human oversight: Patrol commanders receive deployment recommendations and can override them. In practice, automation bias means the AI allocation is rarely contested. Oversight is present in form; absent in substance.

1 — Where could this tool produce indirect discrimination, and through which proxy?

Township and historically Black residential area residents are the affected group. The neutral-looking rule is: “deploy patrols where recorded crime density is highest.” The proxy is historical arrest density — which in SA reflects where police looked, shaped by decades of over-policing specific areas. This creates the feedback loop from the Encounter: more patrols → more recorded arrests → model reads as higher crime → more patrols. The discrimination is not in the algorithm. It is in what the algorithm was trained to optimise.

2 — How would you detect it?

Compare patrol hours per capita across two suburb groups: historically designated Black residential areas and historically designated white residential areas of comparable population size. A statistically significant disparity (>2:1 patrol density ratio is a useful starting threshold) constitutes prima facie indirect discrimination evidence under PEPUDA. A second check: compare recorded crime rates (police data) against independent victimisation survey data by area — if these diverge sharply, the recorded data is reflecting policing intensity rather than actual crime rates, confirming the bias mechanism.

3 — Who is accountable under SA law, and what is your one recommended control?

The departmental official who procured and approved the system bears primary accountability under PEPUDA — not the algorithm, and not individual officers following deployment recommendations. “The system recommended it” is not a defence for a public authority deploying AI that produces racially discriminatory outcomes on a listed ground. One control: institute a quarterly equity review comparing patrol deployment density with independent crime data and suburb demographic profiles, with a mandatory audit trigger if deployment ratios between comparable areas exceed a defined threshold. The current absence of any outcome monitoring is itself an accountability gap.
