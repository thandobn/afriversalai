Module 7 · Sector Specific · Medical & Healthcare

AI in Healthcare:
Efficiency, Accountability, and Who Pays

When a hospital group announces AI will replace hundreds of administrative staff to save R2 billion — who asks whether that's good for patients? This module starts with a real SA case where those questions went unanswered, and builds the framework to ask them in your own work.

60 minutes · 4 phases Medical & Healthcare track · Requires Module 0 foundation
1
Encounter — The press release and the denial
Read both. Answer before we unpack · ~15 min

In April 2025, two stories ran in South African media about the same healthcare organisation within 48 hours of each other. Read them in order.

Daily Maverick · 15 April 2025
Mediclinic freezes hiring and cuts admin jobs — AI strategy targets R2 billion in savings
South Africa's second-largest private hospital group has announced a hiring freeze on all non-essential administrative positions and is offering voluntary severance packages to existing admin staff. The group said robotic process automation is already "performing significant hours of work annually." A new Chief Data Officer has been appointed to lead the AI and automation strategy. The target: R1.8–2 billion in annual cost savings by FY2027.
Daily Maverick · 17 April 2025
Mediclinic claps back: "There is no specific directive to replace staff with AI"
Following media coverage of its AI cost-reduction strategy, Mediclinic issued a clarifying statement saying the organisation has "no specific directive to replace staff with AI." The group said automation is being used in back-office shared services and not in clinical roles. Administrative staff who do intake, records, billing, and scheduling were not specifically addressed.

Before reading further — your instincts:

What This Case Actually Shows

The Mediclinic case isn't about whether AI is good or bad in healthcare — it's about who gets to ask the patient-centred questions when AI decisions are made.

Mediclinic is using AI in: robotic process automation for back-office shared services, automated patient no-show prediction (resulting in 100 additional bookings per day in trials), automated medical coding and eligibility checks, and patient flow management. These are legitimate efficiency applications. The efficiency gains are real.

What the press coverage surfaced — and what the organisation did not publicly answer — was the connection between administrative efficiency and patient care quality. Who handles insurance pre-authorisation when the AI fails to get it right, and the patient is already in surgery prep? Who does the human follow-up when an automated scheduling system sends a patient to the wrong clinic? These are not hypothetical questions. They are the practical consequences of removing human capacity from administrative processes.

The question this case forces: When AI is used as a cost-reduction tool in a healthcare setting, the "savings" come from somewhere. The professional obligation — clinical and administrative — is to ask where.

3
Concept — The accountability chain in SA healthcare AI
Who is responsible — and what the law says · ~20 min

The unique accountability challenge in clinical AI

When AI makes a wrong call in healthcare, the consequences can be irreversible. This creates a specific accountability obligation that goes beyond most other professional contexts: the practitioner cannot delegate judgment to the system, no matter how sophisticated the system is.

The HPCSA position (November 2025)

The Health Professions Council of South Africa revised its ethical guidelines on AI in November 2025. Core principle: the practitioner retains full accountability for every clinical decision, even when AI assists. The system can recommend. The clinician decides. Patients must be informed when AI is used in their care. Practitioners must not rely on "informal or unrecognised tools." This is guidance, not law — but it establishes the professional standard against which conduct will be measured.

AI tools active in SA healthcare right now

Aidoc aiOS — critical pathology detection in radiology (Western Cape)

Deployed across 8 hospitals, used by 35+ radiologists daily. Flags suspected intracranial haemorrhage, pulmonary embolism, rib fractures, breast cancer. Documented catch: flagged a haemorrhage in a patient scheduled for surgery who would have received anticoagulants without it.

Qure.ai qXR — TB screening in KwaZulu-Natal (community program)

Portable X-ray vans + AI detection. 6,500 people screened in first 6 months, 187 TB cases identified that passive screening would have missed. Validated in Lancet Digital Health 2024. Works in areas with no radiologist.

Discovery Personal Health Pathways

2.1 million adult members. AI generates personalised health recommendations from 33TB of claims + Vitality data. Flags screening reminders, chronic disease alerts, care escalations. 500GB of new data processed daily. Global first.

PRIME — AI clinical decision support for primary care

SA-built tool for GPs, clinics, and emergency rooms. References SA clinical guidelines specifically. Supports nurse-led care with automatic forwarding to a supervising doctor for remote review. Each consultation reviewed by a human doctor — AI extends reach, doesn't replace oversight.

The four legal frameworks that apply

POPIA — Health information is Special Personal Information

Processing health data is prohibited unless narrow exceptions apply (explicit consent, or necessary for medical treatment). AI model training requires large historical datasets — patient data cannot be repurposed for training without consent from the original consultation. A patient whose 2018 records are now training a 2024 diagnostic AI may never have consented to that use.

Health Professions Act — Accountability follows the clinician, not the algorithm

Does not mention AI. Governs professional conduct and negligence. If a clinician acts on an AI recommendation that causes harm, the Act is the instrument of discipline — not any AI-specific law. The AI developer carries product liability under common law. Clinical accountability lands on the human who acted on the output.

SAHPRA AI/ML Medical Device Guidance (August 2025)

First formal regulatory requirements for AI-enabled medical devices in SA. Requires: device classification, training/validation dataset disclosure, clinical validation on representative populations. Key consequence: an AI diagnostic tool validated on European or US datasets may not meet SAHPRA's standard if it hasn't been tested on SA patient populations — where TB burden, HIV comorbidity, and population genetics differ significantly from training data.

NHI Act (2023) — The largest patient dataset SA will ever create

Requires electronic health records and interoperability for the NHI fund. This will create the most powerful AI training set in SA healthcare history. Who controls it, who can access it for AI development, and what consent mechanisms apply are not yet settled. The NHI Act contains no AI-specific provisions. Healthcare AI governance in SA is a field being built in real time.

Where healthcare AI gets it right — the Qure.ai TB program: The key design decision that makes the KZN TB program work: the AI triages for follow-up testing, not treatment. A false positive means an extra test. A false negative in SASSA means no food money. A false negative in a clinical AI treatment recommendation could mean no treatment. The consequence of error shapes how much autonomy the AI should have. Qure.ai's designers understood this. Not all healthcare AI designs do.
4
Apply — The nurse's override decision
Run the Funda Five on a real clinical AI scenario · ~15 min
Scenario — Primary Care Nurse · Limpopo Clinic

The AI says escalate. You disagree. You need to document why — and the system won't close without a reason.

You are a nurse at a primary care clinic in Limpopo using the PRIME clinical decision support tool. A patient presents with symptoms the AI classifies as "high urgency — recommend escalation to district hospital." You have seen this presentation many times. You know this patient. You believe the symptoms are manageable at clinic level and that sending her to the district hospital 40km away would be unnecessarily disruptive and potentially dangerous for an elderly patient with limited transport options. The AI's classification is based on symptom inputs alone — it doesn't know her travel situation, her age context, or your clinical history with her.

The PRIME system requires you to document an override reason before you can close the consultation. What do you write — and what professional obligation does that documentation create?

Your task: Use the Funda Five to analyse what is happening in this interaction between a nurse, an AI system, and a patient — and what professional responsibility looks like in this moment.
1
TASK — What is the AI designed to do? Is it the right kind of decision for AI?
What type of AI task is clinical decision support? Is "should this patient be escalated?" a decision AI can make reliably? What inputs does the system have — and what is it missing?
2
DATA — What data is the AI using to produce this recommendation?
What context is the AI working from? What patient data is being processed — and under what data protection obligations? When a consulting record enters PRIME, who owns that data?
3
TOOL — Is PRIME the right tool for this specific decision? And is the override documentation a feature or a burden?
PRIME is designed to extend clinical capacity in under-resourced settings. Is this the scenario it was designed for? The override documentation requirement — is that protecting patients or constraining clinicians?
4
TRUST — How would you detect if your override decision is wrong?
You've decided not to escalate. What follow-up plan ensures you'd know if the patient deteriorates? What's the monitoring and safety net for a decision that deviates from an AI recommendation?
5
HUMAN — Who is accountable if this patient deteriorates after your override?
You overrode the AI recommendation. You documented your reasoning. If the patient worsens at home, what is your professional accountability under the Health Professions Act and the HPCSA ethical guidelines? What would the override documentation you wrote mean for your professional standing?
