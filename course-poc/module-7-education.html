Module 7 · Sector Specific · Education

AI in Education:
When the Detection Tool Is the Problem

UCT became the first South African university to formally scrap AI detection software — because the tool designed to catch cheating was itself producing unfair results. This module starts with that decision and builds the framework educators need to think clearly about AI in assessment, teaching, and institutional governance.

60 minutes · 4 phases Education track · Requires Module 0 foundation
1
Encounter — The university that stopped detecting AI
Two headlines. One institutional decision. What do you think? · ~15 min

Read these two stories. They are about the same problem, approached from different angles. Then answer the questions below before we unpack the full picture.

Daily Maverick · 5 April 2025 · "CheatGPT Crisis"
SA universities faced with a burgeoning degree of AI-written academic assignments
Lecturers at UCT, Wits, UJ, and Unisa are receiving assignments they suspect are AI-generated but cannot prove it. AI detection software is described by one contract lecturer as "a victim of hope and marketing." A survey of undergraduates found 37.3% had used ChatGPT to answer an essay question. Students report using AI primarily to understand concepts their lecturers didn't explain clearly — not simply to avoid doing work.
UCT News · 24 July 2025
UCT scraps flawed AI detectors — Turnitin AI Score discontinued from 1 October 2025
The University of Cape Town announced it will discontinue the use of all AI detection tools, including Turnitin's AI Score, effective 1 October 2025. The Senate Teaching and Learning Committee found the tools produce unreliable false positives and false negatives. The official reason: continued use "compromises student trust and academic fairness." UCT's Framework for AI in Education was simultaneously released, establishing principles for constructive integration rather than detection and punishment.

Before reading further:

What the Research Shows

The detection tools have a documented equity problem — and SA's multilingual student population is exactly where that problem is sharpest.

What the tools actually do: AI detection tools like Turnitin's AI Score work by identifying stylistic patterns associated with AI-generated text — formal structure, certain syntactic patterns, lack of idiomatic variation. The problem: these are also features of writing by people for whom academic English is not their first language. A student writing formal, careful, less idiomatic English because it's their second or third language can be flagged by the same patterns the system uses to identify AI text.

The SA dimension: In a country with 11 official languages, where over 60% of university students are writing academic work in a language that is not their home language, this is not a marginal edge case — it is the mainstream population. UCT named "compromising student trust" as the key reason for scrapping the tools. That's institutional acknowledgement of the equity problem.

What students say: Research across UCT, Cape Peninsula University of Technology, Stellenbosch, Rhodes, and Wits found that the primary stated use of AI among students was understanding concepts their lecturers hadn't explained clearly — not avoiding work. 41% of AI use happens on laptops; 29.8% on smartphones, meaning a significant proportion of student AI interaction is mobile-first.

The core tension: AI detection tries to solve an academic integrity problem by using AI — a technology that produces unreliable results in exactly the context where it's most consequential. UCT's response is the cleaner one: if you can't detect reliably, design so there's nothing to detect.

3
Concept — The regulatory and institutional framework for AI in SA education
What applies, what's missing, and where Siyavula got it right · ~20 min

The structural challenge: SA's AI tools weren't built for SA classrooms

The vast majority of AI tools deployed in SA education were trained on English-language, high-connectivity, Global North data. SA classrooms — with 11 official languages, urban/rural device disparity, load shedding, and a multilingual student population — require something different. When you import a tool that wasn't built for your context, the question isn't whether it will fail — it's who it will fail for.

The language equity problem in practical terms

SA's Language in Education Policy requires instruction in a learner's home language where reasonably practicable, especially in the Foundation Phase. AI tutoring tools, writing assistants, and grading tools are overwhelmingly trained on English data. They will systematically perform worse for learners whose primary language is isiZulu, Sesotho, Afrikaans, isiXhosa, or any of the other 8 official languages. This is not a hypothetical — it's a predictable consequence of where training data exists globally.

AI tools active in SA education right now

Siyavula — CAPS-aligned adaptive practice (2M+ learners, Grades 4–12)

ML algorithm sequences practice questions by demonstrated mastery. Zero-rated by Vodacom and MTN. 10M+ DBE textbooks printed for schools without devices. Designed for the constraint from the start — not retrofitted.

Ms Zora — DBE-endorsed coding and robotics AI tutor

Designed specifically to support the DBE's Coding and Robotics curriculum rollout — which launched without enough trained teachers. Curriculum-aligned, low-device compatible, teacher analytics dashboard. Built for SA curriculum before any other market.

Turnitin AI Score — being discontinued at UCT from Oct 2025

Stylistic pattern detection for AI text. Documented false positive rate disproportionately affects non-native English writers. UCT formally stopped using it. Other SA institutions still use it — policy is not consistent nationally.

Lelapa AI VulaVula — isiZulu, Afrikaans, Sesotho, English NLP

SA-built speech-to-text, transcription, and translation for African languages. Not yet deployed at classroom scale but represents the infrastructure needed for truly multilingual AI in SA education. Founded by Dr. Pelonomi Moiloa.

Google Workspace for Education + DHET partnership (2025) · Microsoft Copilot via DHET MOU

Both major platforms are entering SA higher education through formal government partnerships. 10,000 Google Career Certificate scholarships, AI teacher training, curriculum support. Copilot embedded in Microsoft 365 across universities and TVET colleges. Institutional-level decisions made without systematic AI literacy training for the educators who'll use them.

What applies legally in SA education contexts

POPIA — Learner data is personal information subject to full protection

Parental consent is required for processing personal information of learners under 18. AI platforms embedded in school systems create structural consent problems — learners often cannot opt out without losing access to the educational tool. Learner records must be retained for 7 years after leaving, meaning data held in AI platforms needs appropriate long-term security. When a UK-based edtech company deploys in SA schools, whose data protection law governs?

SA Schools Act — School Governing Bodies have procurement authority

SGBs have governance authority over school management decisions, including technology procurement. If a principal adopts an AI platform, the SGB has a governance role in that decision. The Act also protects learner rights in ways that intersect with AI — a learner cannot be disciplined for suspected AI use without due process that the school must be able to demonstrate.

HEQC and Umalusi — accreditation implications of AI in assessment

Changes to assessment methods that affect accredited programme outcomes can have HEQC accreditation implications. Umalusi's integrity framework for matric exams (human invigilation, centralised marking) means the NSC is currently the least AI-affected assessment tier — but that position requires active maintenance as AI tools become more capable. Institutions that significantly change their assessment design in response to AI need to consider whether those changes require HEQC notification.

What Siyavula gets right that most SA EdTech gets wrong: Siyavula designed for the constraint before building the product. Zero-rated access (no data costs for students). Print layer for schools without devices (10M textbooks distributed). CAPS-aligned before any other market. The adaptive AI sequences based on SA curriculum — not imported from a US or UK context. The result: 2 million learners, including township and rural learners at a scale no private school platform has reached. This is the design benchmark: start with the SA constraint, not the Global North assumption.
4
Apply — The platform pitch scenario
Run the Funda Five before a school adopts an AI tool · ~15 min
Scenario — School Principal · Soweto

A UK edtech company is pitching an AI tutoring platform for your 1,200 learners. The results look impressive. The data collection is extensive.

An edtech company based in the UK pitches you an AI-powered personalised tutoring platform for Grades 7–12. The pitch deck shows results from schools in the UK and Kenya. The platform collects data on learning behaviour, performance, device usage, and content engagement. The server infrastructure is based in the Netherlands. The subscription costs R240 per learner per year. Your school has 1,200 learners, 40% of whom share a device, and 4–8 hour daily load shedding.

The company representative says: "We've seen amazing results. The AI adapts to each student's level." The pitch includes a 30-day free trial. You are being asked to make a decision before the next term starts.

Your task: Use the Funda Five to evaluate this platform before saying yes. Every lens surfaces a question this company hasn't answered.
1
TASK — Is this the right kind of job for AI?
Personalised tutoring is a legitimate AI application. But is this AI built for the task as it exists in your school — or built for a different context and sold here? What questions would tell you whether the AI was designed for SA learners?
2
DATA — What learner data is this platform collecting? Under which law?
The servers are in the Netherlands. The learners are SA minors. What POPIA obligations apply? What parental consent process is required before learners' data is processed? Who owns the data — your school, the SGB, or the company?
3
TOOL — Is this platform built for your school's infrastructure reality?
40% of your learners share devices. You have 4–8 hours of load shedding daily. Does this platform work offline? Does it work on a low-end smartphone? What happens to learning continuity during a power cut? The pitch didn't mention either of these.
4
TRUST — How would you detect if the AI is teaching something wrong?
AI tutors can produce incorrect explanations, especially for topics underrepresented in training data. What monitoring process would catch a systematic error before it affected a large number of learners? Does the company provide any mechanism for reporting and correcting AI errors?
5
HUMAN — Who is accountable if a learner is taught incorrectly and it affects their matric results?
The company is based in the UK. If the AI teaches the wrong interpretation of a CAPS topic and a learner uses it in the final exam, who is responsible? What does the contract say about liability? And who at your school owns this decision under the SA Schools Act?
