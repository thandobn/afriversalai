Section 1

Executive Summary

  1. We have real competitors — 8 identified. The AI literacy training market in South Africa is active. The most comparable competitor is the African Academy of AI (AAAI), which uses near-identical language ("premier AI capacity builder in Africa") and already has institutional partnerships with GIBS, Henley Business School, and UCT. We are not the only player, but the market is large enough for specialised positions.
  2. SETA accreditation is our single biggest differentiator — if we achieve it first. College Africa Group holds MICT SETA accreditation for IT courses; no confirmed competitor holds SETA accreditation specifically for AI literacy. First-mover on SETA-accredited AI literacy unlocks Skills Development Levy (SDL) cost recovery for corporate buyers — changing the purchase math dramatically for L&D managers.
  3. Our price point is strategically correct. Henley charges R14,999 for 3 days. UCT charges R9,000 for 3 days. Our founding cohort at R14,995 for 8 weeks of structured learning with a human-assessed capstone is better value — but we must frame it against institutional programs, not against free Microsoft content.
  4. The government vertical is wide open. No competitor is explicitly pitching "department-wide AI literacy for public servants, aligned with South Africa's 2024 National AI Policy." This framing — combined with PSETA accreditation as a future goal — is a high-value, low-competition market position.
  5. A South African AI research model (MzansiLM) exists and we should reference it. UCT released an open-source LLM covering all 11 official SA languages in early 2026. Citing it in our curriculum costs nothing and substantiates our "African-centered AI" claim with a real, citable example. It may also help us build a translation pipeline for future multilingual content.
Section 2

MzansiLM — South Africa's Own AI Model

What It Is

MzansiLM is a foundational language model released in early 2026 by researchers at the University of Cape Town — covering all 11 official South African written languages. It is the first publicly available AI model trained from scratch specifically on South African language data. Think of it as raw material from a local foundry: not a finished product, but something we can shape for specific purposes.

AttributeDetail
Built byUniversity of Cape Town — Dept of Computer Science
Size125 million parameters (research scale — not GPT-4)
LanguagesAll 11 official SA written languages
LicenseCC BY 4.0 — open source, commercial use permitted with attribution
AvailabilityFree download on HuggingFace (anrilombard/mzansilm-125m)
LimitationToo small for general conversation; best for narrow, structured tasks (translation, classification)

How AfriversalAI Can Use It

High Feasibility

Translation pipeline. Fine-tune for SA language translation of course materials. Draft isiZulu/Sesotho content for human review — makes multilingual expansion faster and cheaper.

Medium Feasibility

Quiz generation. Generate assessment questions in SA languages after fine-tuning on educational content. Outputs require human QA before use.

Phase 2 (Later)

Curriculum reference. Cite MzansiLM in Module 5 or 6 as a real example of African AI research. Costs nothing and substantiates our "African-centered" positioning.

aaai.africa
Watch Closely

The most strategically similar competitor. AAAI positions itself as "The Premier AI Strategy, Education, and Capacity Builder in Africa" — language nearly identical to our own. They deliver B2B programs to banks, insurers, regulators, and corporates across Africa, with institutional partnerships at GIBS, Henley Business School, UCT, and the African Association of Business Schools. Target: C-suite, HR, enterprise
Pricing: Quote-based (enterprise)
SETA: None confirmed
Platform: No LMS

henleysa.ac.za
Monitor

Offers "Strategies for Advanced AI Leadership in Africa" — a 3-day face-to-face workshop in Johannesburg at R14,999 per seat (~$820 USD), targeting senior executives. Delivered in partnership with AAAI, so these two are effectively the same training pipeline at the premium executive end. Non-credit-bearing, no SETA accreditation. The institutional brand carries weight with certain buyers but does not serve the workforce-wide L&D manager.

Target: Senior executives
Pricing: R14,999 / 3 days
SETA: None
Format: Face-to-face workshop

Section 5

Strategic Implications

1

SETA accreditation is the single most important action item

No competitor in this landscape holds confirmed SETA accreditation for an AI literacy program. College Africa Group has MICT SETA accreditation for IT courses generally, but not for AI literacy specifically. If AfriversalAI achieves SETA accreditation first, L&D managers can recover 40–60% of training costs through the Skills Development Levy — which changes a R14,995 investment into an effective R7,500–9,000 investment per learner. This is a procurement argument no competitor can currently make. It should be the team's top operational priority.

2

AAAI is our benchmark competitor — differentiate on depth, not positioning

AAAI is the most credible competitor and uses nearly identical positioning language. We cannot out-credential them in the short term (they have GIBS, Henley, and UCT partnerships). Our differentiation must be specific: AfriversalAI goes deeper into judgment skills for a broader workforce audience, at a lower per-seat cost, with a purpose-built LMS and a human-assessed certificate. The pitch to an L&D manager is: "AAAI trains your board — we train your team." That argument only holds if the SETA path is real and the assessor capacity is in place.

3

The government vertical is wide open — and fundable

No competitor is explicitly pitching department-wide AI literacy for public servants as a primary market. South Africa's National AI Policy (2024) creates real institutional pressure on government departments to upskill staff on responsible AI. A cohort positioned as "AI judgment training aligned with the 2024 National AI Policy" — potentially eligible for PSETA (Public Service SETA) funding — is a high-value, low-competition framing. This should be explored as a distinct sales motion alongside the corporate B2B track.

4

Pricing narrative should anchor against Henley and UCT, not free content

Henley charges R14,999 for 3 days with no assessed certificate. UCT charges R9,000 for 3 days. AfriversalAI at R14,995 for 8 weeks of structured learning, live forums, and a human-assessed capstone is meaningfully better value per hour of instruction and per unit of demonstrated competency. All pricing conversations should anchor against institutional alternatives — not against Microsoft's free AI tools training, which serves a different purpose and a different buyer.

5

Multilingual delivery is a real differentiator — but only once it is real

Every competitor in this landscape delivers in English only. AfriversalAI's ambition to offer content in Afrikaans, French, and isiZulu is genuinely differentiated — particularly for government clients who serve Zulu-speaking and Afrikaans-speaking communities. However, this claim should only appear in marketing once translations have been reviewed by native speakers. In the interim, referencing MzansiLM (UCT's 11-language SA model) in the curriculum is a cost-free way to substantiate our commitment to African language inclusion without overpromising delivery.
