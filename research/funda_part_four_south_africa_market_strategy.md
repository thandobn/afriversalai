# Funda AI Literacy Initiative — Part Four Research Pack

## South Africa Market, Procurement, Regulation, Pricing, Accreditation, and Pilot Strategy

**Purpose:** This Part Four research pack fills the practical gaps left by the earlier research. It focuses on whether Funda can become a real South Africa-first product: who buys it, how procurement may work, how regulation matters, what pricing logic makes sense, what accreditation path is realistic, and how to design the first pilot.

**Core conclusion:** Funda should not be positioned as “another AI course.” It should be positioned as a **responsible workplace AI adoption program** delivered through training.

---

# 1. Strategic conclusion

Funda should be positioned as:

> A South Africa-first AI literacy and responsible AI adoption program for organizations whose staff are already using AI but lack a shared standard for safety, quality, verification, and human judgment.

The stronger product category is not only education.

It is closer to:

- training,
- change management,
- responsible AI adoption,
- workplace governance,
- risk reduction,
- organizational capability building.

The strongest buyer-facing line:

> Funda helps organizations move from informal AI experimentation to responsible AI practice.

# 2. Why South Africa is a strong Phase 1 market

South Africa is a strong first market because it has:

- a large formal corporate sector,
- mature HR and Learning & Development teams,
- active digital transformation programs,
- public-sector interest in AI policy,
- major cloud and AI infrastructure investment,
- significant legal/compliance awareness,
- high need for context-specific AI education,
- regional influence across the continent.

Microsoft announced plans to train **1 million South Africans in AI and cybersecurity skills by 2026**, targeting companies, government, youth, and certification pathways. Microsoft also announced additional AI infrastructure investment in South Africa in 2025.

**Implication:** The market demand is real. But Funda should not compete directly with Microsoft-style training. Funda’s differentiator should be the human judgment, verification, governance, and local workplace context layer.

# 3. Why now

Organizations are in a messy middle stage:

| Stage | What is happening |
|---|---|
| Awareness | Executives know AI matters. |
| Experimentation | Employees are already trying AI tools. |
| Confusion | Staff do not know what is safe, allowed, accurate, or useful. |
| Policy lag | Organizations may not yet have clear AI-use rules. |
| Risk | Sensitive data, hallucinations, poor outputs, and overtrust can create damage. |
| Buyer pressure | HR, IT, digital transformation, legal, and risk teams are being asked to respond. |

Funda’s market opportunity is this messy middle.

Suggested messaging:

> AI adoption is already happening. Funda helps your people use it responsibly before mistakes become policy failures, client issues, or data risks.

# 4. South African AI policy moment

In 2026, South Africa withdrew its first draft national AI policy after it was found to include fictitious or likely AI-generated citations. The communications minister described this as a serious oversight and emphasized the importance of human oversight in AI use. A revised policy is expected to go through further review, with a public comment target in January 2027.

This is important for Funda because it proves that AI literacy is not abstract. A public institution can suffer credibility damage because people used AI or AI-like outputs without proper verification.

Sources:
- Reuters — South Africa withdraws AI policy due to fake AI-generated sources: https://www.reuters.com/world/africa/south-africa-withdraws-ai-policy-due-fake-ai-generated-sources-2026-04-27/
- Reuters — South Africa targets January 2027 for revised AI policy: https://www.reuters.com/world/africa/south-africa-targets-january-2027-revised-ai-policy-after-earlier-withdrawal-2026-05-26/

## 4.1 Course implication: signature case study

### The Policy Draft With Fake Citations

Learners receive a short AI-assisted policy memo with fake citations and subtle errors.

They must:

1. identify unsupported claims,
2. verify citations,
3. classify the risk,
4. decide who should review it,
5. rewrite the workflow,
6. create a rule for future AI-assisted policy drafting.

This should be one of Funda’s strongest South Africa-specific teaching assets.

# 7. POPIA and AI literacy

## 7.1 Why POPIA matters for Funda

South Africa’s Protection of Personal Information Act, 2013 regulates how personal information is processed by public and private bodies. It includes obligations around lawful processing, security safeguards, breach reporting, cross-border transfers, automated decision-making, and special personal information.

This matters directly for generative AI because staff may paste personal, confidential, client, employee, or citizen data into AI tools without understanding:

- whether that counts as processing,
- whether the tool stores data,
- whether the data leaves South Africa,
- whether an approved tool is required,
- whether the output could create harm.

Sources:
- Protection of Personal Information Act overview: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protection_of_Personal_Information_Act%2C_2013
- Information Regulator South Africa: https://inforegulator.org.za/

## 7.2 Funda should not give legal advice

Funda should **not** market itself as a legal compliance certification unless it has legal review and specialist partners.

Instead, say:

> Funda teaches practical AI literacy aligned with responsible data handling and POPIA-aware workplace behavior.

## 7.3 Practical POPIA-aware training topics

Every learner should understand:

- What personal information is.
- What special personal information is.
- What confidential organizational information is.
- What client/citizen/employee data is.
- Why public AI tools may create risk.
- Why approved enterprise tools may still require judgment.
- Why cross-border processing matters.
- Why automated decisions about people are high-risk.
- Why output verification is part of responsible processing.
- When to escalate to IT/legal/compliance.

## 7.4 Signature course activity: “Can I paste this?”

Learners classify examples:

| Example | Can paste into public AI? | Safer alternative |
|---|---|---|
| Public press release | Usually yes | Still check accuracy |
| Internal meeting notes with names | Risky | Remove names or use approved tool |
| Client contract | No unless approved | Use secure enterprise tool / legal review |
| Employee performance review | No | Do not use without explicit policy |
| Citizen complaint with ID number | No | Redact and escalate |
| Generic job description | Usually yes | Remove internal salary or strategy info |
| Financial forecast | Risky/confidential | Use approved tool and review |
| Public legislation text | Usually yes | Verify against official source |

# 8. B-BBEE, SETA, and accreditation pathways

## 8.1 Why this matters

South African corporate buyers may care about training not only as capability building, but also as part of broader skills development, transformation, and formal recognition systems.

This does not mean Funda must be accredited before launching. But it does mean Funda should understand the pathway.

## 8.2 SETA landscape

Sector Education and Training Authorities support vocational skills development in South Africa. SETAs are linked to sectors and are involved in learnerships, internships, unit-based skills programmes, and apprenticeships.

Source:
- SETA overview: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sector_Education_and_Training_Authority

## 8.3 Accreditation reality

There are several different levels of credibility:

| Level | What it means | MVP relevance |
|---|---|---|
| Internal Funda certificate | Certificate issued by Funda after competency assessment | Best for MVP |
| Employer-recognized certificate | Pilot company recognizes completion internally | Strong for first pilots |
| CPD recognition | Professional body recognizes learning hours | Good Phase 2 |
| SETA/QCTO-aligned skills programme | Formal alignment/accreditation pathway | Longer-term |
| SAQA/NQF qualification | Nationally registered qualification | Not MVP |

The South African Qualifications Authority oversees the National Qualifications Framework and quality-assurance structures. Formal NQF/SAQA alignment is a longer and more complex path than launching an MVP.

Source:
- SAQA overview: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_African_Qualifications_Authority

## 8.4 Recommendation

Do **not** delay the MVP for accreditation.

### Phase 1

- Launch with a Funda certificate.
- Tie certificate to capstone and rubric.
- Provide completion report to employer.
- Use pilot testimonials to build credibility.

### Phase 2

- Explore CPD partnerships.
- Explore professional bodies by sector.
- Explore SETA/QCTO skills programme route.
- Explore partnership with an already accredited training provider.

### Phase 3

- Consider formal accreditation if demand justifies cost and complexity.

## 8.5 Best shortcut

Partner with an existing accredited South African training provider or professional body rather than trying to build the full accreditation pathway from scratch.

Possible partners to explore:

- business schools,
- HR professional bodies,
- ICT sector bodies,
- public administration training providers,
- SETA-accredited skills providers,
- universities,
- professional associations.

# 9. Procurement strategy

## 9.1 Corporate procurement

Corporate training purchases usually involve:

1. Need identified by HR, IT, business unit, or leadership.
2. Internal champion finds vendor.
3. Vendor sends proposal.
4. Budget owner reviews.
5. Procurement may request vendor registration.
6. Legal/procurement reviews terms.
7. Pilot or purchase order issued.
8. Training delivered.
9. Completion/reporting reviewed.

## 9.2 Government procurement

Government procurement is likely more complex and slower.

Likely requirements:

- supplier registration,
- tax compliance,
- B-BBEE documentation,
- quotations or tender process,
- scope of work,
- pricing schedule,
- procurement compliance,
- proof of capability,
- references,
- possibly local content or transformation considerations.

## 9.3 Funda’s practical procurement strategy

For early stage, avoid starting with a full government tender unless there is an insider champion.

Better path:

1. Corporate pilot with faster procurement.
2. Public entity or NGO pilot through small training budget.
3. Government workshop through an innovation/digital-skills unit.
4. Larger government procurement only after case studies and references.

## 9.4 Procurement-ready assets Funda needs

Create these before selling:

- one-page capability statement,
- company profile,
- course brochure,
- facilitator bios,
- pricing sheet,
- sample agenda,
- certificate sample,
- assessment rubric,
- data/privacy statement,
- references/testimonials once available,
- proposal template,
- pilot offer letter,
- invoice/payment terms,
- B-BBEE status if applicable,
- tax/vendor registration documents if operating in South Africa.

# 10. Pricing strategy

## 10.1 Pricing problem

Exact South African AI training pricing requires direct competitor quotes and buyer interviews. Public pricing is often hidden, negotiable, or customized.

So this section gives pricing hypotheses, not final prices.

## 10.2 Price anchors

Funda sits between:

- free vendor training,
- low-cost MOOCs,
- paid short courses,
- corporate workshops,
- custom consulting.

Therefore, the price cannot be justified by content alone. It must be justified by:

- facilitation,
- customization,
- assessment,
- manager toolkit,
- risk reduction,
- reporting,
- local case studies,
- pilot report,
- organizational outputs.

## 10.3 Recommended MVP pricing models

### Option A: Pilot cohort package

Best first offer.

Structure:

- 15–25 learners,
- 4 weeks,
- 4 live sessions,
- capstone,
- certificate,
- pilot report,
- manager debrief.

Sell as a package, not per video.

### Option B: Per-seat cohort pricing

Good once product is stable.

Useful for:

- companies with variable learner counts,
- mixed cohorts,
- sector association cohorts.

### Option C: Workshop + upsell

Entry product:

- 2-hour AI literacy awareness workshop.

Upsell:

- 4-week core course,
- manager track,
- sector customization.

### Option D: Enterprise package

Later-stage product:

- annual license,
- multiple cohorts,
- manager training,
- templates,
- refresher sessions,
- office hours,
- reporting.

## 10.4 Suggested price positioning

Avoid being the cheapest. Being cheap makes buyers compare Funda to Udemy or YouTube.

Position as:

> More practical than a webinar, more contextual than a MOOC, less expensive than consulting, and more behavior-focused than vendor certification.

## 10.5 Information still needed

Before final pricing, research:

- local AI course pricing,
- corporate L&D spend per employee,
- procurement approval thresholds,
- willingness to pay for facilitation,
- willingness to pay for certification,
- willingness to pay for customization,
- whether buyers prefer per-seat or cohort pricing.

A good first pilot partner has:

- 20–30 office-based staff,

- supportive manager,
- some policy/risk concern,
- enough trust to share feedback,
- not too much bureaucracy,
- ability to schedule live sessions,
- willingness to provide testimonial if successful.

## 11.2 Best pilot sectors

### First choice: ICT / professional services

Why:

- staff already use AI,
- easier to understand value,
- faster procurement,
- more likely to provide feedback,
- good case study for future clients.

### Second choice: NGO / development organization

Why:

- document-heavy work,
- donor reports,
- ethics and community context,
- strong storytelling,
- possible lower budget but good pilot value.

### Third choice: public entity / government-adjacent organization

Why:

- excellent relevance,
- high credibility,
- public-sector use cases,
- but procurement may be slower.

### Fourth choice: finance/insurance

Why:

- high budget and urgency,
- strong compliance need,
- but harder entry and higher risk.

- innovation heads,
- CIO office contacts,

# 12. Sales strategy

## 12.1 Core sales narrative

Use a simple four-part story:

### 1. Reality

Your staff are already using AI.

### 2. Risk

Most do not have a shared standard for what is safe, accurate, ethical, or allowed.

### 3. Gap

Vendor tutorials teach tools, but they do not teach workplace judgment.

### 4. Solution

Funda gives employees the practical frameworks, verification habits, and data-safety behaviors needed to use AI responsibly.

## 12.2 Strongest buyer sentence

> Funda helps organizations move from informal AI experimentation to responsible AI practice.

## 12.3 Sales deck outline

1. The AI adoption problem.
2. Why tool training is not enough.
3. The South African workplace context.
4. What Funda teaches.
5. The Funda Five framework.
6. Course structure.
7. Learner outcomes.
8. Manager toolkit.
9. Pilot offer.
10. Pricing/options.
11. Why now.
12. Next step.

## 12.4 Objection handling

| Objection | Response |
|---|---|
| We already have Microsoft training | Great. Funda complements it by teaching vendor-neutral judgment, verification, and safe workplace use. |
| Staff can learn from YouTube | They can learn tricks, but not shared standards, risk judgment, or accountable practice. |
| We have an AI policy | Policies do not change behavior unless staff know how to apply them. |
| We are not ready for AI | Staff may already be experimenting. Funda can start with safe-use literacy before advanced adoption. |
| We need accreditation | MVP certificate is competency-based; formal accreditation can be explored with partners. |
| We do not have budget | Start with a short awareness workshop or pilot cohort. |
| AI is changing too fast | Exactly why Funda teaches frameworks, not only tools. |
