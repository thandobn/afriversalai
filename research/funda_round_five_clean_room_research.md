# Funda AI Literacy Initiative — Round Five Clean-Room Research Pass

## If We Started With Only the Idea and No Initial Landscape Document

**Purpose:** This Round Five file re-runs the thinking from scratch. It intentionally ignores the assumptions and framing of the original starting document and asks:

> If someone only came with the raw idea — “I want to build an AI literacy course for African or South African professionals” — what would I research, what would I question, and what might I frame differently?

**Core finding:** Without the starting document, I would not begin by writing a broad AI literacy landscape report. I would begin by defining the **specific market problem**: workplace AI use is spreading faster than training, policy, and human judgment.

---

# 1. Clean-room starting point

If you came with only the idea, I would ask:

> What painful, urgent problem does this course solve, for whom, and who pays?

The raw idea might sound like:

> Build an AI literacy course for African professionals.

That is promising, but too broad.

A stronger problem statement is:

> South African professionals are already using AI tools at work, but many lack clear guidance on when to use them, what data is safe, how to verify outputs, and how to remain accountable for AI-assisted work.

That framing is sharper because it creates an actual buyer reason.

---

# 2. How the original starting file may have shaped the research

The original starting file likely biased the work in several ways.

## 2.1 It pushed the framing toward “AI literacy landscape”

Because the starting file was already a landscape report, my earlier work naturally expanded the landscape logic:

- global courses,
- African programs,
- tool taxonomies,
- adult learning research,
- curriculum implications.

That was useful, but it may have overemphasized course design before proving the buyer problem.

## 2.2 It pushed toward “critical AI literacy” as the answer

The file’s core thesis was that the market teaches tools while Funda should teach judgment. That is a strong thesis, but a clean-room pass should still test whether buyers agree that this is the highest-value problem.

Alternative possibilities:

- Buyers may care more about productivity than judgment.
- Learners may care more about confidence than ethics.
- IT may care more about approved-tool adoption than broad AI literacy.
- Government may care more about procurement and data governance than general training.
- Companies may not yet know they need critical AI literacy.

## 2.3 It may have made “Africa” feel like the market too early

The starting report used Africa-wide framing, then narrowed to South Africa. Without that file, I would start with a much tighter Phase 1:

> South African office-based professionals in organizations with active AI adoption pressure.

Then expand later.

## 2.4 It may have made the product feel like a course too early

A clean-room pass suggests Funda might not be primarily a course. It might be:

- a training product,
- a manager toolkit,
- an AI-use policy behavior program,
- a risk-reduction product,
- an adoption/change-management product,
- a sector-specific AI capability program.

The course is the delivery mechanism, not necessarily the product category.

# 3. Clean-room research questions

If starting fresh, I would organize research around five questions.

## Question 1: Who has the pain?

Possible groups:

- individual professionals,
- managers,
- HR/L&D teams,
- CIO/IT leaders,
- compliance/legal/risk teams,
- government departments,
- professional associations.

The most important question is not “who could benefit?”

It is:

> Who feels enough pain to pay, approve, sponsor, or champion this?

## Question 2: What is the pain?

Possible pains:

- staff do not know how to use AI,
- staff use AI unsafely,
- staff use unauthorized AI tools,
- managers cannot guide AI use,
- AI outputs are poor quality,
- employees fear being replaced,
- companies bought Copilot but adoption is weak,
- policy exists but behavior has not changed,
- executives want AI productivity but do not know how to get it,
- government wants AI readiness but fears public trust failures.

## Question 3: What behavior should change?

A course is only valuable if behavior changes.

Desired behavior:

- staff stop pasting sensitive information into public AI tools,
- staff verify AI outputs before using them,
- staff choose appropriate tool classes,
- staff disclose or document AI use when needed,
- managers set norms,
- teams identify safe AI use cases,
- organizations reduce shadow AI risk.

## Question 4: What proof does a buyer need?

Potential proof:

- pre/post confidence increase,
- pre/post risk judgment score,
- capstone quality,
- safe use-case inventory,
- manager feedback,
- policy alignment,
- completion reports,
- testimonials,
- productivity use cases,
- reduction in unsafe behaviors.

## Question 5: What is the smallest useful product?

Instead of building a full course first, test one of these:

1. 90-minute workshop.
2. 4-week pilot cohort.
3. Manager toolkit.
4. AI safe-use bootcamp.
5. Government policy-verification module.
6. POPIA-aware AI use module.
7. Sector-specific pilot for communications/policy teams.

# 5. Clean-room product hypotheses

If starting with no prior document, I would test these product hypotheses.

## Hypothesis A: AI literacy course

A structured learning program for professionals.

Pros:

- easy to understand,
- certificate-friendly,
- scalable.

Cons:

- crowded category,
- compared to cheap/free courses.

## Hypothesis B: Responsible AI workplace adoption program

Training plus manager toolkit plus safe-use frameworks.

Pros:

- stronger buyer value,
- less commoditized,
- connects to governance and risk.

Cons:

- more complex to deliver.

## Hypothesis C: POPIA-aware AI use training

A compliance-adjacent training program.

Pros:

- urgent and specific,
- clear risk angle.

Cons:

- requires legal care,
- may feel too narrow.

## Hypothesis D: AI for public-sector document quality

Specialized government track.

Pros:

- highly contextual,
- strong case study potential.

Cons:

- slower procurement,
- political sensitivity.

## Hypothesis E: AI adoption toolkit for managers

Manager-first product.

Pros:

- adoption multiplier,
- practical,
- strong B2B value.

Cons:

- may need learner course attached.

## Clean-room recommendation

Start with Hypothesis B:

> Responsible AI workplace adoption program.

Then package it as:

- learner course,
- manager toolkit,
- organizational report,
- optional sector module.

# 6. What I would research first without the starting file

I would not start with global AI courses.

I would first research:

1. Are South African professionals already using AI at work?
2. What tools are they using?
3. What unsafe or low-quality behaviors are happening?
4. What do managers and HR teams worry about?
5. Are companies buying AI training?
6. What procurement path exists?
7. What would make them choose a local/contextual provider?
8. What budget range is realistic?
9. What proof of success do they need?
10. What pilot partner can validate the model?

Only after that would I do a full curriculum.

# 7. Clean-room curriculum approach

Without the starting file, I would design the MVP around behavior change, not topics.

## 7.1 Behavior outcomes

By the end, learners should:

1. Know when AI is appropriate.
2. Know when AI is inappropriate.
3. Know what data not to share.
4. Know how to verify outputs.
5. Know how to prompt clearly.
6. Know how to use approved tools.
7. Know when to escalate.
8. Know how to remain accountable.

## 7.2 Course modules

### Module 1: AI at work — what is actually changing?

Goal:

- ground the course in workplace reality, not hype.

### Module 2: The Funda Five

Framework:

1. Task
2. Data
3. Tool
4. Trust
5. Human

### Module 3: Prompting as delegation

Goal:

- teach clear task communication.

### Module 4: Verification and hallucinations

Goal:

- teach checking habits.

### Module 5: Data safety and POPIA-aware practice

Goal:

- teach what can/cannot go into tools.

### Module 6: Workflow redesign and team norms

Goal:

- turn individual learning into organizational practice.

## 7.3 Capstone

Learner redesigns one real work task using AI responsibly.

They submit:

- task description,
- AI use case,
- tool class,
- prompt/workflow,
- data risk rating,
- verification checklist,
- human review step,
- reflection.

# 8. Clean-room differentiation

If starting from scratch, I would define Funda’s differentiation as:

## 8.1 Not another prompt course

Prompting matters, but it should not be the brand.

## 8.2 Not a developer program

Funda is for people who use AI in their professional work, not people building AI systems.

## 8.3 Not vendor training

Funda should help people use Microsoft, Google, OpenAI, Anthropic, or future tools better, but should not depend on one vendor.

## 8.4 Not generic ethics

Ethics should be practical:

- Can I paste this?
- Should I use AI here?
- Who checks this?
- Could someone be harmed?
- Is this output fair?
- What evidence supports this?

## 8.5 Not just a certificate

The certificate must mean the learner demonstrated competency.

# 10. What might be missing from all earlier research

## 10.1 Direct South African pricing data

Earlier work gives pricing logic but not enough actual numbers.

Need:

- quotes from providers,
- public course prices,
- workshop rates,
- corporate training package estimates.

## 10.2 Procurement details

Need:

- how HR buys training,
- approval thresholds,
- vendor registration requirements,
- government tender implications,
- payment timelines.

## 10.3 Local competitor names

Need a mapped list of specific South African training providers offering AI training, digital skills, Copilot training, data literacy, or responsible AI workshops.

## 10.4 Actual learner language

Need real quotes from learners.

Examples of what we need to hear:

- “I use ChatGPT but only for emails.”
- “I’m scared to paste company documents.”
- “My boss said use AI but gave no rules.”
- “I don’t trust it.”
- “I don’t want to look behind.”
- “I want examples from my actual job.”

## 10.5 Buyer urgency

Need to know whether buyers feel this is urgent now or merely interesting.

## 10.6 Accreditation importance

Need to validate whether accreditation is a dealbreaker or a nice-to-have.

## 10.7 Delivery preference

Need to know whether buyers want:

- 2-hour workshop,
- 1-day training,
- 4-week cohort,
- self-paced LMS,
- blended,
- manager-only session.

## Week 1: Problem validation

- Interview 10 professionals.
- Interview 3 managers.
- Interview 3 HR/L&D buyers.
- Ask about current AI use, risk, fear, policy, and training needs.

## Week 2: Buyer and market validation

- Interview 5 buyers.
- Collect 5 competitor pricing examples.
- Identify 20 pilot prospects.
- Test three positioning statements.

## Week 3: Product prototype

- Build one 90-minute workshop.
- Build one case study.
- Build one learner workbook sample.
- Build one manager toolkit sample.

## Week 4: Pilot sales

- Pitch 5 organizations.
- Try to secure one pilot.
- Revise offer based on objections.

# 14. What I would avoid

If starting from scratch, I would avoid:

- writing a huge course before buyer validation,
- building a custom LMS too early,
- pursuing accreditation before pilot demand,
- trying to serve all of Africa at once,
- making the course mostly about prompt engineering,
- over-indexing on tool lists,
- sounding too academic,
- selling only to individual learners,
- promising legal compliance,
- promising productivity gains without measurement.

# 15. Final clean-room conclusion

If there had been no starting document, I would still arrive at a similar broad opportunity, but with a different emphasis.

Earlier framing:

> There is a gap in AI literacy education for African professionals.

Clean-room framing:

> Organizations are facing unmanaged workplace AI adoption, and they need staff who can use AI safely, critically, and accountably.

That second framing is stronger for building a real product.

The next best step is not more broad research. It is:

1. buyer interviews,
2. learner interviews,
3. competitor pricing research,
4. pilot partner outreach,
5. a small prototype workshop.

The highest-risk assumption is not that AI literacy matters.

The highest-risk assumption is that South African organizations will pay Funda specifically to solve this problem.

That is what Round Six should test.
