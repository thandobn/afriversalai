## Infrastructure Realities

Any program that ignores these will fail where it matters most.

**Connectivity:**
- Large portions of rural Africa lack reliable internet — many regions have 2G/3G at best with intermittent access
- Urban/rural divide is dramatic: urban African learners may have reasonable connectivity; rural learners may not
- Always-on connectivity assumptions make programs inaccessible to the people who need them most
- **Design implication:** offline-capable content is not optional for a truly African-serving program

**Devices:**
- Smartphones (especially low-end Android) are the primary computing device for most Africans — not laptops or desktops
- Desktop-first course design will have dramatic drop-off in African contexts
- Screen sizes, processor speeds, and storage on entry-level Android devices are dramatically different from developer machines
- **Design implication:** every interaction must be designed and tested on a low-end Android phone at 3G speeds

**Electricity:**
- Power reliability is uneven across the continent — South Africa has load-shedding, many rural areas have no consistent electricity
- Content design must account for interrupted sessions (auto-save, resume capability)
- Mobile-only learners charge phones when possible, not necessarily when needed

**Data costs:**
- Africa has some of the highest data costs globally relative to average income
- 1GB of mobile data in many African countries costs a significantly higher % of daily income than in the US/Europe
- Heavy video-first designs impose a cost-based exclusion that bars lower-income learners
- **Design implication:** text-heavy, compressed media, offline downloads — not YouTube-embed-everything

**Technology gap root causes (UNCTAD):**
The technology gap in Africa is rooted in structural factors:
1. Weak institutions
2. Skills deficits
3. Inadequate infrastructure
4. Limited financing
5. Missing technology clusters (concentration of expertise/companies/investment in very few cities)

Sustainable programs address structural causes, not just symptoms. A training program that doesn't also advocate for or connect to policy and infrastructure improvements is working around the problem, not through it.

## Digital Divide & Inclusion

**Women:**
- 2.3 billion women globally lack internet access; rates are highest in Africa
- Without explicit female participation targets AND gender-responsive design, programs default to male-dominated outcomes
- This is not about discrimination — it's about structural access barriers (economic control, social norms, safety in tech spaces, family decision-making about who gets training)
- **From STRYDE evidence:** 52% female participation was only achieved because explicit targets were set AND specific strategies were deployed: door-to-door recruitment, gender safe spaces, targeting women's existing community structures, female staff

**Rural vs. urban:**
- ~70% of African youth remain in rural areas
- Urban-centered programs (built around city offices, city internet speeds, city literacy assumptions) miss the majority
- Rural learners often have stronger community networks and motivation — they're not harder to reach, they just require different outreach strategies

**Youth demographic:**
- Africa is the world's youngest continent (median age ~19)
- Programs designed for mid-career professionals in Europe need significant redesign for an African youth context
- Many African learners are the first in their families to access professional training

**Literacy and prior knowledge:**
- Programs must not assume prior familiarity with digital tools beyond basic smartphone use
- Many target learners will never have used a laptop; they may not have used email regularly
- AI literacy programs that assume prior coding knowledge, prior data literacy, or prior Western corporate experience will lose most of their target audience immediately

## What Works in African Skills Programs (STRYDE Evidence)

STRYDE (Skills and Rural Transformation for Youth Development & Entrepreneurship) — TechnoServe program in East Africa (Kenya, Uganda, Tanzania, Rwanda), 2011-2019, 68,000+ youth trained.

This is the most rigorously evaluated large-scale youth skills program in Sub-Saharan Africa. Its lessons are directly applicable to Funda.

**Word-of-mouth recruitment is most effective.**
Community trust networks outperform advertising. Successful alumni are the best recruiters. People join programs their neighbors recommend, not programs they see on billboards or social media.

**Aftercare is not optional — it's the program.**
Training alone produces weak, short-lived outcomes. Sustained follow-up support (minimum 9 months post-training) significantly improves income retention and skill application. Think of training as the beginning, not the product.

**Diversification over specialization.**
Teaching multiple skills and income pathways produces more resilient outcomes than single-skill training. Applied to AI: don't just train people to use one tool — train them to reason about AI broadly.

**Master Trainer model — train the trainers.**
Programs that train facilitators from within communities are dramatically more sustainable and culturally resonant than importing external instructors. The Master Trainer model also creates local career pathways and community ownership.
- **Cost comparison**: $6/student via trained-trainer/partnership model vs $34/student for direct delivery. Scale mathematics favor the Master Trainer approach dramatically.

**Partnership model for sustainability.**
Embedding curriculum in existing institutions (government colleges, NGOs, vocational schools) is far more sustainable than standalone direct delivery. You're building on existing trust, infrastructure, and relationships.

**Government partnership from day one.**
Programs that engage local government authorities early gain:
- Legitimacy in communities
- Policy integration
- Potential for sustainable funding beyond donor cycles
- Protection during political transitions

**Women-specific design features (not just targets).**
Hitting a 50% female target requires actual programmatic changes:
- Gender safe spaces (women-only sessions, especially for initial modules)
- Female staff in delivery roles
- Targeted mobilization in women's existing community structures (savings groups, market associations)
- Addressing household-level resistance directly (involve families, frame as economic benefit)

## Decolonial Technology Critique

This is not just academic — it has direct design implications.

**Digital hegemony from the Global North:**
Technologies originate in the US, Europe, and China with assumptions, epistemologies, and power relationships embedded in their design. Deploying them in Africa without critical reflection doesn't just fail to liberate — it can actively replicate colonial knowledge structures.

**The key question for every Funda module:**
Does this module teach Africans to use technology built elsewhere, or does it build capacity for Africans to understand, critique, and eventually shape technology on their own terms?

**Freire's warning applied to AI:**
Education as banking — depositing Western AI knowledge into African minds — is domestication, not liberation. It produces people who know how to use tools others built, for purposes others defined, on terms others control. Problem-posing education that starts from learners' questions and realities produces people with genuine agency.

**Indigenous Knowledge Systems:**
African ways of knowing are not inferior to Western epistemologies. They are different — often more grounded in community, relationships, and context than in individual optimization and efficiency maximization. Programs that treat African contexts only as problems to be solved by AI miss the value of existing African knowledge.

**Gonye & Moyo (Zimbabwe):**
Research specifically on AI and digital education in Zimbabwe. Key finding: AI education that doesn't account for African epistemologies reproduces colonial knowledge hierarchies even when the people delivering it have good intentions. The curriculum must be genuinely African-centered, not just translated.

## Program Sustainability

**The donor dependency trap:**
Programs that can only survive with external donor funding are fragile. External funding can become leverage for external control. A sustainable model must have a path to financial independence — which is why the B2B corporate/government revenue model for Funda matters.

**Local ownership as the exit strategy:**
The program should be building African capacity to run itself. Not "we'll run this program in Africa" but "we'll build the program, train the trainers, build the institution, then transfer." In years 3-5, Funda should be running primarily through local facilitators and institutions.

**Government integration:**
Programs embedded in government curricula or policy frameworks survive leadership changes and funding cycles. Government partners are slow to acquire but durable once secured.

## Country Context (Quick Reference)

From Atlantic Council 5-country analysis (2023):

| Country | AI Policy Maturity | Key Context |
|---------|-------------------|-------------|
| Ghana | Most advanced digital governance in the study | Digital Rights Regulation 2023, National AI Strategy, proactive regulatory stance |
| South Africa | Leads responsible AI rankings; national strategy still developing | POPIA data law, strong civil society, established tech sector in Johannesburg/Cape Town |
| Nigeria | Leveraging youth demographic; enforcement gaps | Largest African economy by GDP; Lagos as continental tech hub; 2023 NDPC enforcement beginning |
| Kenya | Strong fintech/digital infrastructure foundation | M-PESA heritage; Nairobi as East African tech hub; existing digital ID infrastructure |
| Zambia | Fragmented governance; early stage | Limited existing AI policy; strong opportunity for ground-up program design |

**Key cross-cutting observation:**
AI governance is fragmented at every level — continental AU frameworks, regional bodies (AfCFTA, COMESA), and national strategies often don't align. Programs operating across countries must navigate this. The safest strategy is to engage national governments directly and treat continental frameworks as credibility context, not operational infrastructure.
