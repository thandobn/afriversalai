## Bloom's Taxonomy (Revised) — Learning Objective Levels

The cognitive ladder for learning objectives. Each level requires mastery of the level below.

```
CREATE       ← Producing new work: design a course, build a system, write a policy
EVALUATE     ← Making judgments: critique an AI output, assess reliability, defend a position
ANALYZE      ← Breaking down: identify bias in a model, compare approaches, distinguish claims
APPLY        ← Using knowledge: use a tool correctly, run a prompt, implement a process
UNDERSTAND   ← Explaining: describe what AI does, explain how it works
REMEMBER     ← Recalling: define "machine learning," list AI use cases
```

**Critical insight for AI literacy courses:** Most corporate training programs top out at REMEMBER and UNDERSTAND ("what is AI", "what are the types of AI"). Real empowerment starts at APPLY and EVALUATE. A learner who can describe AI bias (UNDERSTAND) but not identify it in a specific AI output in their job (ANALYZE) is not equipped to work safely with AI.

**Funda design rule:** Every learning objective must be written at APPLY level or above for core outcomes. REMEMBER and UNDERSTAND are scaffolding, not end states.

**Diagnostic verbs by level:**
- Remember: define, list, recall, name, identify
- Understand: explain, describe, summarize, classify, interpret
- Apply: use, execute, implement, demonstrate, solve
- Analyze: differentiate, compare, examine, break down, distinguish
- Evaluate: assess, critique, judge, justify, defend, weigh
- Create: design, produce, construct, develop, formulate

## Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle

Learning as a cycle, not a sequence. All four stages must be present.

```
1. CONCRETE EXPERIENCE      → Doing something real (try a task, get a result)
2. REFLECTIVE OBSERVATION   → Thinking about what happened (what worked, what didn't, why)
3. ABSTRACT CONCEPTUALIZATION → Building the theory from the reflection (naming the principle)
4. ACTIVE EXPERIMENTATION   → Testing the theory in a new situation
```

**The common mistake:** Most training programs do stages 3 and 4 only — teach theory, then practice. They skip 1 and 2. But theory without experience to anchor it doesn't stick. The concrete experience gives the learner something real to reflect on; the reflection is what makes the theory meaningful rather than abstract.

**Funda design rule:** Every module starts with a concrete experience drawn from learners' actual professional contexts — not a hypothetical or a US corporate example. Reflection is scaffolded (journal prompts, peer discussion) not assumed.

## Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs (Applied to Learning)

Learning is a higher-order need. It cannot happen when lower-order needs are unmet.

```
Self-actualization (growth, purpose)
Esteem (confidence, recognition)
Belonging (acceptance, community)      ← Learning requires this
Safety (security, economic stability)  ← Learning requires this
Physiological (food, shelter, health)  ← Learning requires this
```

**Relevance to Africa:**
- Economic precarity is common among target learners — stress about income actively blocks cognitive engagement
- Social belonging matters especially for women and rural learners entering tech-coded environments (historically male, urban)
- Programs must create psychological safety explicitly — not assume it

**Funda design rule:** Don't treat learning as purely cognitive. Course design must build belonging (peer cohorts, community, representative facilitators), not just deliver content.

## Freire's Pedagogy of the Oppressed — Banking vs. Problem-Posing

Paulo Freire's foundational critique of oppressive education.

**Banking model (what to avoid):**
- Learner = passive vessel; teacher = depositor of knowledge
- Knowledge is predetermined, fixed, external to learner
- Learners "receive, memorize, and repeat"
- Produces compliant consumers of existing knowledge, not critical thinkers
- Applied to AI: teaches people to use AI correctly, not to question whether they should, or what the AI is doing to them

**Problem-posing model (what to build):**
- Education as dialogue — learner and teacher both think together
- Starts from learners' own questions, lived realities, and problems
- Knowledge is produced in the encounter, not deposited
- Produces people with genuine agency — they can act on the world, not just in it
- Applied to AI: builds people who can interrogate AI systems, understand whose interests they serve, and decide whether and how to use them

**The Funda test:** Is the curriculum asking "what does the AI industry want you to know?" or "what do you need to know to serve your community, do your job well, and stay in control of decisions that matter to you?"

## Critical Digital Pedagogy (Köseoğlu et al.)

Digital technologies are not neutral. They carry embedded power structures, assumptions, and epistemologies from the cultures that produced them.

**Key claims:**
- Technologies designed in the Global North embed Global North assumptions about work, knowledge, authority, and relationships
- Deploying these technologies in other contexts without critical examination reproduces those power structures
- Digital literacy that doesn't include critique is not real literacy — it's compliance training
- True digital empowerment requires learners to see, name, and act on the power dynamics embedded in the tools they use

**Applied to AI education:**
- Who built these AI systems, and whose problems were they designed to solve?
- What data were they trained on, and whose lives does that data reflect?
- When an AI makes a recommendation, who benefits from that recommendation?
- What happens when an AI trained on Global North data is applied to African contexts?

**Funda design rule:** Every module includes at least one "interrogate the tool" moment — where learners are asked to examine a specific AI output critically, not just evaluate whether it's "right."

## Gonye & Moyo — Decolonial Digital Pedagogy (Zimbabwe Context)

Research specifically on AI and digital education in Zimbabwe/African contexts.

**Core argument:** AI education in Africa that doesn't account for African epistemologies and Indigenous Knowledge Systems reproduces colonial knowledge structures — even when well-intentioned.

**Key insights:**
- African learners are not "behind" Western learners — they have different knowledge contexts, not deficient ones
- Indigenous Knowledge Systems (IKS) are not obstacles to overcome; they are legitimate ways of knowing that AI education should be in conversation with
- Curriculum that treats African realities only as "problems AI can solve" while importing solutions from outside misses the depth of existing African knowledge
- The question of whose AI and who it serves is more acute in African contexts than in Western ones

**Applied to Funda:**
- Examples and case studies must be genuinely African — not tokenistic
- Module design should ask "how does this relate to what learners already know and do" before asking "here's what AI can do for you"
- Facilitate conversations about what AI means in relation to African professional practices, not just in addition to them

## Bates — Teaching in a Digital Age

Practical instructional design framework for technology-mediated learning.

**ADDIE Model:**
```
ANALYZE    → Who are the learners? What do they need? What context do they learn in?
DESIGN     → What are the learning objectives? How will we assess them? What sequence?
DEVELOP    → Build the content, activities, and assessments
IMPLEMENT  → Deliver to learners with appropriate support
EVALUATE   → Did learners achieve the objectives? What needs to change?
```

**Key Bates principles for Funda:**

1. **Needs analysis is not optional.** Designing before you understand your learners produces a course designed for the designer, not the learner.

2. **Instructor presence matters.** Self-paced online content alone has high dropout rates. Human touchpoints — facilitator check-ins, peer learning groups, Q&A sessions — dramatically improve completion and learning quality.

3. **Workload realism.** Working professionals have limited time. A course that claims "2 hours/week" but actually requires 5 will fail. Estimate honestly and design accordingly.

4. **Quality = building skills for the digital age.** Not just content delivery — building capability that transfers to learners' actual situations.

5. **Mobile-first design in African contexts:** This is not just a nice-to-have. Content must be designed to work on low-end Android smartphones at 3G speeds or below, with intermittent connectivity, and ideally with offline capability for key content.

## Synthesis: Funda's Pedagogical DNA

The frameworks above point in the same direction. Funda's pedagogical approach combines:

| Framework | What It Contributes |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Bloom's Taxonomy | Learning objectives set at APPLY/EVALUATE, not REMEMBER/UNDERSTAND |
| Kolb's Cycle | Every module built around experience → reflection → theory → experiment |
| Maslow | Course design creates belonging and safety, not just delivers content |
| Freire | Curriculum starts from learner questions, builds critical agency not compliance |
| Critical Digital Pedagogy | Every module interrogates the tool, not just instructs on the tool |
| Gonye & Moyo | African epistemologies centered, not colonized |
| Bates | Instructor presence, mobile-first design, realistic workloads, aligned assessments |
