# Funda AI Literacy Initiative — Deep Dive Research Part Two

**Purpose:** This is the second-layer research pack for shaping Funda into a high-quality, differentiated AI literacy course for South African corporate and government professionals. It goes deeper than the first landscape scan by turning the research into course-design decisions, validation questions, curriculum architecture, assessment ideas, buyer positioning, and research tasks.

**Core thesis:** Funda should not compete as another “learn ChatGPT in 30 days” product. It should become a **critical AI literacy and workplace judgment program**: a course that helps professionals decide when to use AI, which tool class to use, how to verify outputs, how to protect data, how to avoid bias, and how to redesign work responsibly.

---

## 1. What the starting document already gets right

The starting landscape report is directionally strong. It identifies the main gap clearly:

- Existing programs mostly teach tool use, prompting, productivity tricks, or developer pathways.
- The underserved learner is the non-technical professional who is being told to “use AI” at work but has no framework for trust, verification, risk, data privacy, or organizational judgment.
- The likely strongest entry market is B2B: corporate and government training, especially in South Africa as the first market.
- The MVP should be desktop/laptop-first for office-based learners, while being designed so a lower-bandwidth/mobile version can come later.
- The course should use ADDIE: Analysis → Design → Develop → Implement → Evaluate.

The strongest sentence from the current positioning is essentially:

> The market teaches people to use specific tools. Funda should teach people to think well about AI.

That is the strategic spine. Everything else should support that.

## 2. Updated external context: why this is urgent now

### 2.1 South Africa is becoming an AI-skilling battleground

Microsoft announced a plan to train **1 million South Africans in AI and cybersecurity skills by 2026**, aimed at companies, government, youth, and industry-recognized certification pathways. This matters because it validates market demand but also creates a positioning challenge: Funda cannot win by being “Microsoft training but smaller.” Funda needs to win by being more contextual, more critical, more practical, and more vendor-neutral.

**Implication for Funda:**  
Position Microsoft, Google, IBM, Coursera, and LinkedIn Learning as useful infrastructure, not enemies. Funda’s differentiator is the layer above tools: **judgment, context, governance, and workplace application.**

### 2.2 South Africa’s withdrawn AI policy is a perfect teaching case

In 2026, South Africa withdrew its draft national AI policy after it was found to contain fake or AI-generated citations. The minister described the failure as a credibility and oversight issue and emphasized the need for human oversight in AI use. A revised draft is expected to go through a more careful process.

This is not just policy news. It is the perfect Funda case study because it shows that AI literacy is not abstract. A public institution can suffer reputational damage because people used AI outputs without verification.

**Implication for Funda:**  
Build a full case exercise called **“The Citation Failure: What went wrong?”** Learners analyze a mock policy memo with fake citations, identify red flags, create a verification workflow, and write a short “responsible AI use protocol” for their department.

### 2.3 AI literacy is becoming a compliance issue, not just a skills issue

The EU AI Act includes phased obligations and has pushed “AI literacy” into regulatory conversations. Even though South Africa is not under the EU AI Act, multinational firms, financial institutions, legal firms, consulting firms, public-sector partners, and exporters may increasingly treat AI literacy as part of risk management.

**Implication for Funda:**  
Do not frame the program only as productivity training. Frame it as:

1. workforce readiness,
2. responsible AI adoption,
3. data/privacy risk reduction,
4. AI governance enablement,

### 2.4 AI adoption is happening faster than formal training

Recent workplace surveys and reporting repeatedly show a pattern: workers are already using AI, but organizations often lack policies, training, workflow redesign, and clear accountability. This creates unmanaged risk: sensitive data pasted into tools, false outputs reused, poor-quality “AI slop” circulating internally, and employees overestimating what AI can do.

**Implication for Funda:**  
The learner problem is not “people do not use AI.” The sharper problem is:

> People are already using AI informally, unevenly, and without shared standards.

That is a much stronger buyer pitch.

Useful framing for sales conversations:

- “Your staff are probably already using AI. The question is whether they are using it safely, effectively, and consistently.”
- “Funda turns shadow AI use into governed, useful, measurable AI practice.”

## 4. Deeper learner research: who exactly is Funda for?

The current persona “Nomvula” is a strong start. The next step is to split the learner base into more precise segments.

### 4.1 Primary learner segments

#### Segment A: Office professional asked to “use AI”

**Examples:** communications officer, HR coordinator, analyst, executive assistant, policy officer, program officer, finance associate, operations coordinator.

**Pain points:**
- “I know AI can help, but I do not know where to start.”
- “I can get an answer, but I do not know if it is right.”
- “I am scared of looking incompetent.”
- “I do not know what I am allowed to paste into AI tools.”
- “I can summarize emails, but I do not know how to use AI for real work.”

**Course needs:**
- confidence,
- practical workflows,
- verification habits,
- data/privacy boundaries,
- judgment about when not to use AI.

#### Segment B: Manager responsible for AI adoption

**Examples:** team lead, department head, HR/L&D manager, operations manager, public-sector director.

**Pain points:**
- “My team is using AI inconsistently.”
- “I do not know what policy we need.”
- “I need productivity gains but cannot afford mistakes.”
- “I need to know how to coach my team.”
- “I need to identify safe use cases.”

**Course needs:**
- governance basics,
- team norms,
- use-case prioritization,
- risk matrix,
- change management,
- coaching playbook.

#### Segment C: Public-sector professional

**Examples:** ministry official, municipal staff member, policy analyst, procurement officer, service-delivery coordinator.

**Pain points:**
- public trust,
- misinformation,
- procurement risk,
- policy drafting risk,
- citizen data,
- transparency expectations.

**Course needs:**
- citation verification,
- public accountability,
- bias and fairness,
- procurement of AI tools,
- document review workflows,
- transparent use of AI in public communications.

#### Segment D: High-stakes professional

**Examples:** finance, legal, healthcare administration, insurance, compliance, education leadership.

**Pain points:**
- mistakes have consequences,
- confidentiality,
- regulatory exposure,
- audit trails,
- professional liability.

**Course needs:**
- when not to use AI,
- human-in-the-loop review,
- documentation,
- escalation rules,
- data governance,
- hallucination testing.

### 4.2 Learner assumptions to validate immediately

The research should not assume these are true. It should test them.

1. Do target learners already use ChatGPT, Copilot, Gemini, Claude, or WhatsApp-based AI tools?
2. Are they using personal accounts or approved company tools?
3. What tasks do they already delegate to AI?
4. What mistakes have they seen AI make?
5. What are they afraid of: job loss, looking foolish, privacy, mistakes, ethics, management expectations?
6. How much time would they realistically spend on training?
7. Do they prefer live workshops, self-paced lessons, cohort learning, or workplace challenges?
8. What proof/certificate would matter to them?
9. What language do they actually prefer for learning?
10. What devices do they use outside office hours?

## 6. Curriculum architecture: recommended MVP

### 6.1 Course model

Best MVP format:

**Blended 4-week core track**
- Weekly 90-minute facilitated session.
- 30–45 minutes self-paced prep per week.
- Short workplace exercise after each module.
- Final practical capstone.
- Certificate tied to demonstrated competency.

Why blended?
- Pure self-paced courses have weak completion and transfer.
- One-day workshops create excitement but little behavior change.
- A 4-week model gives time to practice, reflect, and change habits.
- Corporate buyers can schedule it without too much disruption.

### 6.2 MVP course title options

1. **AI Literacy for Work: Judgment, Tools, and Trust**
2. **Responsible AI for African Professionals**
3. **Funda AI Literacy Core**
4. **AI at Work: Practical Literacy for Professionals**
5. **Using AI Without Losing Judgment**

Recommendation:  
Use **Funda AI Literacy Core** as the product name, and subtitle it:

> Practical AI judgment for African workplaces.

### 6.3 Module structure

#### Module 1 — What AI is, what it is not, and why it fails

**Purpose:** Build accurate mental models without getting too technical.

Learners should understand:
- AI is pattern-based, not truly understanding in a human sense.
- Generative AI predicts plausible outputs.
- AI can be useful and wrong at the same time.
- Hallucinations are not rare edge cases; they are a known failure mode.
- Different AI tools do different jobs.

Activities:
- “AI or not AI?” sorting exercise.
- Compare three AI answers to the same question.
- Identify where confidence and correctness diverge.

Assessment:
- Learner explains one AI failure mode in plain language and gives a workplace example.

#### Module 2 — The AI tools map: choosing the right tool class

**Purpose:** Replace tool panic with a durable taxonomy.

Teach tool classes:
- chatbots,
- search/research assistants,
- document tools,
- spreadsheet/data tools,
- image/video/audio tools,
- automation agents,
- coding assistants,
- meeting/email assistants,
- industry-specific AI systems.

Teach decision questions:
1. What is the task?
2. What data is involved?
3. How high-stakes is the output?
4. Does the tool need current information?
5. Does the output need verification?
6. Is the tool approved by my organization?
7. What human review is required?

Activities:
- Tool-selection decision tree.
- “Wrong tool/right tool” scenarios.
- Create a personal AI tool map for one’s job.

Assessment:
- Learner chooses an appropriate tool class for three workplace scenarios and explains why.

#### Module 3 — Prompting as communication, not magic words

**Purpose:** Teach prompting as structured delegation.

Core prompt components:
- role,
- task,
- context,
- source material,
- constraints,
- audience,
- output format,
- verification request,
- limits / what not to do.

Important philosophy:
Prompting is not about secret formulas. It is about clear thinking.

Activities:
- Rewrite weak prompts into strong prompts.
- Prompt an AI to produce a draft, then revise it.
- Create a reusable prompt for a real work task.

Assessment:
- Learner submits a prompt and explains how each part improves output quality.

#### Module 4 — Verification: how to check AI outputs before they matter

**Purpose:** Make critical evaluation the heart of the course.

Teach verification methods:
- source tracing,
- citation checking,
- triangulation,
- fact/date checking,
- internal consistency check,
- bias check,
- missing-stakeholder check,
- numerical sanity check,
- expert escalation,
- red-team review.

Activities:
- Fake citation case study.
- “Find the hallucination” exercise.
- Compare AI summary to original document.
- Create a verification checklist for their role.

Assessment:
- Learner reviews an AI-generated work product, identifies issues, and corrects it.

#### Module 5 — Data, privacy, and ethics in everyday AI use

**Purpose:** Make safe use practical.

Teach:
- public vs private data,
- confidential company data,
- personal information,
- client/customer/citizen data,
- intellectual property,
- bias and fairness,
- transparency and disclosure,
- when to ask permission,
- when not to use AI.

Activities:
- “Can I paste this?” sorting activity.
- Rewrite a risky workflow into a safe workflow.
- Bias detection in hiring/customer-service/policy scenarios.

Assessment:
- Learner creates a safe-use rule for one recurring task.

#### Module 6 — AI in your workflow: from individual use to team practice

**Purpose:** Move from isolated prompting to workplace change.

Teach:
- identify repeated tasks,
- decide automate/augment/avoid,
- design human-in-the-loop workflows,
- create team norms,
- document AI use,
- measure value.

Activities:
- AI use-case canvas.
- Team AI charter.
- Workflow redesign mini-project.
- Manager coaching guide.

Assessment:
- Learner submits a capstone: one AI-assisted workflow with risk rating, verification steps, and human responsibility.

## 7. Assessment strategy

### 7.1 Avoid empty certificates

A certificate should not mean “watched videos.” It should mean:

> This learner can apply AI safely and critically in a workplace scenario.

### 7.2 Competency rubric

Use a 4-level rubric:

| Level | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 — Awareness | Can define the concept but struggles to apply it |
| 2 — Guided application | Can apply with prompts/templates |
| 3 — Independent application | Can apply to new workplace scenarios |
| 4 — Transfer/leadership | Can help others apply it and improve team practice |

### 7.3 Core competencies to assess

1. Explains what generative AI can and cannot do.
2. Chooses an appropriate AI tool class for a task.
3. Writes a structured prompt for a work-relevant task.
4. Evaluates AI output for accuracy, completeness, bias, and appropriateness.
5. Applies data/privacy rules to decide what can be shared with AI.
6. Identifies when AI should not be used.
7. Designs a human-in-the-loop workflow.
8. Communicates AI use transparently when needed.

### 7.4 Sample final capstone

**Task:**  
Choose one recurring work task. Redesign it using AI responsibly.

Learner submits:
- description of task,
- why AI is appropriate or not,
- tool class selected,
- prompt/workflow,
- data risk rating,
- verification checklist,
- human review step,
- final output sample,
- reflection on limitations.

Grading rubric:
- relevance to real work,
- quality of tool choice,
- quality of prompt/workflow,
- risk awareness,
- verification strength,
- clarity of human accountability.

## 8. Pedagogy: what will make the course actually work

### 8.1 Adult learning principles

Adult learners need relevance, respect, practical application, and autonomy. For Funda, this means:

- Use real workplace examples, not toy examples.
- Let learners bring their own tasks.
- Avoid shaming people for not knowing AI.
- Start with quick wins.
- Build toward harder judgment questions.
- Use discussion and reflection.
- Make the course immediately useful.

### 8.2 Problem-posing pedagogy

Instead of saying “Here are 50 AI tools,” start with the learner’s world:

- What tasks drain your time?
- What decisions feel risky?
- Where do errors happen?
- What do you wish you could check faster?
- What should never be delegated?
- What does your organization need to protect?

Then introduce AI frameworks as tools for solving those problems.

### 8.3 Practice design

Every module should include:

1. a short concept,
2. a realistic case,
3. a hands-on exercise,
4. peer discussion,
5. a reflection,
6. a workplace transfer task.

### 8.4 “Confidence first, complexity second”

The course should not start with ethics, doom, or technical architecture. Start with:
- “You can use this.”
- “You can understand the risks.”
- “You do not need to be a programmer.”
- “You already have professional judgment — AI literacy strengthens it.”

Then later introduce risk, governance, hallucination, and bias.

## 10. Tool taxonomy: deeper version for the course

### 10.1 Teach tool classes, not brands

Brands change. Categories endure longer.

| Tool class | Learner question | Main risks | Funda teaching focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chatbots | Can it help me think, draft, summarize? | hallucination, overtrust, privacy | prompting + verification |
| AI search | Can it find current info? | bad sources, search bias | source quality |
| Document AI | Can it summarize policies/contracts? | missing nuance | compare to source |
| Spreadsheet/data AI | Can it find patterns? | false calculations | numerical checks |
| Meeting assistants | Can it summarize meetings? | consent, confidentiality | disclosure and storage |
| Email assistants | Can it draft replies? | tone, privacy | human review |
| Image/video AI | Can it create visuals? | copyright, misrepresentation | disclosure and brand safety |
| Automation agents | Can it perform multi-step tasks? | unintended actions | permissions and oversight |
| Coding assistants | Can it automate scripts? | security, wrong code | testing and review |
| Sector AI tools | Can it support specialized work? | professional liability | escalation and governance |

### 10.2 The Funda tool-decision framework

Use this five-step framework:

1. **Task** — What exactly am I trying to do?
2. **Sensitivity** — What data or people could be affected?
3. **Tool class** — What type of AI system fits this task?
4. **Trust level** — How wrong could the output be, and how would I know?
5. **Human responsibility** — Who checks, approves, or owns the final decision?

Mnemonic: **T-S-T-T-H** is ugly. Better learner-facing name:

> **The Funda Five: Task, Data, Tool, Trust, Human.**

## 11. AI risk framework for non-technical professionals

### 11.1 Simple risk tiers

Teach risk in plain language:

| Risk tier | Example | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| Green | brainstorming, rewriting a non-sensitive paragraph | AI can assist freely with review |
| Yellow | internal report, meeting summary, policy draft | AI can assist but must be checked |
| Orange | client/citizen data, financial/legal implications | Use only approved tools and strict review |
| Red | final decisions about people, confidential data in public tools, medical/legal advice | Do not delegate; escalate or use governed system |

### 11.2 The “should I use AI?” checklist

Before using AI, ask:

1. Would I be comfortable telling my manager/client/citizen I used AI here?
2. Does this involve personal, confidential, or sensitive data?
3. Could someone be harmed if the output is wrong?
4. Do I have the expertise to check the answer?
5. Is there an approved tool for this?
6. Is the output going directly to someone else?
7. Does this require empathy, accountability, or professional judgment?
8. What verification step will I use?

If the learner cannot answer these, pause.

## 19. Evidence-backed design principles

### 19.1 Make AI literacy role-embedded

Generic AI training produces shallow understanding. Learners need to apply AI to the actual documents, decisions, communications, and constraints of their role.

Design rule:
> Every module must include a role-specific adaptation.

### 19.2 Teach evaluation as a habit

Verification should not be a single module learners forget. It should appear every week.

Design rule:
> Every AI output in the course gets checked.

### 19.3 Use real errors

Learners remember failure cases more than abstract warnings.

Design rule:
> Use hallucinated citations, biased recommendations, wrong summaries, missing caveats, and overconfident answers as teaching materials.

### 19.4 Normalize uncertainty

Good AI users are not people who trust AI more. They are people who know how much trust is appropriate.

Design rule:
> Reward learners for saying “I do not know yet; I need to verify.”

### 19.5 Keep technical depth purposeful

Do not overteach transformer architecture. Teach only enough technical background to support better judgment.

Design rule:
> If a technical concept does not change workplace behavior, leave it out of the core course.

## 23. One-page strategic summary

Funda should become the trusted AI literacy course for South African and African organizations that need staff to use AI responsibly, not just enthusiastically. The opportunity exists because AI tools are spreading faster than organizational training, policies, and judgment frameworks. Existing programs are either vendor-led, generic, technical, or tool-specific. Funda’s wedge is a course that is:

- non-technical but rigorous,
- African-contextualized,
- vendor-neutral,
- workplace-based,
- judgment-centered,
- assessment-backed,
- useful for both learners and managers,
- designed for B2B delivery.

The MVP should be a 4-week blended course with a practical capstone and certificate. The most important proof point will be a pilot showing that learners become better at choosing AI use cases, protecting data, verifying outputs, and designing human-in-the-loop workflows.

The next move is not to build the whole course. The next move is to validate the learner and buyer assumptions quickly, then build the Learning Design Brief and one pilot-ready module.
