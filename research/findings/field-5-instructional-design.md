## Core Design Principles

These are not preferences — they are evidence-backed requirements for a program that actually produces learning, not just completion.

---

## 1. Facilitation Is Not Optional

**The evidence is unambiguous:**

| Delivery format | Completion rate | Long-term retention |
|----------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Self-paced online (no facilitator) | 5–15% | Low |
| Self-paced with community support | ~25–35% | Moderate |
| Cohort-based with live facilitator | **70%+** | High |
| Cohort + formative assessments (quizzes, exercises) | **80%+** | High (+25–40% over 6 months) |

Source: 2026 Training Industry benchmark data; Data Society instructor-led training analysis; multiple corporate L&D studies.

**The Funda implication:** Self-paced delivery is not appropriate for the Core Track or any sector track during Phase 1. A live facilitator is a product requirement, not a premium add-on. This is also what the master trainer model (STRYDE evidence) validates for African adult learners specifically — local facilitators dramatically outperform imported remote content.

**Delivery model for Phase 1 pilots:**
- 6 sessions per cohort, 2–3 hours each (total: 12–18 hours of contact time)
- Delivered over 6–8 weeks (one session per week — sustainable for working professionals)
- In-person or live virtual (Zoom/Teams) — not recorded/asynchronous
- Cohort size: 12–20 learners (small enough for real discussion, large enough for group dynamics)
- Facilitator: Thando (pilot phase); Master Trainer model in Year 1 scale

**Between-session work:** No more than 1–2 hours per week (working professionals cannot absorb more). Between-session tasks are application-focused, not reading-focused.

## 1.5. Facilitating the 4 Adopter Segments

Research on technology adoption in organizational settings consistently identifies 4 types of adopters. Every Funda cohort will contain all 4. The facilitator who can only manage one type — usually the enthusiastic majority — will lose the skeptics and enable the early enthusiasts to dominate the room.

| Adopter Type | % of typical cohort | How they show up | Core facilitation approach |
|-------------|--------------------|-----------------|-----------------------------|
| **Early Enthusiast** | 15–20% | Already using AI; excited; wants to share tips; may be overconfident about AI outputs | Channel their energy into the Encounter phase — they're good at generating initial responses. Counter their overconfidence by assigning them the verification tasks. Worst mistake: letting them set the tone, which alienates everyone else. |
| **Curious Majority** | 50–60% | Interested but uncertain; watching what peers think before committing; asks "but how do I know if it's right?" | This is the primary audience. They move on peer examples faster than facilitator explanation. The Reflect phase (Phase 2) is where they engage most. Peer discussion > lecture for this group. |
| **Skeptic** | 20–25% | Raises objections; asks hard questions; "what about jobs?"; "what if it's wrong?"; not opposed — wants evidence | Never argue with skeptics. Give them the frameworks and let them test them. Skeptics who convert become the strongest advocates and the best critical evaluators. Their questions often improve the whole cohort. |
| **Resistant User** | 5–10% | Feels threatened; fears replacement; disengages or complies superficially; may not engage in group exercises | Never challenge or shame in the group. One-on-one conversation during a break or after the session. The "professional identity" frame is the tool here: AI changes how the job is done, not whether the person is needed. Competency 10 (When/When Not) is particularly important for this group — it gives them language for where AI does not belong. |

**Group dynamics rule:** The most dangerous scenario is an Early Enthusiast who talks over skeptics and gets reinforced by the facilitator. This causes silent resistance that shows up as low completion and poor capstone performance. Actively redirect Early Enthusiasts during group discussion: "That's a useful experience — what does someone see this differently?"

**The "professional identity" module:** Based on ChatGPT cross-reference research, there is a recognized content gap for the Resistant User specifically — not a full module, but a framing that runs through every session: *"AI changes what your job involves; it does not invalidate the expertise you've built."* This reframe is most effective when introduced in Session 1 and reinforced in the Encounter phase by letting learners find the AI errors before the facilitator names them.

## 2. The Session Architecture — Kolb Cycle Operationalized

Every session follows the same 4-phase structure. This is Kolb's experiential learning cycle made explicit and consistent. The order is non-negotiable — starting with theory (as most courses do) is the banking model. Starting with experience is Freire and Kolb.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  PHASE 1: ENCOUNTER (20 min) — Concrete Experience              │
│  Give learners something to engage with BEFORE explaining it.   │
│  Artifact: A real AI output (document, recommendation, decision) │
│  Task: "What do you notice? What would you do with this?"        │
│  Facilitator role: Listen, don't correct. Surface instincts.    │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
         ↓
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  PHASE 2: REFLECT (20 min) — Reflective Observation             │
│  Guided questions that surface what just happened.               │
│  "What made you trust/distrust that output?"                    │
│  "Where would you hesitate to act on it?"                       │
│  "Has something like this happened at your job?"                │
│  Facilitator role: Ask, don't tell. Peer discussion.            │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
         ↓
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  PHASE 3: CONCEPT (30 min) — Abstract Conceptualization          │
│  NOW introduce the framework that explains what they experienced. │
│  "What you were sensing is called [bias / hallucination / ...]. │
│  Here is why it happens and why it matters."                    │
│  Facilitator role: Teach the concept. Use SA case studies.       │
│  Content connects to Phase 1 artifact — not generic theory.      │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
         ↓
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  PHASE 4: APPLY (30 min) — Active Experimentation               │
│  New artifact from learner's actual sector.                     │
│  Task: Apply the framework to evaluate it.                      │
│  Individual or small-group work; facilitator circulates.        │
│  "In your role at [type of organization], what would you do?"   │
│  Output: A brief written or verbal judgment, not just a feeling. │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Why the order matters:** Most corporate training programs run Phase 3 → Phase 4 and skip Phases 1 and 2. They give people the theory, then ask them to practice. Kolb shows that the experience and reflection that precede theory are what make the theory memorable and transferable. Without Phase 1, learners are solving a puzzle they haven't seen yet.

**The Freire connection:** Phase 1 starts with what learners already bring — their instincts, their existing experience of AI in their workplace. The facilitator is not depositing knowledge; they are working with learners to understand something together. The learner's confusion in Phase 1 is not a failure to be corrected — it is the raw material for learning.

## 3. SA Case Studies as the Teaching Medium

Every Phase 1 artifact and Phase 3 concept example must be drawn from the Field 2 case study library. This is the instructional design choice that makes Funda structurally different from every competitor.

**Session-to-case-study mapping (Core Track, illustrative):**

| Session | Theme | Phase 1 Artifact | SA Case Study Used |
|---------|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 1 | What AI actually is | AI-generated policy document section | Case 001 (DCDT policy hallucinations) — learners spot problems before knowing why |
| 2 | How AI learns and fails | Credit scoring output for a women-led business | Case 004 (37% gender penalty) — learners see the number before the mechanism |
| 3 | Bias and discrimination | Predictive policing report | Case 002 (SA predictive policing) — learners evaluate the recommendation |
| 4 | Verification and judgment | AI-generated news report (deepfake context) | Case 006 (SA 2024 election deepfakes) — learners fact-check the output |
| 5 | Accountability and governance | HR shortlisting output | Case 007 (Employment Equity Act exposure) — learners identify who is responsible |
| 6 | When AI works well vs. when it doesn't | Dr Math usage scenario vs. clinical AI decision | Cases 009 + 010 — learners apply the full framework |

All artifacts are based on real SA cases — not invented scenarios, not US corporate examples.

## 4. Formative Assessment — The Retention Mechanism

**Evidence:** Learners who receive biweekly formative assessments retain 30% more over 6 months than those who don't. Adding structured exercises to live sessions increases retention by 25–40 percentage points.

**Funda's formative assessment design:**

| Touchpoint | Format | Purpose | Frequency |
|-----------|--------|---------|-----------|
| Session warm-up question | 1 question, open text or verbal | Surface prior knowledge, reveal misconceptions for facilitator | Each session |
| End-of-session reflection | 3 prompts: What did I notice? What surprised me? What will I do differently? | Consolidate Phase 2 (reflective observation) | Each session |
| Between-session task | 1 real-world observation: "Find one AI you encountered this week — write 3 sentences about it using this session's framework" | Active experimentation between formal sessions | Weekly |
| Mid-cohort peer review | Learners review each other's between-session observations | Build community, surface diverse sector perspectives | Session 3 |
| Pre-final session share-back | Each learner presents one workplace AI they observed and evaluated | Formative before the summative; identifies gaps | Session 5 |

None of the formative assessments are scored — they are required but ungraded. This reduces anxiety while maintaining accountability.

## 5. Summative Assessment — Making the Certificate Mean Something

This is Funda's most critical design decision. The certificate's value depends entirely on whether the assessment actually tests AI judgment — not AI awareness.

**What the assessment must NOT be:**
- Multiple choice quiz on AI definitions
- Fill-in-the-blank on the Kolb cycle
- "Have you completed all sessions?" completion tracking

**What the assessment must BE:**
A **performance task** — something that requires the learner to DO something, not answer questions about it.

### Summative Assessment Design: The AI Judgment Portfolio

**Task brief for the learner (delivered in Session 5, submitted after Session 6):**

> Identify one AI system or AI-generated output that you encountered in your professional work or professional context during this course.
>
> Produce a written evaluation (600–900 words) that:
> 1. Describes what the AI system does and how it is being used
> 2. Identifies at least two ways the system could produce biased, incorrect, or unaccountable outputs — with specific reasoning
> 3. Applies the verification framework from this course to assess whether the output should be acted on
> 4. States clearly what you would do — use it, modify how you use it, flag a concern, or recommend against it — and explains why
> 5. Identifies who in your organization is accountable if the AI produces a harmful outcome

**Assessment rubric (illustrative — to be co-designed with African educator):**

| Criterion | Below Standard | Meets Standard | Exceeds Standard |
|----------|---------------|----------------|-----------------|
| Describes AI accurately | Vague or incorrect description of what the system does | Clear, jargon-free description of system function | Shows understanding of how system learns/fails beyond surface description |
| Identifies bias/failure modes | Generic ("AI can be biased") | Two specific mechanisms explained with reasoning | Mechanisms tied to specific SA context and evidence |
| Applies verification framework | Does not apply course framework | Applies framework with reasoning | Extends framework beyond what was taught to the specific context |
| Makes and defends a judgment | No clear position taken | Clear judgment with stated reasoning | Judgment engages with competing considerations and remains defensible |
| Identifies accountability | "The company is responsible" (generic) | Named accountability chain with specific role | Addresses gaps in accountability and suggests remediation |

**Assessor:** Requires a trained human assessor — not automated scoring. This is a cost, but it is the cost of the credential being real. Assessors must be trained in the rubric. Initially: Thando. At scale: trained Master Assessors.

**Pass/fail:** Learner must meet or exceed standard on at least 4 of 5 criteria to receive the certificate. Below standard on any criterion = a specific feedback report and one revision opportunity.

**Certificate wording (draft):**
> "This certificate confirms that [Name] has demonstrated AI judgment competency — the ability to critically evaluate AI systems and their outputs in professional contexts — as assessed against the Funda AI Literacy Standard."

The wording says "demonstrated" not "completed." That distinction is the certificate.

## 6. Master Trainer Model — How Funda Scales Without Losing Quality

Funda cannot scale through Thando alone. The delivery model must transfer.

**The Master Trainer pathway:**

| Stage | Who delivers | When | Requirement |
|-------|-------------|------|------------|
| Pilot | Thando | Year 1 | Thando as primary facilitator; generates delivery evidence |
| First replication | Trained SA facilitator (co-delivery) | Year 1–2 | Facilitator completes full Core Track as learner, then co-facilitates 2 cohorts |
| Independent delivery | Trained Master Trainer | Year 2+ | Independently facilitates with quality review on 20% of sessions |
| Certification of trainers | Funda Facilitator Certification | Year 2+ | Written case study + observed delivery assessment |

**What the Master Trainer training covers:**
- Deep content knowledge (completing the Core Track as a learner is non-negotiable)
- The Kolb/Freire session architecture — not just the content, but the pedagogy
- Facilitation skills specific to AI literacy: surfacing assumptions, managing fear and resistance, working with the "I'm not technical" objection
- SA case study library — facilitators must know the cases deeply enough to adapt them to learner context
- Assessment: trained in the rubric; able to give specific developmental feedback

**African educator requirement:** Per the Mission Integrity Policy and the council's "African-centered claims need proof" finding — at least one African educator or learning designer must co-review the curriculum before it is taught to learners outside a pilot context. The first Master Trainer candidate should ideally serve this role.

## 7. What SA Employers Consider a Meaningful Credential

Research on this is thin for SA specifically, but the adjacent evidence points clearly:

**Current credibility signals for SA L&D buyers (in order):**

1. **SETA accreditation** — the strongest signal. The SDL recovery system makes this a financial decision, not just a quality signal. An SETA-accredited certificate is literally worth money to the buyer.
2. **NQF level alignment** — signals the program has been formally assessed against national standards.
3. **Peer organization endorsement** — "Company X in our sector used Funda and here is what changed."
4. **Facilitator credibility** — the person in the room matters. In African B2B learning, the facilitator IS the brand during the learning experience.
5. **Assessment-backed (not completion-only)** — sophisticated L&D buyers know the difference; they will ask "what does completing this course mean a learner can do?"

**What Funda's certificate needs to be, short-term (before SETA):**
The pilot case study is the short-term credential signal. A named SA organization that ran a Funda cohort and produced documented behavior change is more persuasive than any certification badge to the next buyer.

**The credentialing roadmap:**
- Pilot phase: "Funda AI Literacy Certificate" — backed by the assessment portfolio
- Year 1: Submit for MICT SETA accreditation
- Year 2: SETA-accredited; SDL recoverable; NQF-aligned credential
- Year 3+: Funda certificate recognized in sector as a meaningful signal
