## What "AI Literate" Actually Means

Synthesized from: UNESCO AI Competency Framework (2024), Alan Turing Institute AI Skills for Business Framework, academic review (Tandfonline 2025), Elements of AI curriculum analysis.

**The consensus definition across all frameworks:**

> AI literacy is the knowledge, skills, and attitudes needed to understand what AI systems are, critically evaluate their outputs and limitations, apply them appropriately to real professional tasks, and navigate their ethical and organizational implications — without requiring technical expertise in building them.

Note what this is NOT: "AI literacy is being able to use AI tools." That is tool familiarity, not literacy. The distinction is important for Funda's positioning and is directly what no SA competitor currently delivers.

## Competency Framework — Core Track

Organized by Bloom's Taxonomy level. All 18 outcomes are observable and assessable — each can be demonstrated, not just self-reported.

### DOMAIN 1: Understanding AI (Bloom's 1–2: Remember + Understand)

These are the foundational concepts. Funda does not stop here — but without these, learners cannot engage the higher levels.

**Competency 1 — Demystify AI**
*Learner can explain what AI systems actually do in plain language — pattern recognition from data — without jargon, myths, or science fiction framing.*
- Assessment method: Explain in 2 sentences what an AI system like a credit scoring model "actually does."
- SA relevance: SA professionals often fear AI because they think it is "intelligent" or "thinking." This fear drives avoidance rather than judgment.

**Competency 2 — Map AI Types to Real Applications**
*Learner can identify which category of AI (generative, predictive, optimization, classification) is being used in a given real-world application and explain what it does.*
- Assessment method: Given 5 real SA AI use cases (SARS fraud detection, ChatGPT, facial recognition, credit scoring), correctly classify each type.
- SA relevance: Professionals encounter AI in very different forms. Conflating them prevents judgment.

**Competency 3 — Know What AI Cannot Do**
*Learner can identify at least 3 categories of tasks where AI performs unreliably or fails — and explain why.*
- Assessment method: Given 4 scenarios, correctly identify which tasks are appropriate for AI assistance and which are not.
- Key examples: AI cannot reliably handle novel situations outside its training distribution; AI cannot explain its reasoning; AI cannot guarantee factual accuracy.

**Competency 4 — Understand How AI Learns**
*Learner can explain in conceptual terms (no math required) how machine learning systems learn from training data — and why the quality and representativeness of that data determines the quality and fairness of outputs.*
- Assessment method: Explain why an AI credit scoring system trained on historical SA bank data might produce discriminatory results for women-led SMEs.
- SA relevance: Uses Case 004 (credit scoring gender bias) as the application context.

### DOMAIN 2: Critical Evaluation (Bloom's 4–5: Analyze + Evaluate)

This is Funda's core differentiator. No SA competitor teaches this systematically.

**Competency 5 — Verify Before You Act**
*Learner can apply a structured verification process before acting on AI-generated content — checking claims, identifying fabrications, and knowing when to escalate.*
- Assessment method: Given an AI-generated document with 2 fabricated and 2 accurate claims, identify which are which and explain verification method.
- SA relevance: Uses Case 001 (DCDT policy hallucination scandal) as the anchor case study. Government officials published AI-written policy without verification. This is the professional failure the competency prevents.

**Competency 6 — Identify Bias Patterns**
*Learner can identify at least two mechanisms through which bias enters AI systems and recognize their effects in at least one professional context relevant to their work.*
- Assessment method: Given a case study, identify the specific bias mechanism (biased training data, proxy discrimination, feedback loops) and explain its impact.
- SA relevance: Draws from Cases 002–005 (policing, facial recognition, credit scoring).

**Competency 7 — Evaluate Output Quality**
*Learner can assess whether an AI-generated output (text, summary, recommendation, prediction) is reliable for a specific professional purpose — including identifying signs of hallucination, oversimplification, or context mismatch.*
- Assessment method: Given 3 AI-generated professional outputs (a report summary, a recommendation, a factual claim), evaluate each for reliability and flag issues.
- SA relevance: Applies to any professional using Copilot, ChatGPT, or any generative AI tool.

**Competency 8 — Question the Decision**
*Learner can identify when an AI system is making or influencing a decision that affects people, and ask the right questions about accountability, explainability, and recourse.*
- Assessment method: Given a scenario where an AI system denies a loan/job application/medical referral, identify who is accountable and what questions should be asked.
- SA relevance: Employment Equity Act liability (Case 007); SA banking AI credit decisions (Case 008).

**Competency 9 — Recognize Deepfakes and Synthetic Media**
*Learner can identify signals that media (text, image, audio, video) may be AI-generated and apply a basic verification protocol before sharing or acting on it.*
- Assessment method: Given mixed authentic and AI-generated content samples, correctly flag the AI-generated ones with explanation of signals used.
- SA relevance: Case 006 — SA 2024 election deepfakes. Also relevant to professional contexts: AI-generated emails impersonating executives are an emerging threat.

### DOMAIN 3: Professional Application (Bloom's 3: Apply)

Using AI well — for the right tasks, with the right oversight.

**Competency 10 — The When/When Not Framework**
*Learner can apply a decision framework for when to use AI assistance, when to use human judgment, and when to use neither — and explain their reasoning.*
- Assessment method: Given 6 professional scenarios, correctly classify each as "AI-appropriate," "human judgment required," or "AI inadvisable" with reasoning.
- Teaching note: This is Funda's "AI judgment" competency made concrete and testable. It is not a feeling — it is a structured decision process.

**Competency 11 — Effective Task Delegation to AI**
*Learner can use AI tools effectively for at least two tasks relevant to their professional role — including writing clear prompts, reviewing outputs critically, and integrating AI assistance into their workflow with appropriate verification.*
- Assessment method: Complete a real professional task using AI assistance; document the prompt, the output, what was verified, and what was changed before use.
- SA relevance: Practical — not tool tutorials, but using tools with judgment. The output matters as much as the process.

**Competency 12 — Data Privacy in AI Contexts**
*Learner can identify what data should and should not be shared with AI tools in their professional context, with reference to SA's POPIA (Protection of Personal Information Act) and their organization's data governance requirements.*
- Assessment method: Given 5 professional data scenarios, correctly classify each as POPIA-compliant or at-risk for AI tool input.
- SA relevance: POPIA is SA-specific law. Any professional using ChatGPT, Copilot, or other AI tools with client, patient, or employee data is subject to POPIA requirements.

### DOMAIN 4: Ethics and Governance (Bloom's 4–5: Analyze + Evaluate)

**Competency 13 — Organizational AI Accountability**
*Learner can explain who in their organization is responsible for AI systems that affect people — and identify the chain of accountability for an AI-assisted decision that goes wrong.*
- Assessment method: Given an organizational chart and an AI deployment scenario, map the accountability chain and identify the weakest link.
- SA relevance: "The algorithm decided" is not a legal defense under the Employment Equity Act (Case 007). Someone is accountable.

**Competency 14 — Raise a Substantive AI Concern**
*Learner can raise a meaningful, evidence-backed concern about an AI deployment in their organization using appropriate professional channels.*
- Assessment method: Draft a brief professional communication (memo or email) raising a concern about a specific AI use case, with evidence and a proposed next step.
- Teaching note: This competency is the test for whether a learner has genuine agency or just awareness. Can they act on their judgment? Many AI training programs produce awareness. Funda produces agency.

**Competency 15 — Navigate an Ethical Dilemma**
*Learner can work through an AI-related ethical dilemma in their professional context — identifying the competing values, the stakeholders affected, and a defensible position — without a pre-set "right answer."*
- Assessment method: Given a realistic dilemma (e.g., "Your organization wants to use AI to monitor employee productivity. What do you do?"), produce a structured analysis with a defensible recommendation.
- SA relevance: Uses SA workplace law and norms as context. Dilemmas are drawn from SA professional contexts.

---

### DOMAIN 5: Organizational Navigation (Bloom's 5–6: Evaluate + Create)

These are the advanced competencies — most relevant to managers, team leads, and professionals with organizational influence.

**Competency 16 — Evaluate AI Vendor Claims**
*Learner can critically assess an AI vendor's marketing claims — identifying what's substantiated, what's vague, what's missing, and what questions to ask before recommending purchase.*
- Assessment method: Given a real AI vendor product sheet, identify 3 claims that need substantiation and draft the follow-up questions a responsible buyer should ask.
- SA relevance: SA organizations are actively purchasing AI tools. The person who evaluates them often does not have technical expertise. This competency gives them a framework.

**Competency 17 — Contribute to an AI Governance Discussion**
*Learner can participate meaningfully in their organization's AI policy or governance discussion — understanding the key issues, asking informed questions, and contributing a perspective grounded in their domain expertise.*
- Assessment method: Participate in a simulated AI governance committee discussion and demonstrate understanding of the key tensions (efficiency vs. fairness, speed vs. oversight).
- SA relevance: DCDT's AI policy framework means SA organizations are actively developing AI governance positions. Funda graduates should be able to participate in these conversations.

**Competency 18 — Advocate for Responsible AI in Your Team**
*Learner can identify an AI use pattern in their immediate work environment that warrants attention — and make a constructive case for changing it.*
- Assessment method: Identify one actual or hypothetical AI use in their own work context, evaluate it against the course frameworks, and produce a 1-page recommendation.
- Teaching note: This is the capstone outcome. If a learner can do this, they are genuinely AI literate — not just certified.

## The Funda Five — Proprietary Decision Framework

This is Funda's primary learner-facing IP: a single repeatable decision framework applied to every AI task, in every session, until it becomes automatic. The point is not to replace judgment — it is to externalize the judgment process so learners can run it consistently rather than improvising each time.

Before using AI on any task, work through each of the five:

| Step | Question to ask |
|------|----------------|
| **1. Task** | What exactly am I trying to do? Is this the right kind of job for AI? Am I solving a problem AI is actually good at? |
| **2. Data** | What information does this involve? Is any of it personal, confidential, client/citizen/employee data, or POPIA-protected? |
| **3. Tool** | What type of AI fits this task? Do I have an approved tool for it? Is this tool appropriate for the sensitivity level of the data? |
| **4. Trust** | How reliable is AI for this specific task type? What are its known failure modes here? How would I detect an error if it made one? |
| **5. Human** | Who checks the output before it matters? Who is accountable if the output is wrong or harmful? |

**Facilitation principle:** The Funda Five is introduced in Session 1 and used in every subsequent session. By Session 4, learners should be able to apply it to a new scenario without prompting. The capstone portfolio is an extended demonstration of running the Funda Five on a real workplace AI encounter.

**Why a named framework matters:** Tool knowledge goes stale in 18 months. The Funda Five works regardless of which specific tool a learner is using. It is the durable IP — what learners remember and apply 2 years after the course when the tools have changed.

## Risk Tier — Learner-Facing Task Classification

Used alongside the Funda Five: before starting a task, classify its risk tier. This determines how much human oversight the output requires.

| Tier | Color | Typical task examples | Rule |
|------|-------|----------------------|------|
| **1** | 🟢 Green | Brainstorming, rewriting a non-sensitive draft, generating ideas, summarizing public information | AI can assist freely — review before sharing, but lightweight check |
| **2** | 🟡 Yellow | Internal reports, meeting summaries, policy drafts, internal communications | AI can assist but human must verify accuracy, completeness, and tone |
| **3** | 🟠 Orange | Client/citizen/employee data, anything with financial or legal implications, public communications | Only approved tools; detailed human review required before any action |
| **4** | 🔴 Red | Decisions about specific people, confidential data in public tools, medical or legal conclusions, anything where an error causes harm | Do not delegate to AI; escalate to the appropriate authorized process |

**Teaching note:** Learners often arrive overconfident about Tier 1 and underconfident about what Tier 3-4 means in practice. The "Can I paste this?" activity (Competency 12) is the practical application of the Risk Tier system to POPIA-specific data scenarios.

**Relationship to the Funda Five:** The Risk Tier is answered during steps 2 (Data) and 4 (Trust). Explicitly teach learners to state the tier before proceeding: "This is a Yellow task — I'll use AI to draft, then verify before sending."

## Comparison to Elements of AI

Elements of AI (University of Helsinki, 2M+ users) covers: what is AI, search and problem solving, odds and probability, machine learning types, neural networks basics, societal implications.

**What Funda adds that Elements of AI does not have:**
- Critical evaluation / verification skills (Competencies 5–9)
- SA-specific regulatory context: POPIA, Employment Equity Act (Competency 12, 13)
- SA-specific case studies throughout (all 18 competencies)
- Organizational governance and advocacy skills (Competencies 16–18)
- The "when not to use AI" framework (Competency 10)
- Assessment that tests judgment, not just knowledge recall

**What Elements of AI does better than many courses:**
- Demystification: "terminology is scarier than the concepts"
- Accessibility: no math, no programming required
- Progression: builds from simple to complex without overwhelming

Funda should take the same demystification approach while going significantly further on critical evaluation and SA professional context.

## Competencies for Track-Specific Modules

Each sector track will extend the Core Track with sector-specific competencies. These are not designed yet (pending Field 1 learner research) but the pattern will be:

**AI Judgment for Finance Professionals (Track 1)** — will add:
- Interpret AI-generated financial analysis with appropriate skepticism
- Evaluate AI credit/risk models for fairness and explainability
- Apply POPIA and FSCA regulatory requirements to AI use in financial services

**AI Judgment for Government & Public Sector (Track 2)** — will add:
- Apply government accountability frameworks to AI-assisted decisions
- Evaluate AI in public procurement and service delivery contexts
- Navigate AI governance within PFMA constraints

**AI Judgment for ICT & Technology Professionals (Track 3)** — will add:
- Evaluate AI vendor claims for technical accuracy (not just marketing)
- Apply MICT SETA competency requirements to AI literacy
- Advocate for responsible AI within technology team culture

## What Needs to Be Validated (Field 1 dependency)

This framework is built from desk research. Before finalizing, the following must be confirmed through Field 1 learner interviews:

1. **Prior knowledge level**: Do SA working professionals at the target level (Nomvula persona) already have Domain 1 competencies? If so, the core track can start at Domain 2.
2. **Language and framing**: Do SA professionals respond to "AI literacy" as a framing, or does the pitch need different language?
3. **Time available**: For a working professional taking this alongside full-time work, how many hours per week is realistic? This determines how many competencies can fit in a single cohort.
4. **Highest-anxiety competencies**: Which of these 18 feels most urgent or most intimidating to SA professionals? This determines sequencing.
