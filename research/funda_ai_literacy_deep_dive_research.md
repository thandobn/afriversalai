# Funda AI Literacy Initiative — Deep-Dive Research Brief

**Prepared for:** Funda AI Literacy Initiative  
**Purpose:** Convert the current landscape report into a research agenda, curriculum design direction, and evidence-backed course blueprint.  
**Status:** Working research brief for ADDIE Analysis → Design transition.  
**Date:** June 2026

---

## 1. Executive Takeaway

Funda’s strongest lane is **not** “another AI tools course.” The defensible course opportunity is a **critical AI literacy program for South African corporate and government professionals**: people who are already being told to “use AI,” but who lack frameworks for deciding when to use it, how to evaluate its outputs, what risks matter, and how to integrate it responsibly into real work.

The starting document is already directionally strong. The next research step is to validate and sharpen three things:

1. **Learner reality:** What do working professionals actually do with AI today, where do they get stuck, and what would make them feel competent rather than overwhelmed?
2. **Buyer reality:** What do HR, L&D, digital transformation, and government training buyers actually need to justify spending money on AI literacy?
3. **Course proof:** What is the smallest pilot module that can demonstrate measurable improvement in judgment, confidence, and workplace application?

The course should be built around a simple thesis:

> AI literacy is not knowing every tool. It is knowing how to think, choose, check, and decide when working with AI.

---

## 2. What the Starting Document Already Establishes

The existing landscape report makes a clear argument:

- The global market is crowded with AI courses, bootcamps, and corporate trainings.
- Most programs teach tool operation: ChatGPT, Copilot, Gemini, Canva AI, Cursor, Midjourney, etc.
- The missing layer is judgment: choosing the right tool, verifying outputs, recognizing failure modes, understanding risk, and knowing when not to use AI.
- In Africa, many initiatives are developer-track, school-focused, product-led, or broad digital skilling programs rather than critical AI literacy for working professionals.
- Funda’s Phase 1 should focus on South African corporate and government clients, with future expansion toward mobile-first, low-bandwidth, multilingual access.

The report’s strongest existing strategic framing is:

> “Teach the map, not the destination.”

That framing should become the core design principle for the curriculum.

## 3. The Deeper Research Questions to Answer Before Building

### 3.1 Learner Research Questions

These questions should be answered through interviews, surveys, and workplace task analysis.

| Research Area | Key Questions | Why It Matters for Course Design |
|---|---|---|
| Current AI use | Which tools do professionals already use? ChatGPT? Copilot? Gemini? WhatsApp-integrated tools? Canva? | Determines which examples feel real rather than imported. |
| Work tasks | What tasks do they want AI to help with: reports, emails, analysis, translation, policy briefs, grant proposals, customer service? | Modules should be organized around tasks, not tools. |
| Confidence gap | What makes them hesitate: fear of being wrong, privacy, job replacement, looking incompetent, unclear workplace policy? | Course must build confidence and judgment, not just knowledge. |
| Verification habits | How do learners currently check AI outputs? Do they know when outputs are unreliable? | This is likely Funda’s most important competency gap. |
| Learning habits | Do they prefer short workshops, cohort-based learning, WhatsApp support, self-paced LMS, manager-led sessions? | Determines delivery model. |
| Language and context | Do they work in English only? Do they need local-language examples? Are code-switching and translation common? | Determines localization strategy. |

### 3.2 Buyer Research Questions

These should be answered through 3–6 conversations with HR, L&D, transformation, ICT, compliance, and government training leaders.

| Buyer Question | What to Listen For |
|---|---|
| What problem are they trying to solve with AI training? | Productivity, compliance, staff confidence, risk reduction, digital transformation, B-BBEE/skills development alignment. |
| What would make them choose Funda over Microsoft, Google, Coursera, or ALX? | Local context, practical judgment, customization, facilitation, sector-specific cases. |
| How do they measure training success? | Completion, satisfaction, behavior change, adoption, productivity, risk incidents reduced, manager feedback. |
| Who signs off? | HR? Procurement? CIO? Chief Digital Officer? Department head? Public-sector training authority? |
| What budget band exists? | Per-seat pricing, cohort pricing, departmental contract, annual license, pilot fee. |
| What procurement barriers exist? | Vendor registration, proof of accreditation, references, tax documentation, B-BBEE positioning, data privacy. |

### 3.3 Curriculum Research Questions

These questions bridge research and design.

- What is the minimum viable course: 90-minute workshop, 3-hour core module, 1-day intensive, 4-week cohort, or blended track?
- Which sector should anchor the first pilot: corporate communications, government administration, finance, NGOs, HR, education, healthcare?
- What should the capstone assessment be: critique an AI output, redesign a workflow, write an AI-use decision memo, or create a role-specific AI use policy?
- Should certification be completion-based, competency-based, or badge-based?
- What must be included in a facilitator guide so the course can scale beyond the founder?

## 4. Current Landscape Update: What Has Changed or Needs Tightening

### 4.1 South Africa Is the Right Phase 1 Market, But Policy Is Moving

South Africa is still a strong Phase 1 market because it has major corporate activity, strong cloud investment, a maturing AI policy conversation, and a large training/professional development ecosystem. However, the policy environment is unstable and should be described carefully.

In 2025, Microsoft announced a goal to train **1 million South Africans in AI and cybersecurity by 2026**, targeting companies, government, and youth. This validates demand, but it is product/platform-adjacent and broad rather than a locally grounded critical AI literacy program.

In 2026, South Africa’s draft national AI policy became a case study in exactly why critical AI literacy matters: the draft was withdrawn after reportedly containing fictitious, AI-generated references. The minister explicitly connected the failure to the need for vigilant human oversight. For Funda, this is not just a policy update; it is a perfect teaching case.

**Course implication:** Build a module or case study called something like **“The Fake Citation Problem: Why AI Outputs Must Be Verified.”** Use it to teach verification, source-checking, institutional risk, and human accountability.

### 4.2 The Global AI Literacy Field Is Moving Toward Competencies

Recent AI literacy research increasingly frames AI literacy as a set of competencies rather than a list of tools. Several current frameworks converge around the same categories:

- Foundational understanding: what AI is and is not.
- Interaction: prompting, iteration, and task framing.
- Evaluation: checking accuracy, reliability, bias, and usefulness.
- Ethics and governance: privacy, accountability, fairness, transparency, and human oversight.
- Contextual application: using AI appropriately in specific domains and roles.

A 2024 paper on generative AI literacy proposed twelve competencies covering foundations, interaction, ethics, legal issues, and responsible use. Another 2024 AI literacy curriculum paper argued for an interdisciplinary socio-technical curriculum built around technical understanding, responsible interaction, ethics, and social/future implications.

**Course implication:** Funda should not organize the course as “Module 1: ChatGPT, Module 2: Gemini, Module 3: Copilot.” It should organize the course as “Understand, Use, Check, Decide, Govern, Apply.”

### 4.3 Corporate AI Training Must Be More Than Training

AI adoption in organizations is not simply a skills issue. It is also a change-management issue. Workers may resist AI because they fear job loss, surveillance, embarrassment, ethical uncertainty, or unclear expectations. Managers may push AI adoption without being able to coach safe use.

**Course implication:** Funda should sell two tracks, not one:

1. **Core AI Literacy for Staff** — practical competence, judgment, and confidence.
2. **AI Literacy for Managers** — coaching, governance, team norms, risk boundaries, and behavior change.

This matters because managers are adoption multipliers. If managers do not understand AI well enough to set boundaries and evaluate use, staff training will not translate into workplace behavior.

## 5. Competitive Landscape: How Funda Should Position Against Existing Programs

### 5.1 Major Competitor Categories

| Category | Examples | Strength | Weakness | Funda Response |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Big tech courses | Google AI Essentials, Microsoft Copilot training, IBM SkillsBuild | Credibility, scale, low/free cost | Product-led, generic, not locally contextualized | Position as tool-agnostic and locally relevant. |
| MOOCs | Elements of AI, Coursera, edX | Strong reach, structured content | Often self-paced, low support, generic examples | Add facilitation, workplace application, African case studies. |
| Bootcamps/challenges | Udemy, LinkedIn, “30-day AI” programs | Marketable, practical, fast | Tool-specific, quickly obsolete | Teach frameworks that survive tool churn. |
| Developer programs | ALX, DeepLearning.AI, technical academies | Strong technical depth | Not for nontechnical professionals | Clarify: Funda is for users and decision-makers, not AI builders. |
| Corporate consulting | Big 4, tech vendors, internal L&D | Executive access, custom delivery | Expensive, often strategy-heavy or vendor-led | Offer affordable, practical, African-contextualized workforce literacy. |

### 5.2 Elements of AI as the Closest Global Analog

Elements of AI is the strongest analog because it is conceptual, accessible, no-code, and designed for a broad public audience. Its success suggests that learners do want conceptual AI literacy when it is presented clearly and interactively.

But Funda should not copy it directly. Elements of AI was built from a European/public-education context. Funda should adapt the strengths while changing the context:

| Elements of AI Strength | Funda Adaptation |
|---|---|
| Conceptual, no-code accessibility | Keep no-code, but make it workplace-first. |
| Interactive exercises | Use workplace artifacts: emails, reports, spreadsheets, policies. |
| Broad public orientation | Focus Phase 1 on corporate and government professionals. |
| Multi-language reach | Build translation-ready content from the start. |
| National digital literacy strategy alignment | Align with South African skills development and responsible AI needs. |

## 6. Recommended Competency Framework for Funda

Funda should define competency as what a learner can actually do after training. Below is a proposed framework.

### 6.1 The Funda AI Literacy Competency Model

| Competency | Learner Can… | Assessment Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Explain AI clearly | Explain what AI can and cannot do without hype or fear. | Short explanation to a colleague. |
| 2. Match task to tool type | Decide whether a task needs a chatbot, search tool, image tool, spreadsheet tool, automation tool, or no AI. | Tool-choice scenario. |
| 3. Write effective task instructions | Give AI enough context, constraints, audience, and output format. | Before/after prompt improvement. |
| 4. Evaluate output quality | Check for accuracy, completeness, bias, unsupported claims, missing context, and tone mismatch. | Critique of AI-generated output. |
| 5. Verify factual claims | Use source checking, triangulation, and uncertainty labeling. | Fact-checking exercise. |
| 6. Protect sensitive information | Identify data that should not be entered into public AI tools. | Privacy-risk sorting activity. |
| 7. Decide when not to use AI | Recognize high-stakes, ethical, relational, legal, or accountability-sensitive situations. | “Use AI / don’t use AI / use with controls” scenario. |
| 8. Apply AI to a real workflow | Redesign one recurring work task with appropriate human review. | Role-specific workflow map. |
| 9. Communicate AI use responsibly | Disclose, document, or explain AI assistance when appropriate. | Draft of team AI-use note. |
| 10. Keep learning as tools change | Use a taxonomy and evaluation checklist for new tools. | New-tool evaluation worksheet. |

### 6.2 Competency Levels

Funda can use three levels for Phase 1 certification:

| Level | Name | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Level 1 | AI-Aware Professional | Understands AI basics, risks, and appropriate use. |
| Level 2 | AI-Ready Practitioner | Can use AI for role-specific tasks and evaluate outputs. |
| Level 3 | AI-Responsible Team Lead | Can guide team norms, risk boundaries, and workflow adoption. |

For MVP, certify only Level 1 and maybe Level 2. Level 3 can become a manager track.

## 7. Course Architecture Recommendation

### 7.1 MVP Course Shape

The strongest MVP is likely a **blended short course** rather than a pure self-paced course.

Recommended MVP:

- **Format:** 4 modules, delivered over 2 half-days or 4 weekly 90-minute sessions.
- **Audience:** South African corporate/government professionals, nontechnical but computer-literate.
- **Delivery:** Facilitated live sessions + lightweight self-paced exercises.
- **Assessment:** Practical competency portfolio, not just quiz completion.
- **Certificate:** Completion + competency evidence.

### 7.2 Proposed Core Track

| Module | Title | Core Question | Practical Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | What AI Is, What It Is Not | What are we actually dealing with? | AI myth/fact map + confidence baseline. |
| 2 | Choosing the Right AI Help | What tool or method fits this task? | Task-to-tool decision tree. |
| 3 | Prompting for Work, Not Tricks | How do I get useful outputs for real work? | Improved prompt for a real workplace task. |
| 4 | Checking, Risk, and Human Judgment | How do I know whether to trust this? | AI output critique + verification checklist. |

### 7.3 Optional Sector Tracks

Once the core track is validated, build sector-specific versions.

| Sector | High-Value Use Cases | High-Risk Issues | Possible Capstone |
|---|---|---|---|
| Government | Policy briefs, public communications, citizen-service FAQs, meeting summaries | Fake citations, public accountability, procurement, privacy | Critique an AI-generated policy memo. |
| Corporate communications | Client emails, press releases, reports, presentations | Brand tone, misinformation, confidentiality | Rewrite and verify an AI-generated client update. |
| HR / L&D | Training plans, job descriptions, performance feedback drafts | Bias, labor law, sensitive employee data | Audit an AI-generated job description for bias. |
| Finance / banking | Customer communication, compliance summaries, analysis support | Regulatory risk, data privacy, hallucinated numbers | Verify an AI-generated market/client briefing. |
| NGOs / development | Grant proposals, reports, impact narratives | Donor accountability, community representation | Improve a grant narrative while checking claims. |
| Education | Lesson planning, feedback, admin support | Student privacy, over-reliance, assessment integrity | Create an AI-use policy for a classroom/team. |

## 8. Pedagogy: What Will Make the Course Actually Work

### 8.1 Adult Learning Principles to Use

Funda should design around adult learning realities:

- Adults need immediate relevance.
- Adults bring prior experience and workplace constraints.
- Adults resist abstract theory unless they can connect it to a real problem.
- Confidence matters as much as knowledge.
- Practice beats passive content.
- Social learning and peer examples accelerate adoption.

### 8.2 Design Pattern for Every Module

Each module should follow the same learning rhythm:

1. **Start with a real workplace problem.**  
   Example: “Your manager asks you to use AI to draft a client report. What could go wrong?”

2. **Introduce one mental model.**  
   Example: “AI outputs are drafts, not facts.”

3. **Demonstrate with one tool.**  
   Use ChatGPT, Copilot, Gemini, or another accessible tool as an example, but do not make the module tool-dependent.

4. **Practice on a realistic artifact.**  
   Learners work with a memo, email, policy paragraph, report section, or spreadsheet-like scenario.

5. **Reflect and transfer.**  
   Learners answer: “Where would I use this in my job next week?”

6. **Competency check.**  
   Short applied task demonstrating actual ability.

### 8.3 Assessment Strategy

Avoid shallow quizzes as the main evidence. Use performance tasks.

| Assessment Type | Example |
|---|---|
| Diagnostic | “How confident are you using AI at work?” baseline survey. |
| Scenario judgment | Learner chooses whether AI is appropriate for a task. |
| Prompt improvement | Learner revises a weak prompt into a strong task instruction. |
| Output critique | Learner marks hallucinations, bias, missing context, and unsupported claims. |
| Workflow redesign | Learner maps where AI can support a real task and where human review is required. |
| Reflection | Learner writes a personal/team AI-use commitment. |

## 9. The Most Important Course Artifact: The Funda AI Decision Framework

Funda needs a memorable framework that learners can use long after the course. Here is a draft.

### The Funda 5C Framework

Before using AI, ask:

1. **Context** — Have I given enough background, audience, purpose, and constraints?
2. **Capability** — Is this a task AI is actually good at?
3. **Confidentiality** — Am I exposing sensitive, private, client, citizen, or organizational information?
4. **Checkability** — Can I verify the output with reliable sources or human expertise?
5. **Consequence** — What happens if the output is wrong, biased, incomplete, or misunderstood?

This framework should be repeated in every module until learners internalize it.

### Use / Don’t Use / Use With Controls

Learners should practice sorting tasks into three categories:

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Use AI | Brainstorming, summarizing non-sensitive notes, drafting first versions, simplifying explanations. |
| Use with controls | Client reports, policy drafts, data interpretation, public communication, research summaries. |
| Do not use AI or escalate | Legal/medical decisions, confidential personal data, final factual claims without verification, sensitive HR decisions, relationship-sensitive messages. |

## 11. Primary Research Plan

### 11.1 Interviews

Run three interview streams.

| Stream | Target | Number | Goal |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Learners | Corporate/government professionals | 8–12 | Understand real tasks, fears, tools, learning habits. |
| Buyers | HR, L&D, CIO, transformation, government training leads | 5–7 | Understand purchasing criteria and success metrics. |
| Experts | AI educators, policy people, African AI researchers, corporate trainers | 4–6 | Validate curriculum and risks. |

### 11.2 Learner Interview Guide

Ask:

1. What is your role, and what kinds of documents or decisions do you work on weekly?
2. Have you used AI tools at work? Which ones?
3. What have you tried that worked surprisingly well?
4. What have you tried that failed or felt risky?
5. What do you worry about when using AI?
6. Has your organization given you any AI guidance or training?
7. How do you decide whether an AI output is good enough?
8. What would make an AI course worth your time?
9. Would you prefer live workshops, self-paced learning, WhatsApp/community support, or blended delivery?
10. What would you want to be able to do after the course?

### 11.3 Buyer Interview Guide

Ask:

1. Are staff already using AI tools? Officially or unofficially?
2. What problems are you seeing: productivity, risk, compliance, inconsistent use, fear, overuse?
3. What training have you already purchased or considered?
4. What would make a local AI literacy program attractive?
5. Who would need to approve a pilot?
6. What budget model is realistic: per person, per cohort, annual contract, department license?
7. What evidence would you need after the pilot to expand it?
8. What sectors, departments, or roles should be trained first?
9. Are there internal AI policies learners must follow?
10. What would make you say, “This worked”?

### 11.4 Survey Instrument

After interviews, run a short survey to quantify patterns.

Measure:

- Current AI tool use.
- Frequency of use.
- Confidence level.
- Top use cases.
- Top fears.
- Existing workplace guidance.
- Preferred learning format.
- Willingness to complete certificate.
- Role/sector segmentation.

### 11.5 Pilot Success Metrics

Track before and after:

| Metric | Tool |
|---|---|
| AI confidence | Pre/post self-rating. |
| Ability to identify risky use | Scenario test. |
| Ability to improve prompts | Prompt rubric. |
| Ability to critique outputs | Output-evaluation rubric. |
| Workplace transfer | 2-week follow-up: “Did you use this?” |
| Manager-observed change | Manager pulse survey. |
| Buyer satisfaction | Debrief interview. |

## 12. Recommended MVP Pilot

### 12.1 Pilot Hypothesis

If Funda teaches professionals a simple framework for choosing, prompting, checking, and deciding, then learners will become more confident and safer AI users — even without deep technical training.

### 12.2 Pilot Design

| Feature | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Pilot size | 15–25 learners. |
| Organization type | South African ICT company, ministry department, university admin office, NGO, or professional services firm. |
| Delivery | Two 2-hour facilitated sessions, one week apart. |
| Between-session task | Use AI on one real but non-sensitive work task. |
| Final assessment | Critique an AI-generated work artifact and submit a workflow-use plan. |
| Output to buyer | Pilot report: confidence change, competency scores, learner feedback, recommended rollout plan. |

### 12.3 MVP Learning Outcomes

By the end of the pilot, learners should be able to:

1. Explain AI’s strengths and limits in plain language.
2. Select an appropriate AI tool category for a task.
3. Write a useful prompt for a real work task.
4. Identify at least five common AI output failure modes.
5. Apply the 5C framework before using AI.
6. Decide whether a task is safe for AI, AI-with-controls, or no-AI.
7. Create one role-specific AI workflow with human review built in.

## 14. Risks and How to Avoid Them

| Risk | Why It Matters | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Becoming another tool course | Tools change too quickly and big tech wins on product training. | Keep tool-agnostic framework at center. |
| Over-academic content | Professionals need practical application. | Start every module with workplace tasks. |
| Empty certification | Weakens credibility. | Tie certificate to competency tasks. |
| Too broad an audience | “African professionals” is too large for MVP. | Start with South African corporate/government professionals. |
| Weak buyer proof | Learners may like it but buyers need ROI. | Measure confidence, risk detection, and workplace transfer. |
| Ignoring managers | Staff behavior may not change if managers do not support it. | Add manager track early. |
| Imported examples | Generic US/Europe cases weaken differentiation. | Build African-context case bank. |
| Policy overclaiming | South Africa AI policy is in flux. | Frame policy as evolving; emphasize responsible practice. |

## 19. Appendix: Draft One-Page Course Promise

**Course Name:** Funda AI Literacy Core  
**Audience:** South African corporate and government professionals.  
**Format:** Facilitated short course with practical workplace exercises.  
**Promise:** Learn how to use AI responsibly at work by choosing the right tasks, writing better instructions, checking outputs, protecting sensitive information, and knowing when human judgment must lead.

**Not a course about:** Coding, building AI models, memorizing tool features, or chasing every new app.

**A course about:** Thinking clearly with AI.
